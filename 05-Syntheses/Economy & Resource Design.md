# Economy & Resource Design

> **What this is:** Everything the canon says about the flow of resources in games — internal economy mechanics, defining game parts, sinks and sources, economic loops, inflation/deflation, zero-sum vs. positive-sum design, and the two-currency F2P system. Aggregated across s001, s005, s006, s017.

---

## 1. What a Game Economy Is

A game economy is any system in which **resources are created, stored, exchanged, and destroyed**. This definition is much broader than currency — it includes:

- Hit points generated, lost, and restored
- Ammunition collected, expended, and found
- XP accumulated and converted to levels
- Energy gained and spent on abilities
- Time generated by completing objectives and consumed by the challenge
- Information discovered and used (or lost)

**Adams** (s006, ch.14) identifies **internal economy** as one of his five core mechanic families — the second-most fundamental after physics. Any game where resources matter has an economy.

**Sellers** (s001, ch.8): *"An internal economy is a system in which the game tracks the creation, storage, movement, and consumption of game parts."* These parts (coins, ammo, health, time, actions) have **attributes** (current value, max value, regeneration rate) and exist in relationships with other parts.

## 2. Defining Game Parts (Sellers, s001, ch.8)

Before designing an economy, define its components with precision:

### Parts
The individual entities that participate in the economy. A gold coin, a health point, a skill point, an energy cell. Parts exist in the game model and have defined identities.

### Attributes
Properties of a part, each with a current **state** (value). Key attributes for economy:
- **Quantity** (how many exist right now)
- **Maximum** (the cap on accumulation)
- **Minimum** (usually 0; sometimes negative for debt)
- **Rate of change** (how fast it increases or decreases passively)
- **Transferability** (can it move between entities?)

### Behaviors
How a part acts over time without player input:
- Regeneration (health regenerates at 5 HP/second when not in combat)
- Decay (ammo does not decay; food items in survival games do)
- Interest (virtual currency accumulating passive income)
- Depreciation (gear durability declining with use)

### Relationships
How parts connect to each other and to players:
- Cost relationships (ability costs 3 energy; attacking costs durability)
- Exchange relationships (spend 100 gold, receive 1 item)
- Conversion relationships (XP converts to levels at specified thresholds)

**Practical tool** (s001, ch.8): document parts in a spreadsheet — part name, all attributes with current values and caps, behavior rates, relationships. This creates a machine-readable model of the economy before implementation.

## 3. Resource Types

Not all resources behave the same way. Key distinctions:

### Hard Resources vs. Soft Resources
- **Hard resources** (limited supply): finite; their value comes from scarcity. Once spent, gone. Examples: lives, one-use items, rare crafting materials.
- **Soft resources** (flowing supply): continuously generated by play. The player manages rate of income vs. rate of expenditure. Examples: gold, XP, energy.

Most games use both: hard resources for high-stakes decisions, soft resources for continuous engagement.

### Consumables vs. Durables
- **Consumables**: used once and gone (a health potion, a bomb, a one-shot ability)
- **Durables**: used repeatedly but degrade or break (weapons with durability, vehicles that take damage)
- **Permanent**: acquired once, held forever (unlocked skills, discovered areas, story progress)

### Currencies (Exchangeable Resources)
Resources primarily valuable for what they can be traded for, not for themselves:
- **Primary currency** (earnable through play): gold, coins, credits — the main reward for engagement
- **Premium currency** (harder to earn; sometimes purchasable): gems, crystals — the F2P revenue layer
- **Social/reputation currency**: faction standings, friendship levels, community contributions

## 4. Sinks and Sources — The Flow Balance

**The fundamental economic design challenge:** if the player accumulates resources indefinitely, they become worthless. If resources deplete without renewal, the player is blocked. The economy must maintain **dynamic balance** through opposing flows.

### Sources (Resource Generation)
Where resources enter the game economy:
- Enemy drops
- Task/mission completion rewards
- Passive generation (income per turn, regeneration per second)
- Trading with NPCs
- Crafting from other resources
- Time-based rewards (daily login bonus)

### Sinks (Resource Consumption)
Where resources leave the game economy:
- Purchasing items or upgrades
- Ability costs
- Death penalties (drop resources on death)
- Crafting inputs
- Upkeep costs (troops cost gold per turn)
- Time-limited resources that expire

### Balance Between Sources and Sinks

**Inflation** (sources >> sinks): resources accumulate faster than they can be spent. They lose perceived value. Players stop caring about gold because they have too much.

**Deflation/scarcity** (sinks >> sources): resources are consumed faster than generated. Players feel constantly resource-starved, blocked from progressing, or forced to grind.

**Healthy economy:** sources and sinks roughly balance over the intended play experience, with slight surplus that creates a sense of progression and player empowerment — without creating abundance that eliminates meaningful resource decisions.

**Sellers** (s001, ch.9): *"Every benefit has a cost, and the two are inextricably linked."* Resource design should ensure that acquiring something always means something else was sacrificed.

## 5. Economic Loop Design

Game economies are loops, not linear flows:

### The Basic Economic Loop
```
Player action → Resource gained → Resource spent → Capability gained → Better player actions → More resources
```

This is a **reinforcing loop** if unchecked — more resources → more power → more resources. The reinforcing nature of most game economies is intentional (progression feels good) but must be bounded by increasing costs, caps, or diminishing returns.

### Core Loop Integration
Resources must be integrated into the core gameplay loop, not bolted on:
- Resources earned through the things the player would do anyway (fun activities generate currency)
- Resources spent in ways that directly affect gameplay (not just cosmetics, if meaningful progression is the goal)
- The resource cycle should reinforce the desire to play, not create a sense of obligation

### The Compulsion Loop (and its ethics)
The **compulsion loop** (action → reward → action → reward) is the psychological mechanism underlying F2P retention. The dopaminergic reward signal is strongest under variable ratio schedules (s014, ch.7). This produces powerful engagement but can become exploitative if the activity itself ceases to be enjoyable and the reward is the only reason to continue.

**Ethical test:** is the player still enjoying the *activity*, or only the *reward*? If the activity is unenjoyable, the economy has become a Skinner box.

## 6. Zero-Sum vs. Positive-Sum Systems

**Zero-sum:** one player's gain is exactly another's loss. Total resources in the system remain constant. Examples: real money gambling, competitive point systems where top score steals from second score.

**Positive-sum:** all players can gain simultaneously. The total resources in the system grow. Examples: cooperative progression, most single-player XP systems, guild activities that benefit all members.

**Negative-sum:** resources are lost without equivalent gains elsewhere. Examples: battle passes that expire (resources "lost" to time), durability systems where items break and aren't replaced.

**Design implication:** the economic model should match the desired social experience:
- Competitive: some zero-sum mechanics create stakes and real competition
- Cooperative: positive-sum mechanics align incentives
- Party/social: avoid strong zero-sum economic mechanics (they create resentment; competitive scoring of *actions* is fine, but economic *depletion* from other players feels bad)

## 7. Game Balance as an Economic Problem

Many balance problems are economic problems at root:

### Dominant Strategy as Economic Domination
A dominant strategy often emerges from an economic advantage: one option generates resources faster than alternatives, or requires fewer resources to achieve the same outcome. The economic imbalance creates the strategic imbalance.

**Fix:** ensure all strategy archetypes have similar economic efficiency. The fast strategy and the slow strategy should reach comparable power states at comparable resource investment.

### Power Curves as Economic Curves
The progression of player power over time IS an economic curve: how fast do resources accumulate, what do they buy, and what's the power-per-resource exchange rate at each level?

**Sellers** (s001, ch.10): *"Progression curves regulate the pace of the game while keeping the player feeling that they are getting more powerful over time."* The economic model must make the curve feel rewarding without making it trivial.

### The Treadmill Problem
When the economy is the game — when grinding for resources is the primary activity — the system has become a treadmill. Players are on the treadmill because of loss aversion (don't want to fall behind) and sunk cost (already invested so much), not because the activity is intrinsically valuable.

## 8. F2P Economy Design (s017)

The F2P two-currency system is a deliberate economic design:

### Soft Currency (Earnable)
- Earned through normal gameplay (the fun activities)
- Buys most common items, cosmetics, and upgrades
- Gives non-paying players access to significant content
- Creates the feeling of economic activity and progression

### Hard Currency (Purchasable)
- Earned slowly through play OR purchased with real money
- Buys premium items, time-skips, or exclusive content
- The monetization layer
- Must not make soft currency feel pointless (if everything good requires hard currency, the soft economy is fake)

### Price Anchoring (s014)
The first price the player encounters becomes a reference point. F2P pricing catalogs are deliberately structured around this:
- Prominent mid-tier bundle makes smaller purchases look cheap and larger ones look reasonable
- Starter packs are priced to convert free players at a low commitment point
- After the first purchase, the psychological barrier to subsequent purchases is lower

### The Hard Currency Abstraction
Converting real money to an in-game currency creates **psychological distance** from the real cost. "That skin costs 800 gems" feels different from "that skin costs $8" even when equivalent. This is a deliberate design choice by F2P developers — acknowledged widely as psychologically manipulative.

## 9. Schell's Economy Lens (s005, ch.11, Lens #46)

> *"Is the game's economy properly balanced? Are resource rates appropriate? Are there sinks and sources for all key resources? Does the economy feel fair to all player types?"*

Additional questions from the balance chapter context:
- Do players feel resource-starved (sinks dominate) or overloaded (sources dominate)?
- Is the cost of each action proportional to its power?
- Are there dominant resource strategies (one resource type is always more valuable)?
- Does the economy create meaningful choice (spend here OR there) or is the optimal path obvious?

## 10. Hot Potato Economy Analysis

Hot Potato's primary "economy" is the **FAP (Fuck Around Points) system**:

| Economic element | FAP system role |
|---|---|
| Resource | FAP points |
| Source | Trolling actions (tagging opponents, using items on runners, parkour stunts near others) |
| Sink | None — FAP accumulates until match end; no spending mechanism |
| Cap | None specified — linear accumulation throughout the match |
| Win condition | Highest FAP + not being the Potato at timer expiry |

**Current economic model:** positive-sum with respect to FAP (all players accumulate FAP simultaneously — there is no fixed pool of FAP to compete over). The competition is in *rates* (who earns FAP faster) not in *depletion* (taking FAP from others).

**Open design question (from _open-questions.md_):** Should FAP have any spending mechanism (spending FAP for items, abilities, or special actions)? A spending mechanic would:
- Create more interesting resource decisions
- Introduce strategic depth (save FAP vs. spend for advantage)
- Risk creating an economy-dominant strategy if one spend is clearly optimal

The current no-spend model is simpler and more legible — appropriate for a party game.
