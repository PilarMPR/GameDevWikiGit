# Player Audience — Demographics, Psychographics & the 5 Domains

> **What this is:** Everything the canon says about knowing your players before designing for them — Adams' detailed demographic breakdown, VandenBerghe's Five Domains of Play, Schell's demographic analysis, and psychographic profiling. This is the foundation of player-centric design. Aggregated across s006, s005, s014, s001.

---

## 1. Why You Must Know Your Audience

**Adams** (s006, ch.2):
> *"The designer is the player's advocate. You cannot advocate for someone you don't know. Define your target audience before designing — then design for them, not for yourself."*

**Schell** (s005, ch.8):
> *"To know your audience better than they know themselves, study them like a cultural anthropologist. Don't assume. Observe."*

**The asymmetry problem:** the designer is almost always *different* from the target audience in at least one important dimension — age, gaming experience, cultural context, gender, or physical ability. Designing for "people like me" when the audience is not like you produces games that confuse or exclude.

**The practical tools:**
1. Demographics — observable, measurable player characteristics
2. Psychographics — psychological traits, values, preferences
3. Player type taxonomies (Bartle, VandenBerghe) — motivational profiles
4. Cognitive development stages — how age shapes what kind of play is possible

## 2. Demographics — The Observable Layer

### Age Groups (Adams, s006, ch.4; Schell, s005, ch.8)

The industry's standard age segmentation — organized not by arbitrary numbers but by **cognitive and social development milestones:**

| Age Group | Label | Cognitive stage | What games can offer | Design implications |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **0–3** | Infant/Toddler | Sensorimotor (Piaget) | Sensory stimulation, cause-and-effect | Extremely simple; immediate visual/audio feedback; no text; very forgiving |
| **4–6** | Preschooler | Pre-operational | Simple rules, fantasy, discovery | 1–2 rules maximum; bright colors; character-driven; no reading required |
| **7–9** | Kids | Concrete operational | Rule-based play, collecting, mastering skills | Can read; enjoy challenge and completion; competitive but still cooperative; 10–20 min sessions |
| **10–13** | Tween | Emerging formal operations | Complex systems, social identity | "Age of obsession" — will invest deeply in something they love; peer influence paramount; deeper systems possible |
| **13–18** | Teen | Formal operations | Abstract reasoning, identity expression, competition | Male/female interests diverge; strong social peer context; narrative can carry complex themes; hours-long sessions possible |
| **18–24** | Young Adult | Fully formal | Full game complexity; competition; creative expression | Maximum gaming time + income; core gamer demographic; tolerates high complexity |
| **25–35** | Adult (peak family) | N/A | Nostalgic comfort, family play, efficient engagement | Limited time; family responsibilities; often split between hardcore return-gamers and casual players |
| **35–50** | Midlife Adult | N/A | Family and social games; nostalgic brands | Buys games for family; plays cooperatively; familiar mechanics preferred |
| **50+** | Empty Nester/Senior | N/A | Social engagement, cognitive challenge, accessibility | May have motor/visual/cognitive accessibility needs; social games attractive |

**Key insight (Adams, s006):** the younger age groups are divided by *mental development* milestones (Piaget's stages). The older groups are divided by *life transition* milestones (family formation, career stability). These are fundamentally different design constraints.

### Gender Demographics (s005, ch.8; s006, ch.4)

**Critical caveat first:** these are *population-level statistical tendencies*, not individual rules. Every individual is different. Use for calibrating default experiences, never for excluding audiences.

**Schell** (s005, ch.8): *"If you're designing for women, don't design what you think women want — find out what the actual women in your audience want."*

**Adams** (s006, ch.4) cites documented population-level tendencies from player research:

**Males tend to enjoy:**
- Mastery and competition (demonstrating skill in a ranked context)
- Destruction (satisfying destruction mechanics — blowing things up)
- Spatial/physical challenges (navigation, precision)
- Trial-and-error learning (experiment, fail, improve)

**Females tend to enjoy:**
- Emotional engagement and relationships (character connections, story)
- Real-world or social contexts (simulations of recognizable situations)
- Nurturing and caretaking mechanics
- Verbal and dialogue-driven puzzles
- Learning by example rather than trial-and-error

**Design application:** these tendencies inform which *secondary* features to include, not the core design. *Toontown Online* case (s005, ch.8): noticing that healing others was appealing to female players while combat was male-skewed, the designers made the healer role genuinely essential (you *can only* heal others, not yourself) — this made the social/cooperative dimension the mechanical prerequisite, not an afterthought.

### Gaming Experience Demographics

**The experience axis:**
- **Core gamer:** 20+ hours/week; genre literate; tolerates learning curves; actively seeks new games
- **Mainstream gamer:** 5–20 hours/week; familiar with popular genres; prefers accessible mechanics
- **Casual gamer:** <5 hours/week; primarily mobile or party games; no tolerance for complex onboarding
- **Non-gamer:** plays only if brought in by social context (party games)

Party games like Hot Potato must serve **all four levels simultaneously** in a single session. This is uniquely difficult and the reason party game design is its own discipline.

## 3. Psychographics — The Psychological Layer

Psychographics go beyond demographics to model *psychological motivations, values, and lifestyle preferences*:

**Relevant psychographic dimensions (s006, ch.4):**
- Attitude toward competition (loves vs. tolerates vs. avoids)
- Social play preference (solo vs. co-op vs. competitive vs. mixed)
- Narrative engagement (story-first vs. gameplay-first vs. emergent narrative)
- Completionism tendency (explores everything vs. follows main path)
- Risk tolerance (prefers guaranteed rewards vs. enjoys gambling mechanics)
- Aesthetic preference (photorealistic vs. stylized vs. abstract)

**Why psychographics matter more than demographics for design:** two 25-year-old male gamers with the same demographic profile can have completely different motivational profiles. One wants competition and status; the other wants exploration and story. Psychographics, not demographics, predict actual design feature preferences.

## 4. VandenBerghe's Five Domains of Play

**Jason VandenBerghe's model** (cited by Adams, s006, ch.4), developed from Big Five personality psychology applied to gaming:

> *"The five domains represent the five fundamental sources of fun — each corresponding to a core dimension of human personality. Games that excel in one domain attract the corresponding personality type; games that excel in all five attract everyone."*

### Domain 1: Novelty
**Openness/Curiosity:** the desire for new experiences, discovery, and exploration.

- **What players want:** new content, surprises, secrets, procedural variety, exploration rewards
- **Game features that serve Novelty:** open worlds, procedural generation, secrets and hidden areas, diverse content updates, surprising mechanics
- **Motivation:** "I want to see what's around the corner"
- **Koster connection (s016):** Novelty = pattern-learning in a discovery mode. The brain seeks new patterns; Novelty players lean hardest into this drive.

### Domain 2: Challenge
**Conscientiousness/Mastery:** the desire to improve skills and demonstrate competence.

- **What players want:** clear skill gaps to close, measurable improvement, hard challenges with earned victories
- **Game features that serve Challenge:** leaderboards, ranked modes, achievement systems, hard difficulty options, skill-based progression
- **Motivation:** "I want to be good at this; I want to prove I'm good at this"
- **Schell connection (s005, ch.9):** Challenge domain = Maslow level 4 (esteem). Competence from SDT.

### Domain 3: Stimulation
**Excitement/Sensation:** the desire for intense sensory and emotional experiences.

- **What players want:** fast-paced action, intense moments, visual/audio spectacle, adrenaline, physical sensation
- **Game features that serve Stimulation:** explosive particle effects, fast gameplay, dramatic music, combat intensity, spectacle moments
- **Motivation:** "I want my heart to race"
- **MDA connection:** Sensation aesthetic primarily.

### Domain 4: Harmony
**Agreeableness/Social:** the desire for positive social experiences, cooperation, and peace.

- **What players want:** cooperative play, peaceful exploration, social connection, collaborative achievement
- **Game features that serve Harmony:** co-op modes, guilds, trading, friendship mechanics, non-violent progression options
- **Motivation:** "I want to share this with people I care about"
- **Bartle connection:** Socializers. MDA's Fellowship aesthetic.

### Domain 5: Threat
**Emotional Stability/Risk:** the desire to safely experience fear, threat, and danger.

- **What players want:** horror elements, permadeath, high stakes, narrow escapes, the feeling of genuine danger
- **Game features that serve Threat:** horror atmosphere, roguelike permadeath, high-risk/high-reward mechanics, tension-building design
- **Motivation:** "I want to feel genuinely scared in a safe context"
- **Schell connection (s005, ch.9):** controlled fear as play; the "fight or flight" system engaged safely within the magic circle.

### Applying the Five Domains

**Single-domain games** appeal strongly to one audience segment; may exclude others.
**Multi-domain games** appeal broadly; harder to design with coherent identity.

**Hot Potato domain mapping:**
- **Novelty:** moderate (map variety, item variety — not exploration-driven)
- **Challenge:** high (FAP optimization, parkour skill, competitive positioning)
- **Stimulation:** high (fast pace, chaos, explosive interactions)
- **Harmony:** high (social laughter, group play, party context)
- **Threat:** moderate (being the Potato = low-stakes fear; comedic rather than genuinely threatening)

## 5. The Audience-First Design Process

**Adams' player-centric design process** (s006, ch.2):

1. **Define the target audience** before designing. Who are they? What do they want? What do they already play?
2. **Define the player experience** — what should the player feel and do? (MDA's Aesthetics-first approach)
3. **Design mechanics to produce that experience** for that audience
4. **Validate against the audience** through playtesting, not through internal team gut-feeling

**The target audience definition template** (s006, ch.2):
- Age range and gender composition of expected audience
- Gaming experience level (core/mainstream/casual/non-gamer)
- Platform they use (and how they hold the controller/phone)
- Other games they play (competitive set)
- What kind of fun they're seeking (apply Five Domains)
- How much time they have per session
- Where they discover games

## 6. Knowing What Players Don't Know They Want

**Schell** (s005, ch.8): *"Know your audience better than they know themselves."*

**The Henry Ford problem:** *"If I had asked people what they wanted, they would have said faster horses."* Focus groups produce answers constrained by existing mental models. Genuine product innovation requires observing what people *do*, not just what they *say*.

**Anthropological observation techniques (s005, ch.2):**
- Watch people play (don't explain anything; observe what confuses them)
- Note what they laugh at, where they lean forward, where they put down the controller
- Ask open questions *after* play, not before
- Compare what they say with what they actually did during play

| Dimension | Hot Potato target | Design calibration |

| Age | 12–35 primary; all ages accessible | Cartoon aesthetic; no mature themes; humor universal not cultural |
