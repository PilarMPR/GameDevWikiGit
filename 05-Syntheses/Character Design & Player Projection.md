# Character Design & Player Projection

> **What this is:** Everything the canon says about designing the agents in games — player characters (the key distinction between projection and empathy), avatar design, the empathy mechanism, NPC design principles, character arcs, and characters as indirect control. Aggregated across s005, s014, s013, s012, s006.

---

## 1. The Central Distinction: Projection vs. Empathy

**Schell** (s005, ch.9) identifies the most important difference between game characters and characters in all other media:

> *"In film, we empathize with characters — we feel for them as separate entities. In games, we project INTO characters — the player IS the character. The player is making the decisions. This projection is deeper than empathy; the player literally embodies the character's agency."*

### Why This Matters

**Film audience:** feels *for* the character. The character is other. The audience's relationship is emotional but observational.

**Game player:** projects their *self* into the character. The player makes the character's decisions. Their identity temporarily merges with the avatar.

Practical consequences:
- Player character **deaths** feel personal ("I died"), not observational ("they died")
- Player character **failures** feel shameful ("I messed up"), not sympathetic ("they messed up")
- Player character **victories** feel earned and personal ("I did it"), not vicarious ("they did it")

This is why game characters can be designed with less psychological complexity than film characters and still produce strong emotional experiences — the player's own psychology fills in the character.

## 2. The Empathy Mechanism — How It Works

**Heider & Simmel's experiment** (cited by Schell, s005, ch.9): humans presented with abstract shapes moving on a screen spontaneously attribute personality, intent, and emotion to them. Two triangles and a circle are seen as a bully, a victim, and a protector within seconds. This is not taught — it is a fundamental cognitive reflex.

**Design implication:** we cannot help projecting agency and personality onto perceived agents. The cost of creating a relatable character is *lower than intuition suggests* — a few consistent behavioral rules and visual cues produce strong player attachment.

**Elements that trigger projection and empathy** (s005, ch.9):
- **Large eyes** → perceived vulnerability → protective instinct (why baby animals, Mario, Yoshi, and most mascot characters have large eyes)
- **Symmetrical features** → health, trustworthiness
- **Expressive animation** → emotional state communicated instantly, often overriding verbal content
- **Consistent motivation** → makes the agent feel like it has goals, not like a prop

**Hodent** (s014, ch.8): emotions are **fast, pre-cognitive evaluations**. The amygdala responds to character expressions before the cortex can reason about them. A character's fear communicates to the player faster than any text label.

## 3. Player Character Design

### The Silent vs. Voiced Protagonist Tradeoff

| Approach | Examples | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **Silent protagonist** | Link (Zelda), Gordon Freeman (Half-Life), the character in *Celeste* | Maximum player projection; player imagines themselves in the role |
| **Voiced/characterized** | Geralt (The Witcher), Kratos (God of War), Ellie (The Last of Us) | Rich narrative arcs; specific authored personality; character-driven drama |
| **Customizable/player-defined** | Most RPG protagonists | Player authorship; investment through personalization |

**Key principle:** align the choice with the game's narrative goals. If the game is about *player choices mattering*, maximize projection space. If the game is about *a specific person's journey*, a characterized protagonist carries that story better.

**Schell's warning** (s005, ch.18): a silent protagonist makes it harder to carry a rich character arc — the character has no voice to express growth. A voiced protagonist narrows the player's identification space — "Geralt wouldn't do that" can break immersion. Neither is universally superior.

### Avatar Design Principles

**Silhouette readability** (visual design): the player character must be instantly recognizable at any distance, even in visual chaos. Distinctive silhouette, strong color contrast against the typical environment, clear movement animations.

**Visual personality communication:** the character's visual design communicates their personality before the player reads a word of dialogue:
- Round, soft shapes → friendly, safe, harmless
- Angular, sharp shapes → dangerous, aggressive, intense
- Large, heavy proportions → powerful, slow, imposing
- Small, nimble proportions → quick, agile, vulnerable

**Animation as personality:** how the character moves is as important as how they look. A character who lands heavily communicates weight. A character who bounces communicates energy. Players experience the character through movement thousands of times per session — animation is the dominant personality communicator.

## 4. NPC Design Principles

**Adams** (s006, ch.10) and Schell (s005, ch.18) converge on the same functional framework.

### The Four Functions of NPCs

1. **Narrative delivery** — quests, backstory, worldbuilding, exposition, dialogue
2. **Indirect control** — guiding player attention, movement, and decision-making (s005, ch.16)
3. **Emotional texture** — making the world feel inhabited; creating attachment that motivates care about outcomes
4. **Mechanical challenge** — enemies, rivals, and puzzle-posing entities

**Design test:** for each NPC, which of these four functions are they serving? An NPC serving only one function is a candidate for removal or combination. An NPC serving all four simultaneously is excellent design.

### Believability, Not Realism

NPCs don't need to simulate full human cognition. They need to be **consistent with the world's logic** and **emotionally resonant** within their role.

**The simplicity principle** (s005, ch.18):
> *"An NPC who always behaves consistently within their defined character is more believable than one with many behaviors that occasionally contradict each other. Simpler but consistent beats complex but contradictory."*

A merchant who is always grumpy, slightly helpful, and fixated on profit is more believable than one with 20 random behavioral modes that sometimes contradict. Consistency creates the *impression* of a personality.

**Genre conventions calibrate the threshold:** a fantasy RPG NPC following a patrol route feels natural. A modern thriller NPC following the same route feels uncanny. The world's physics and narrative logic set the standard.

### Enemy Character Design

Enemies are NPCs with a specific mechanical purpose: to provide challenge. But enemy *design* — visual, behavioral, auditory — communicates their nature:
- Visual design communicates threat level (small/nimble = fast challenge; large/heavy = powerful challenge)
- Pre-attack animations (tells) communicate timing (fairness principle — the player had the information)
- Behavioral patterns teach the player the enemy's mechanic language

**Kremers** (s012, ch.2): enemy placement and design is one of the primary teaching tools in level design. Enemies encountered for the first time should be placed where the player can safely observe them before engaging.

## 5. Character Arcs and Transformation

**Schell's transformation lens** (s005, Lens #81):
> *"Does the protagonist change? Is the transformation meaningful and earned?"*

**The hero's journey pattern** (s005, Lens #68) provides the structural scaffolding:
1. Call to adventure — the character is pulled from their ordinary world
2. Threshold crossing — commitment to the journey
3. Trials — challenges that test and change the character
4. Ordeal — the defining moment of transformation
5. Resolution — the changed character returns to the world (or a new world)

**The inner contradiction** (Lens #82): the most compelling protagonists have a flaw or internal conflict that must be resolved for the story to reach full emotional impact. The resolution of this inner contradiction is the true climax — not the external victory.

### Games and Character Transformation

The challenge: player agency makes authored character transformation difficult. The player might not make the choices the arc requires, or might play in ways that contradict the character's supposed growth.

**Solutions used in practice:**
- Cutscenes deliver transformation regardless of player action (character arc protected from player)
- Mechanical reflection (unlocked abilities reflect narrative growth — the character *can* do more because they've grown)
- Multiple ending arcs (player's choices determine which transformation arc plays out)

## 6. Interpersonal Dynamics

**The interpersonal circumplex** (Schell, s005, Lens #78): characters can be mapped on two axes:
- **Dominant ↔ Submissive** (who has power)
- **Friendly ↔ Hostile** (disposition toward others)

A cast covering different positions on this grid creates natural dramatic tension:
- A dominant-hostile villain contrasts with a dominant-friendly hero
- A submissive-friendly companion provides emotional warmth
- A submissive-hostile antagonist (resents others but can't directly oppose them) creates interesting friction

**Character web** (Lens #79): relationships between characters should be mapped. Relationships that *change over time* create narrative momentum. Static relationships (character A is always Character B's friend) feel flat.

**Status transactions** (Lens #80): status is a primary human social motivator. Moments where characters gain or lose status are inherently dramatic. A protagonist defeating a bully gains status; being publicly humiliated loses it. Design these status transactions deliberately.

## 7. Characters as Indirect Control

**Schell** (s005, ch.16): characters are one of the six methods of indirect control:

- **Explicit guidance:** NPC says "the treasure is north" → player goes north
- **Implicit guidance:** NPC runs toward objective → player follows
- **Environmental storytelling:** dead NPC near a door → player tries the door
- **Character gaze:** NPC looks in a direction → player looks there
- **Companion positioning:** companion standing at a specific location draws player there

Characters who guide movement feel like invitation, not instruction. The player follows because they want to (narrative pull) rather than because they're compelled to (mechanical push).

## 9. Hot Potato — Character Design Implications

| Decision | Rationale |
|---|---|
| 8 distinct character designs with high-contrast silhouettes | Observable play; player identity; team readability |
| Cartoon proportions (large eyes, expressive faces) | Party game tone; triggers empathy mechanism; makes characters likeable even when losing |
| Exaggerated emotional animation (fear when Potato, triumph when tagging) | Communication through animation; projection is emotional |
| No silent protagonist dilemma — characters are avatars, not protagonists | Party game: character is a toy, not a narrative agent |
| Voice lines/reactions specific to each character | Personality through audio; reinforces identity; creates attachment |
| Potato role communicated through animation (distress, urgency) | The character *shows* that the role is stressful; player's stress is externalized |
| Loss state animation (Potato "loses" dramatically) | Punchline mechanic: the character's reaction sells the comedy of defeat |
