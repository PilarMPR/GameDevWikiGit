# Mark Cerny

**Role:** Game designer, producer, systems architect · **Known for:** Marble Madness, Crash Bandicoot, Spyro, Ratchet & Clank, PlayStation 4 and 5 lead system architect

---

## Key ideas

- **The Cerny Method** (also called "2-level pre-production"): before entering full production, spend roughly two years building only two polished levels that demonstrate the core experience works. If those two levels aren't demonstrably fun, the design is not ready for production. Full production begins only after the core is proven. (cited in s005, ch.7)
  - This directly addresses the industry's most common failure mode: spending years and millions building a game around an unproven core mechanic, then discovering in late production that the core isn't fun.
- **Pillar-based design**: define the game's design pillars — 3–5 fundamental principles — before production begins. Every major decision is evaluated against these pillars. This produces alignment without requiring constant escalation to a creative director.
- **Technology as creative constraint and enabler**: Cerny's platform architecture work (PS4, PS5) demonstrates that hardware design and game design are not separate disciplines. The SSD in PS5, for example, was designed to eliminate loading screens — a direct game design decision.

## Notable works

- **Marble Madness** (1984, Atari) — designed at age 17; a technically and mechanically innovative arcade game
- **Crash Bandicoot** (1996, Naughty Dog) — producer and designer; a landmark in 3D platformer design
- **Spyro the Dragon** (1998, Insomniac) — producer; defined open-world 3D platformer aesthetics
- **Ratchet & Clank series** (2002–, Insomniac) — long-running franchise demonstrating consistent quality through pillar-based design
- **PlayStation 4** (2013) — lead system architect; the console's design reflected a deep understanding of game development pipeline needs
- **PlayStation 5** (2020) — lead system architect; the custom SSD and DualSense haptic system reflect game design priorities at hardware level
- **Knack** (2013, Japan Studio) — director; the PS4 launch title

## Tensions with other thinkers

- **vs. Schell's Rule of the Loop (s005)**: Schell emphasizes maximizing iteration count; Cerny emphasizes proving the core before scaling. Both are about de-risking early, but the emphasis differs — Schell on speed of iteration, Cerny on the milestone threshold before full production commitment.
- **vs. Fullerton's playcentric (s008)**: Fullerton treats playtesting as continuous throughout; Cerny treats the 2-level milestone as the production gate. These can be integrated: continuous playtesting during pre-production, with the Cerny milestone as the go/no-go criterion.
