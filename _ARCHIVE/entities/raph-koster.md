# Raph Koster

**Role:** Game designer, author, entrepreneur · **Known for:** Ultima Online, Star Wars Galaxies, *A Theory of Fun*

---

## Key ideas

- **Fun = learning**: fun is the pleasurable signal the brain produces when it successfully acquires a new pattern. The brain is a "voracious consumer of patterns," and games are "exceptionally tasty patterns." (s016)
- **Grokking**: the deepest form of pattern acquisition — not just intellectual understanding but complete internalization, where the pattern becomes automatic. When a game's patterns are fully grokked, boredom is inevitable. (s016)
- **Boredom as the failure signal**: "Boredom is the opposite of learning. When a game stops teaching us, we feel bored." Boredom is the brain reporting that no new patterns are available. (s016)
- **Games as teachers**: "That's what games are, in the end. Teachers. Fun is just another word for learning." Games are formal systems stripped of real-world noise that present patterns for the brain to master. (s016)
- **Games as an expressive medium**: alongside Anthropy & Clark (s002), Koster argues games can carry meaning about the human condition — not just skill challenges. Art games teach something about what it means to be human.
- **The learnable middle = the flow channel**: Koster's "learnable middle" is structurally identical to Csikszentmihalyi's flow channel — both describe the zone where patterns are learnable but not yet trivial.

## Notable works

- *A Theory of Fun for Game Design* (2004, 2nd ed. 2013) — a short, illustrated book that is among the most influential texts in game design theory. (s016 in this wiki)
- Ultima Online (1997) — one of the first massively multiplayer online games; foundational to the MMORPG genre
- Star Wars Galaxies (2003) — ambitious, player-driven economy and crafting systems; notorious for a controversial "New Game Enhancements" redesign
- Metaplace (2008–2010) — a web-based virtual worlds platform; ahead of its time

## Living output

- raphkoster.com — regular blog posts extending and applying Theory of Fun theory; responds to current industry debates
- Talks: "A Theory of Fun: 10 Years Later" (GDC 2013) — revisiting the theory with a decade's hindsight
- Active on social media; prolific commentator on game design

## Tensions with other thinkers

- **vs. LeBlanc (MDA, s011)**: MDA rejects "fun" as too vague and replaces it with 8 specific aesthetics. Koster gives "fun" a specific meaning (learning/mastery). Reconcilable: Koster's "fun" ≈ MDA's Challenge + Discovery aesthetics specifically. MDA covers the full aesthetic space; Koster focuses on the most durable form of engagement.
- **vs. Sellers (s001)**: Sellers argues that sustained engagement is the goal, not "fun" — a game can be deeply engaging without being pleasurable in the conventional sense (horror, tragedy). Koster would likely agree that his "fun" includes non-hedonic engagement (mastering a horror game's patterns is fun in his sense).
- **vs. Macklin & Sharp (s009)**: Macklin & Sharp note that absorption — deep engagement without the strict challenge=skill balance of flow — is also valid. Koster's theory requires learnable patterns; absorption can occur without them. A genuine tension, not just vocabulary.
