# Katie Salen & Eric Zimmerman

**Role:** Co-authors of *Rules of Play*; game designers; educators · **Institution:** Salen: DePaul University, founded Quest to Learn; Zimmerman: NYU Game Center

---

## Key ideas (shared)

- **Meaningful play**: play where the relationship between player action and system outcome is both discernible (perceivable) and integrated (mattering over time). The central design goal. (s013)
- **Magic circle as design concept**: the conceptual boundary separating the game world from ordinary life; adopted from Huizinga and made into a design tool. (s013 ch.9)
- **Three schemas**: games must be analyzed at three levels simultaneously — formal (rules), experiential (play), and cultural (culture). Each is necessary; none is sufficient alone. (s013)
- **Games as systems**: games are formal systems with feedback loops; understanding games requires cybernetic and systems thinking. (s013)
- **The game definition**: "a system in which players engage in an artificial conflict, defined by rules, that results in a quantifiable outcome." (s013) — the most widely cited game definition in the field
- **Emergent narrative vs. embedded narrative**: games contain both designed story (embedded) and player-generated story (emergent); good design works at both levels. (s013)

## Key ideas (Salen specifically)

- **Quest to Learn (Q2L)**: a New York City public school designed around game-based learning. The curriculum uses "game-like learning" principles — challenge, feedback, iteration, meaningful failure — to structure all subjects. Directly relevant to the educational games studio context.
- **Games for change**: Salen has been involved in the broader movement to use games for social impact; her work bridges game design and educational policy.

## Key ideas (Zimmerman specifically)

- **Lusory attitude** (credited to Suits, but developed in Rules of Play): the voluntary acceptance of constitutive rules as a condition of play — the psychological prerequisite for games.
- **NYU Game Center**: a graduate program focused on game design as a discipline; produces some of the most theoretically sophisticated designers currently working.

## Notable works

- *Rules of Play: Game Design Fundamentals* (2003, with Katie Salen) — the most comprehensive academic treatment of game design; 700+ pages. (s013 in this wiki)
- *The Game Design Reader* (2006, eds. Salen & Zimmerman) — anthology of key game studies texts
- Quest to Learn School, NYC (Salen, 2009–) — proof of concept for game-based learning at scale

## Living output

- Eric Zimmerman blogs and speaks actively; "Manifesto for a Ludic Century" (2013) is a widely read statement on games as the dominant art form of the 21st century
- Katie Salen's educational work has produced academic publications; search Institute of Play and Q2L
- Both are active in academic game studies (DiGRA, Foundations of Digital Games)

## Tensions with other thinkers

- **vs. Huizinga (s004)**: They adopt the magic circle from Huizinga but immediately acknowledge its porousness — the circle is never fully sealed. Huizinga's version is more utopian; theirs is more design-realistic.
- **vs. Sellers (s001)**: Both use systems thinking, but Salen & Zimmerman include cultural analysis as essential; Sellers focuses on system mechanics. Complementary scopes.
- **vs. Schell (s005)**: Rules of Play is exhaustive and academic; Art of Game Design is practitioner and craft-oriented. Neither is wrong — different audiences, different purposes.
