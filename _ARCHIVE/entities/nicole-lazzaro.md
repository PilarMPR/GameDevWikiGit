# Nicole Lazzaro

**Role:** Game researcher, UX designer, author · **Company:** XEO Design (San Francisco) · **Known for:** 4 Keys to Fun framework, emotion in games research

---

## Key ideas

- **4 Keys to Fun**: a practitioner-oriented alternative to MDA's 8 aesthetics, derived from observational research watching people play games:
  1. **Hard Fun** — challenges, strategies, puzzles; the emotion is frustration leading to pride. Players who seek this want to feel clever and accomplished.
  2. **Easy Fun** — exploration, curiosity, wonderment; the emotion is wonder. Players who seek this want to lose themselves in a world.
  3. **Serious Fun** — meaning, personal improvement, real-world goals; the emotion is excitement or relaxation. Players who seek this want the game to matter beyond the game.
  4. **People Fun** — social interaction, cooperation, competition; the emotion is amusement. Players who seek this want to share the experience.
- **Emotions as core game design material**: emotions are not a byproduct of good design — they are the material you are designing. Every mechanic should be evaluated for the emotion it produces, not just the rule it creates.
- **Research-based approach**: Lazzaro's framework comes from observational research, not theory — watching 30+ people play games and categorizing what made them stop, laugh, tense up, or disengage. This is its core strength and its limitation (sample was small by modern standards).

## Position in the wiki

Lazzaro's 4 Keys fills a gap in [[../concepts/player/player-types]] — her framework is more audience-segmented than Bartle's and more practitioner-friendly than MDA. It is particularly useful for party game design (Hot Potato must serve Hard Fun, Easy Fun, and People Fun simultaneously) and educational games (Serious Fun is the primary target). ## Tensions with other thinkers

- **vs. MDA (s011)**: The 8 aesthetics are more granular and designer-facing; Lazzaro's 4 Keys are coarser but audience-segmented and emotion-labeled. Both are useful; MDA for designing toward an experience, Lazzaro for understanding the audience you're designing for.
- **vs. Bartle**: Both are player typologies; Lazzaro's is motivation-based and emotion-labeled; Bartle's is orientation-based (world vs. player axis). Complementary: use both.
