# Parts, Loops, and Wholes

The formal vocabulary of systems in [[../sources/s001-advanced-game-design|Advanced Game Design]] (s001, ch.2),
and the three levels a systemic designer works at (s001, ch.5).

## The definition
> "A set of parts that together form loops of interaction between them to create a
> persistent *whole*. The whole has its own properties and behaviors belonging to the
> group but not to any single part within it." (s001, ch.2)

Equivalent top-down phrasing: *"A system is the integrated whole that arises out of
independent, interacting parts. Those parts have their own internal state, boundaries, and
behaviors by which they mutually affect each other. This whole persists over time, adapts to
external conditions, and has its own coordinated behaviors that emerge from the interactions
of its parts."* (s001, ch.2)

## Parts
- Have **internal state, boundaries, and behaviors** by which they affect each other (s001, ch.2).
- In a game, the most detailed/grounded level: **attributes, values, and behaviors**, down to
  **atomic** (indivisible) parts; documented in text and spreadsheets to be implementable (s001, ch.8).

## Loops
- Connecting parts via their behaviors forms **loops**, which create the emergent whole —
  "more than the sum of the parts" (s001, ch.7).
- **Reinforcing loop**: an effect feeds back to amplify itself (e.g. governor speeding up).
- **Balancing loop**: an effect feeds back to counteract itself (e.g. governor slowing down).
  Both illustrated with the centrifugal governor (s001, ch.2). Relationships may be linear but
  most are **nonlinear**.
- See the game-specific loops in [[the-four-principal-loops]].

## Wholes
- The persistent, emergent system with its own properties — for games, the overall
  **experience** captured in the design vision / concept document (s001, ch.6).
