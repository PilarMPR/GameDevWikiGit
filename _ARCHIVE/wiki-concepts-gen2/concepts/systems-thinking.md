# Systems Thinking (in game design)

The foundational lens of [[../sources/s001-advanced-game-design|Advanced Game Design]] (s001):
**games are systems, and game design is system design.**

## Core idea
- A **system** is understood both in terms of its constituent parts *and* how they
  dynamically combine into something greater — both at once. Any definition of a system
  must itself be systemic (s001, ch.2).
- Systems are **dynamic, not static**: you can't understand one by freezing it; you must
  see it operating in context (s001, ch.2).
- Systems are everywhere — physical and social — and form **hierarchies**: a system contains
  lower-level systems and is part of higher-level ones (s001, ch.2).
- Embodies Christopher Alexander's "quality without a name" (s001, ch.2).

## Why designers need it
- Requires **metacognition** — thinking about how you (and players) think (s001, ch.1).
- Lets a designer switch between **bottom-up** (parts → whole) and **top-down**
  (whole → parts) views of their game at will, which the book calls a particular skill
  game designers need (s001, ch.2).
- Seeing the world as systems is what enables creating **emergent, engaging** game systems
  rather than static content (s001, ch.1, ch.7).

## Other sources that frame games as systems
- **Salen & Zimmerman** ([[../sources/s013-rules-of-play|s013]]) — games as **cybernetic
  systems** with feedback loops, plus information-theory and game-theory system views.
- **Fullerton** ([[../sources/s008-game-design-workshop|s008]]) — **system dynamics**:
  objects, properties, behaviors, relationships.
- **MDA** ([[../sources/s011-mda-framework|s011]]) — games as **artifacts** whose content is
  their *behavior* (≈ dynamics), not media. See [[mda-framework]].
