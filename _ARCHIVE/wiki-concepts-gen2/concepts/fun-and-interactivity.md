# Interactivity and Fun

The systemic account of what makes games engaging — and why "fun" is an inadequate target concept for game designers. Three major sources give different but reconcilable answers.

---

## Sellers: engagement over fun (s001, ch.4)

Sellers' foundational argument: a game does not have to be "fun" in the conventional hedonic sense — **as long as it remains highly interactive and engaging**.

> "Fun is too shallow a target. What we're really designing for is sustained engagement — the player's ongoing desire to continue interacting with the game system." (s001, ch.4, paraphrased)

The game+player system produces engagement through the **interactive loops** — the reciprocating feedback between player intent and game state. What keeps a player playing is not a constant pleasurable stimulus but a continuously interesting loop of action → consequence → new decision.

### The components of engagement (s001, ch.4)

Sellers identifies three interacting components:

1. **Mental models** — the player's internal representation of how the game works. Engagement requires that the mental model is continuously updated: too little update (nothing new to learn) = boredom; too much update at once (model can't absorb the new information) = frustration. The healthy zone = continuous small model updates at the edge of comprehension.

2. **Arousal** — the player's physiological and emotional activation level. Too low = boredom (understimulated); too high = anxiety (overwhelmed). The optimum is the **flow channel** (Csikszentmihalyi), where arousal matches the challenge level. Sellers maps this directly to the interactive loop: arousal is regulated by the loop's pace and stakes.

3. **Engagement** — the emergent quality of the interaction when mental model updating and appropriate arousal are both present. Engagement is the *felt* quality of the game+player system working correctly. It is not identical to pleasure — horror games, tragedy games, and difficult games can produce deep engagement without being "pleasant."

### The provocative conclusion

A game can be scary, frustrating, melancholy, or morally disturbing and still be profoundly engaging — and therefore succeeding as design. The game's job is to maintain the interactive loop in the healthy zone (appropriate arousal, continuous model updating), not to produce a specific emotional valence.

This directly challenges the naive view that "if it's not fun, it's failed." It also opens space for art games, educational games, and serious games that produce engagement without conventional fun.

## Koster: fun = pattern learning (s016)

Koster's mechanistic account: fun is the pleasurable cognitive signal that the brain produces when it successfully acquires a new pattern.

> "Fun from games arises out of mastery. It arises out of comprehension. It is the act of solving puzzles. In other words, with games, learning is the drug." (s016, ch.4)

**Why this matters for engagement**: learning is intrinsically motivated — the brain seeks patterns because doing so was evolutionarily adaptive. Games provide high-density, low-noise pattern environments. The player is "having fun" as long as they are learning; the moment the patterns are exhausted, boredom is inevitable.

**Koster's diagnostic:**
- Game not fun? Ask: what pattern is failing to be learned?
- Patterns too trivial (visible immediately) → boredom
- Patterns too noisy (look random) → frustration
- Patterns learnable at the right pace → engagement (fun)

The overlap with Sellers: both identify the "right pace of model updating" as the core condition for engagement. Koster names it "pattern acquisition"; Sellers names it "mental model updating." The same phenomenon, different vocabularies.

## MDA: dissolving "fun" into 8 aesthetics (s011)

Hunicke, LeBlanc & Zubek's response to the problem of "fun": **abandon the word entirely** and replace it with 8 specific aesthetic experiences that games can deliberately target.

| Aesthetic | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| Sensation | Game as sense-pleasure |
| Fantasy | Game as make-believe |
| Narrative | Game as drama |
| Challenge | Game as obstacle course |
| Fellowship | Game as social framework |
| Discovery | Game as uncharted territory |
| Expression | Game as self-discovery |
| Submission | Game as pastime |

**The value of this replacement**: "fun" is a single word covering wildly different experiences. A horror game and a racing game are both "fun" but provide completely different aesthetics (Sensation + Fantasy vs. Challenge + Sensation). When designers target "fun," they don't know what they're designing for. When they target "Fellowship + Challenge," they can make concrete design decisions.

**Engagement through multiple aesthetics**: MDA doesn't claim any one aesthetic is primary. Different players (and the same player at different times) seek different aesthetics. The richest games provide multiple aesthetics simultaneously at different intensities.

## The reconciliation: sustained engagement as the unifying concept

All three frameworks converge:

| Framework | Their term | The underlying claim |
|-----------|-----------|---------------------|
| Sellers s001 | Engagement | Continuous interactive loop → ongoing mental model updating + appropriate arousal |
| Koster s016 | Fun = learning | Ongoing pattern acquisition → pleasurable cognitive signal |
| MDA s011 | Target aesthetics | Specific emotional responses → produced by mechanics → create the reason to continue |

**Synthesis**: the "real target" of game design is **sustained engagement** — the player's ongoing desire to continue interacting with the game system. Fun, pleasure, and aesthetics are the *qualities* of that engagement, not the engagement itself. A game can produce many different aesthetic qualities while maintaining engagement; it fails when the interactive loop collapses (boredom, frustration, or confusion).

> ⭐ **Design implication**: don't design for "fun." Design for sustained engagement by:
> 1. Maintaining the interactive loop (Sellers — always give the player something meaningful to do)
> 2. Providing learnable patterns at the right pace (Koster — keep the learning signal active)
> 3. Targeting specific aesthetic experiences (MDA — know what kind of engagement you're designing for)

## Engagement and the ethical dimension (s014, s017)

Hodent adds the ethical counterpoint: not all engagement mechanisms serve the player well.

- **Intrinsic engagement**: the player engages because the game itself is genuinely interesting (pattern learning, aesthetic experience, social connection)
- **Extrinsic engagement**: the player continues because of external rewards, FOMO, or habit formation that is disconnected from the game's genuine value

Variable-ratio reinforcement schedules (loot boxes, slot machine mechanics) produce engagement through habit formation, not genuine interest. This produces engagement *metrics* without engagement *value* — players continue but don't feel they're gaining anything.

Ethical design maintains engagement through intrinsic mechanisms: genuine pattern depth, authentic aesthetic experiences, and meaningful social connection. → [[../player/motivation]], [[../business/monetization]]
