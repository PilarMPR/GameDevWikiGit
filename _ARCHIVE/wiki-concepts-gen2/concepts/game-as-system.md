# The Game as a System

How [[../sources/s001-advanced-game-design|Advanced Game Design]] (s001, ch.3) analyzes a game
in systemic terms.

## What a game is
- Takes place in a separate, **nonconsequential** context — the **magic circle** (after
  Huizinga): its own space and time "within which special rules obtain" (s001, ch.3).
- Play is **voluntary** and requires **participation, not just observation** (s001, ch.3).
- Provides players a defined world, **meaningful decisions**, opposition, interaction, and
  different types of goals (s001, ch.3).

## Three systemic levels of a game
1. **Structures** — the game's parts: its tokens and rules, the "nouns and verbs" of the game.
2. **Functional elements** — the looping operational components built as "phrases" from those
   nouns and verbs; they create the game's **world model** (a second-order creation) and, in
   turn, the player's mental model, meaningful decisions, and goals.
3. **Architectural & thematic construction** — the whole game experience: the balance of
   content and systems, narrative, and overall play.
   (All three: s001, ch.3.)

## The game + player system
- The playful experience exists **only** when game and player come together as parts of a
  larger system (s001, ch.3–4). The game has an internal model; the player builds a
  **mental model**; they couple through interactive loops. See [[fun-and-interactivity]].
