# World Building

The craft of constructing a coherent, believable, and compelling fictional world for a game — the background against which story and play occur. A game world isn't just scenery; it is the context that makes mechanics meaningful, the space that holds narrative, and the canvas for player imagination.

---

## What a game world does (s005, ch.17; s012, ch.13)

A game world serves multiple simultaneous functions:

1. **Contextualizes mechanics**: a sword attack feels different when the world establishes it as combat between knights than when it's abstract. The world gives mechanical actions meaning beyond their function.
2. **Houses narrative**: story only works within a consistent world; inconsistencies break immersion and undermine emotional investment.
3. **Motivates player movement**: an interesting world gives the player reasons to explore — there is something beyond the next corner.
4. **Encodes theme**: the visual and structural design of a world can embody thematic ideas (a world of decay conveys entropy; a world of vertical hierarchy conveys social stratification).
5. **Enables imagination**: a well-designed world gives players enough cues to fill in the rest with imagination (Schell's binocular effect: close-up detail remembered, then imagination fills the gaps). → [[../../player/player-psychology]]

---

## Consistency as foundation (s005, ch.17)

Schell's core principle: **the world must be internally consistent**. Players accept impossible worlds (dragons exist, magic works) without hesitation — but they notice and are disturbed by inconsistencies within a world's own logic.

> The question is not "is this realistic?" but "does this follow the rules this world has established?"

If the world establishes that fire is dangerous, fire must always be dangerous (or the exception must be explained). If the world establishes that locked doors can be picked, all locked doors should be pickable (or the exceptions clearly marked as different). Players build mental models of world-rules, and violation of those rules feels like betrayal. (s015, Norman — mental model alignment) → [[../../player/player-psychology]]

**Design implication**: before adding a world element, ask: "does this fit the rules I've already established?" If not, either don't add it or revise the rules explicitly.

## World structure and genre conventions (s012, ch.13; s006)

### Closed vs. open worlds
- **Closed world**: the game space is bounded and fully authored. The player can experience everything the designer made. Design challenge: make the bounded space feel alive and sufficient.
- **Open world**: the game space is expansive with player-generated exploration paths. Design challenge: ensure density and variety of interesting content; avoid "empty" world syndrome.

### Biomes and regions
Large worlds are typically organized into distinct regions with consistent visual, audio, and mechanical identity. Biomes serve:
- **Navigational orientation**: the player knows which region they're in
- **Tonal variety**: each region delivers a different emotional experience
- **Gameplay variety**: different regions have different environmental hazards, enemy types, and mechanics

### Vertical layers
World architecture often has vertical layers: sky/surface/underground. Each layer conventionally signals different content (surface = main game; underground = danger/treasure; sky = freedom/aspiration). Level design exploits these conventions as pre-built indirect control.

## Environmental storytelling (s012; s005; s003)

The world tells story without words through:

- **What has happened**: damage, detritus, and debris tell stories of past events. A burned village says something about what preceded the player's arrival. A laboratory with overturned equipment suggests the experiment went wrong.
- **Who lives here**: the design of a space tells us about its inhabitants. A tidy, systematic arrangement of objects suggests one kind of character; a chaotic, defensive pile of barricades suggests another.
- **What is valued**: what is most prominent in a space signals what matters. A throne room tells you about power. A kitchen full of luxuries tells you about wealth. A bare cell tells you about deprivation.
- **What is happening now**: active environmental elements (fires still burning, enemies in mid-patrol, machinery running) make the world feel like it exists independent of the player.

> "The environment tells the story before the player arrives and after they leave. It is the only story tool that works when no one is watching." (s012 paraphrased)

## World and theme (s005, ch.5 and ch.17)

Schell argues strongly for world design being driven by **theme** — the core thematic statement the game is making. The theme is not the story (what happens) but the meaning behind it (what it's about).

Examples:
- A game about freedom vs. safety should have a world that makes both tangible — spaces where freedom is beautiful but dangerous, and spaces where safety is comfortable but constraining
- A game about corruption should have a world where corruption is visible in the architecture, the characters, the economy
- A game about hope should have a world that maintains beauty and possibility even in darkness

When theme drives world design, the world doesn't just hold the story — it *is* the argument the story is making. The player experiences the theme spatially and procedurally, not just narratively. → [[../../theory/meaningful-play]] (procedural rhetoric)

## World-building as fiction contract (s013)

Salen & Zimmerman's three schemas (formal/experiential/cultural) apply to world-building:

- **Formal layer**: what the rules say the world contains (which objects exist, what actions are possible)
- **Experiential layer**: what the player feels the world is like (the texture of exploration, the emotional tone)
- **Cultural layer**: how the world relates to real-world cultures, histories, and ideologies

A world that handles all three coherently creates the fullest sense of a real place. A fantasy world might be formally distinct from reality (magic, mythical creatures) while being experientially grounded (characters have motivations players recognize) and culturally resonant (the world reflects or refracts real human concerns).

## Transmedia worlds (s005, ch.17)

Henry Jenkins coined the concept of **transmedia worlds** — fantasy worlds that can be entered through many different media (print, video, animation, toys, games, and others). The key insight: **the world exists apart from the media that support it**. The media are gateways into the world, not the world itself.

### The Star Wars case study
When Star Wars launched in 1977, the action figures that followed were wildly successful. Schell's analysis of *why* reveals something surprising: children playing with the figures seldom re-enacted scenes from the film. Instead, they invented entirely new stories, gave characters different names and relationships, and used the toys as vehicles for original dramas.

This means it wasn't the film's plot or even its characters per se that children loved — it was **the world of Star Wars**. The toys were valuable because they provided a gateway into that world: interactive, participatory, flexible, portable, and social.

> "More and more often, the real product that is created is not a story, or a toy, or a game, but a world. But you can't sell a world, so various products are sold as gateways into this world, each leading to different parts of it." (s005, ch.17)

**The consistency requirement**: if the gateways contradict each other (game says X, film says not-X), the world **crumbles into dust**. Players and viewers hold the world's rules in memory across all touchpoints; inconsistency isn't just a creative failure — it destroys commercial value by undermining the world's reality in the imagination.

### The Pokémon case study
Pokémon demonstrates transmedia world design at scale: the TV show, the game, the trading cards, and the merchandise all reflect the *same world rules* — specifically the same battle mechanics and creature types. The TV show gave the game's primitive Game Boy graphics a "binocular effect": watching Pokémon in full color and animation gave players a vivid mental image that they then mapped onto the crude sprites when playing. The world became more real, not less, through multiple gateways.

### The binocular effect
Named for the practice of looking through binoculars at a sporting event early on — to get close-up detail — and then setting them down while the close-up image remains in memory, allowing the imagination to fill in the detail for the rest of the event.

In game design: give players one close-up, high-detail experience of something in the world early on. Their imagination will sustain that detail level for the rest of the experience. This is how the Pokémon anime made primitive GameBoy graphics feel vivid.

Applied to game world-building: you don't need to fully render every aspect of your world. One detailed, loving moment in each area builds the mental model; the imagination sustains it.

## The 10 tips for world design (s005, ch.17)

Schell identifies specific properties that make worlds work:

1. **The world must make sense** — if events in your world don't follow logically from the world's rules, players lose trust in the world as a real place
2. **The world must be rooted in a feeling** — what is the emotional identity of this world? (grim, wondrous, melancholic, playful) — every element should reinforce it
3. **The world must support great stories** — the world's structure should make interesting conflicts inevitable
4. **The world should have many kinds of individuals** — diversity of characters with distinct perspectives makes the world feel inhabited and real
5. **The world should surprise** — predictable worlds are boring; unexpected elements that still feel consistent create delight
6. **The world should be worth revisiting** — depth is more valuable than breadth; better a smaller world with layers than a large world with nothing to discover on return
7. **The world should be compatible with the gameplay** — the rules of the world must not contradict the rules of the game (or must acknowledge and justify the contradiction)
8. **The world should be transmedia-ready** — if someone loves your world, what other forms could they access it through?
9. **The details of the world should be carefully curated** — every element should reinforce the world's theme and rules; no noise
10. **The world should have geographic or spatial clarity** — players should be able to form a mental map and feel oriented within the world

## Monomyth and world archetypes (s005, ch.17–18)

Schell draws on Joseph Campbell's **Hero's Journey** (monomyth) as a framework for understanding why certain world archetypes recur across cultures and media: they map to deep structures in human psychology that players (and readers, viewers) respond to strongly.

The classic world structure of the monomyth: a **mundane world** (familiar, safe, limited) and a **special world** (extraordinary, dangerous, transformative). The hero crosses from one to the other; their journey transforms them; they return changed.

Game worlds often exploit this structure directly:
- The village before the dungeon (mundane → special)
- The overworld before the boss room (safe → dangerous)
- The prologue before the adventure begins

This structure doesn't require an explicit "hero's journey" narrative — it's a spatial and tonal archetype that players recognize pre-consciously.
