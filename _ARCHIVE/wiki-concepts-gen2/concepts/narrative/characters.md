# Characters

The designed agents that inhabit the game world — player characters, NPCs, enemies, and companions. In games, characters operate differently from all other media: the player character is the player's proxy, and the player's relationship to it is not empathy but **projection and identification**.

---

## Player characters: projection, not empathy (s005, ch.9)

In film and literature, the audience experiences empathy: they feel *for* the character because the character is other. In games, the relationship is different:

> "In games, the player is not just watching the character — the player IS the character. The player is making the decisions. This projection is deeper than empathy with a film character; the player literally embodies the character's agency." (s005, ch.9)

Schell's argument: humans project decision-making capacity into perceived agents. Players project their entire identity into the player character — not just empathy, but full identification. This is why:
- Player character deaths feel personal, not observational
- Player character failures feel shameful, not sympathetic
- Player character growth feels rewarding, not vicarious

**Design implication**: the player character must be designed not just as a character (with their own personality, backstory, and arc) but as an **avatar for player projection**. The more clearly the player can project themselves into the character, the stronger the engagement.

---

## The silent protagonist problem (s005)

Schell discusses the tension between player character personality and player projection:

- **Silent protagonist** (Half-Life's Gordon Freeman, Zelda's Link): no defined personality → maximum player projection. The player can imagine the character as themselves. Disadvantage: difficult to carry a rich narrative character arc.
- **Voiced, characterized protagonist** (The Witcher's Geralt, Kratos): strong personality → rich narrative potential but narrower player identification. The player experiences the story through the character but may not fully project themselves.

Neither approach is universally superior. The choice should align with the game's narrative goals:
- If the game is about the player's choices mattering, a near-blank protagonist maximizes projection
- If the game is telling a specific story about a specific person, a characterized protagonist carries that story better

## NPCs: function and believability (s005, ch.18; s006)

Non-player characters serve multiple functions:

1. **Narrative delivery**: NPCs carry story information, quests, backstory, and world-building
2. **Indirect control**: NPCs guide player attention and movement (see [[indirect-control]])
3. **Emotional texture**: NPCs make the world feel inhabited and alive; players respond emotionally to characters they encounter repeatedly
4. **Mechanical challenge**: enemy NPCs provide the primary challenge of combat systems; AI behaviors are gameplay mechanics

**Believability, not realism**: NPCs don't need to be realistic (fully simulated humans) to be believable (consistent with the world's logic and emotionally resonant). The threshold for acceptable NPC behavior is set by the world's genre conventions. A fantasy RPG NPC who follows a patrol route feels fine; a modern thriller NPC who follows an identical patrol route feels uncanny.

**Consistency over complexity**: an NPC who always behaves consistently within their defined character is more believable than one with many behaviors that occasionally contradict each other. Simpler but consistent beats complex but inconsistent. (s005, ch.18)

## The empathy mechanism (s005, ch.9)

Schell draws on Heider & Simmel's classic experiment: humans project personality, intent, and emotion onto abstract shapes moving on a screen. We cannot help but see a "bully" and a "victim" in simple geometric motions.

In games:
- **Large eyes** signal vulnerability and trigger protective instincts (babies, Pikmin, Yoshi)
- **Symmetrical faces** signal trustworthiness and health
- **Expressive animation** communicates emotional state instantly, often overriding any verbal content
- **Consistent motivation** makes characters feel like agents with goals rather than props

> "We can project emotional life onto any agent we perceive to have intentions. The game designer can exploit this freely — the cost of creating a relatable character is lower than intuition suggests." (s005, ch.9 paraphrased)

## Character and theme (s005, ch.5)

Like world design, character design should serve the game's theme:

- Characters who embody the theme's tensions (a character who represents freedom in a game about freedom vs. safety)
- Characters whose arcs enact the thematic argument (the character who chooses safety over freedom and suffers for it; the one who chooses freedom and pays a different cost)
- Player character alignment with theme: the player character's mechanical abilities should reinforce their narrative identity. A character defined as stealthy should have stealth mechanics that reward the thematic choice.

When characters and theme are misaligned, the game's argument becomes muddled. When they align, every character interaction reinforces what the game is about. (s005, ch.5) → [[world-building]]

## Companions and sidekicks (s005, s012)

Companion characters — who accompany the player throughout the game — have unique design requirements:

- **Reliability**: a companion who dies frequently or blocks the player's path becomes a frustration, not an asset
- **Personality that complements the player character**: companions can carry emotional weight that a silent/near-silent protagonist cannot
- **Guidance without condescension**: companions who direct the player ("try hitting it with fire!") are useful when the player is stuck and irritating when the player has already figured it out
- **Emotional investment**: the companion's wellbeing matters narratively; if the player doesn't care whether the companion lives or dies, their presence fails

The companion as indirect control mechanism: companions who run ahead guide movement without explicit instruction, combining narrative (I want to follow my companion) with spatial (they're moving in this direction). → [[indirect-control]]

## Character across formal elements (s013, s008, s002)

Salen & Zimmerman distinguish:
- **Characters as formal elements**: defined properties, states, and behaviors — what the character *can do* in the game system
- **Characters as dramatic elements** (Fullerton): the narrative identity, motivation, and arc — who the character *is* in the story

Good character design integrates both: the character's formal properties (what actions they can perform, what they're capable of) express their dramatic identity (who they are and what they want).

Anthropy & Clark (s002) add: characters are defined by their verbs. What actions the player can perform *as* or *with* a character defines that character's narrative identity more deeply than any cutscene. The Tomb Raider character is defined by the climbing, jumping, and shooting verbs — these create Lara Croft as much as the story does.
