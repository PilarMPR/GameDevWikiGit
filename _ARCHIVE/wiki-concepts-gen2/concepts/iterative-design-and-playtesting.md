# Iterative Design & Playtesting

The strongest **methodological** consensus across the design books: you don't design a great
game on paper — you **build, test with players, learn, and repeat.**

## The shared loop
- **Macklin & Sharp** ([[../sources/s009-games-design-and-play|s009]], ch.5):
  **Conceptualize → Prototype → Playtest → Evaluate**, repeated; *embrace failure to succeed* (s009).
- **Fullerton** ([[../sources/s008-game-design-workshop|s008]]): the whole *playcentric* method
  is iterative prototyping + playtesting; prototypes are questions, design quality is **discovered
  through play** (s008).
- **Schell** ([[../sources/s005-art-of-game-design|s005]], ch.7–8): risk-driven looping,
  "eight tips for productive prototyping," **fail faster** (s005).
- **Sellers** ([[../sources/s001-advanced-game-design|s001]]): prototyping & playtesting close
  the **designer's loop** around the game+player system ([[the-four-principal-loops]]).
- **F2P handbook** ([[../sources/s017-f2p-design-handbook|s017]]): live-service tuning via
  feedback loops and data.

## Prototypes
- **"Playable questions"** — choose the prototype that answers your current uncertainty
  (s009, ch.10, which lists **8 kinds of prototypes**: paper, physical, playable, art/sound,
  interface, code/tech, …) (s009).
- Test for different things at different stages: foundation/structural, formal, dramatic, and
  **balance/fun** testing (s008).
