# Design Reference: Production & Process

---

## Schell's Lenses (s005)

### Ideation (ch.5–6)
**Lens #11 — Infinite Inspiration**
- What is the true source of inspiration for this game?
- Am I making what I want to make, or what I think I should make?
- Is the experience I want to create reflected in the design?

**Lens #12 — The Problem Statement**
- Can I clearly state the problem I am trying to solve?
- Have I restated the problem in as many different ways as possible?
- Have I identified the unspoken assumptions in the problem statement?
- What is the core constraint I'm working within? Does it need to be a constraint?

### Iteration (ch.7)
**Lens #13 — The Eight Filters**
Before moving forward with a design, check it against these eight filters:
1. Does it feel right from an artistic standpoint?
2. Will the target audience like it?
3. Is it genuinely novel? (Or just new-to-you)
4. Is it commercially viable? Will it sell?
5. Do the stakeholders (team, publisher, client) approve?
6. Is it playable with the available technology?
7. Does the team have the skills to build it?
8. Does the design achieve the designer's vision for the experience?

**Lens #14 — Risk Mitigation**
- What could go wrong with this design?
- Which risks are most likely to occur? Which have the highest impact?
- How can I test the riskiest elements first?
- What is the minimum prototype that would reveal whether the riskiest assumption is correct?

### Team & Process (ch.23–24)
**Lens #89 — The Team**
- Is the team aligned on what the game is trying to be?
- Does every team member feel ownership of the game?
- Are there communication breakdowns that are preventing good decisions?
- Is the team capable of making the game they are trying to make?

**Lens #90 — Documentation**
- Does the design documentation reflect the current state of the game?
- Does the documentation communicate the designer's intent clearly?
- Is the documentation used? Or is it being ignored?
- Is there a source of truth that everyone can refer to?

**Lens #91 — Playtesting**
- Are we playtesting with the right audience? (Not just the team)
- Are we playtesting at the right stage? (Not just at the end)
- What specific questions are we trying to answer with this playtest?
- Are we observing without interfering? Are we watching behavior rather than asking opinions?
- Are the playtest results actually changing the design?

### Technology & Future (ch.25–26)
**Lens #92 — Technology**
- What technology are we using? Is it the right technology for this game?
- Are there any technology risks that could prevent the game from being made?
- Are we building features that require technology we don't have?

**Lens #93 — The Crystal Ball**
- What will gaming be like in 2 years? 5 years? When this game ships?
- Are we designing for the present or the future?
- Are there trends that could make this game more or less relevant?

### Business & Responsibility (ch.27–28)
**Lens #94 — The Client**
- Who is the client? (Publisher, platform, internal stakeholder)
- What does the client want? Do I understand it fully?
- Is what the client wants the same as what the client needs?

**Lens #95 — The Pitch**
- What is the one sentence that describes this game?
- Is the pitch compelling? Does it make people want to play?
- Does the pitch accurately represent what the game actually is?

**Lens #96 — Profit**
- Is this game going to be profitable? How do I know?
- What is the business model? Is it appropriate for the game?
- Are the costs and revenues understood and realistic?

**Lens #97 — Transformation**
- Does this game transform the player? Does playing it change them in some way?
- Is that transformation positive?

**Lens #98 — Responsibility**
- Am I willing to be publicly responsible for this game?
- Does the game reflect my values?

**Lens #99 — The Raven**
- Does this game have heart? Does it have something real to say?
- Is there something in this game that only I could make?

**Lens #100 — Your Secret Purpose**
- Why am I making this game?
- What does this game mean to me?
- Have I found a way to put my true purpose into the game?

## Tips from Other Sources

### Fullerton: Playcentric design process (s008, ch.1)
**Per iteration:**
- What experience are we trying to create? (State it explicitly before each prototype)
- What specific questions is this prototype trying to answer?
- Is the prototype the minimum required to answer those questions? (Remove everything else)
- Are we playtesting with the right people for the current stage?

**Prototype principles:**
- Use physical prototypes when possible — they're 10x faster to modify
- Don't playtest with the team only — they know too much
- Make the prototype fail fast — learn quickly what doesn't work
- Document what broke and why, not just what works

**Playtesting conduct:**
- Define your observation goals before the session starts
- Never help playtesters or explain anything during play
- Watch behavior, not just what players say
- Look for patterns across multiple testers, not individual reactions
- After the session: separate observations from interpretations in your notes

### Macklin & Sharp: Design values framework (s009, ch.1–2)
Before each iteration, explicitly state:
1. **Design values**: what are the 3–5 things this game is trying to achieve?
2. **Design questions**: what specific things is this iteration trying to answer?
3. **Evaluation criteria**: how will we judge whether the iteration succeeded?

After each iteration:
- Did the prototype answer its questions? What did we learn?
- What does this tell us about the design values? Have any shifted?
- What is the single most important change to make next?

### Sellers: Designer's loop health (s001, ch.7)
**Per design decision:**
- What experience outcome am I designing toward?
- Does this decision serve that outcome?
- How will I know if it worked? (What to observe in playtest)
- Is this a systemic fix (addresses the root cause) or a symptomatic fix (patches the surface)?

**Systemic diagnostic questions:**
- Is the game's core loop clearly defined? Does everyone on the team agree?
- Are there loops within the core loop that are not yet designed?
- Are all feedback loops visible to the player?
- Are any reinforcing loops risking runaway dominance?

### Adams: Pre-production checklist (s006)
- Is the core loop fun in isolation (before level design, before story, before polish)?
- Is the game's unique selling point clear in the first 5 minutes?
- Does the game prototype without a tutorial suggest obvious actions?
- Are all five mechanic types considered? (Physics, economy, progression, tactical, social)
- What is the hardest thing to build? Has it been prototyped yet?

### Kremers: Level design process (s012, ch.4)
- Block out before art. No art investment until the gameplay layout works.
- Playtest the block-out with no guidance. Observe where confusion occurs.
- One mechanic introduction per encounter area. Never introduce two new things simultaneously.
- Validate the floorplan with at least 3 playtests before environment art begins.
- Track the interest curve for each level. Does it rise? Is there a climax?
