# Design Reference: Player & Psychology

---

## Schell's Lenses (s005)

### Knowing the Player (ch.8)
**Lens #16 — The Player**
- In everything I do, am I thinking about the player? Am I thinking about what they are doing, feeling, and wanting?
- What are the demographics of my audience? Do I understand them well?
- What is the psychographic profile of my target player? What do they like, dislike, fear, and desire?
- Would the player find this fun, interesting, or useful? How do I know?

**Lens #17 — Pleasure**
- What pleasures does my game create? (Use LeBlanc's 8 aesthetics as a checklist)
- Which pleasures am I trying to provide? Am I succeeding?
- Are there pleasures I could be delivering but am not?
- Are there any unintended pleasures that are overshadowing the intended ones?

### Flow & Engagement (ch.9)
**Lens #18 — Flow**
- Are the goals of the game clear? Do players always know what they're trying to do?
- Are there distractions that pull players out of flow?
- Does the game provide a steady stream of challenges that evolve with skill?
- Are challenges too easy (boredom) or too hard (anxiety)?
- Is there a sense of continuous and immediate feedback?
- If I draw an interest curve of the engagement, does it keep rising?

**Lens #19 — Needs**
- What needs does my game fulfill?
- Does my game promise to fulfill certain needs? Does it deliver on that promise?
- Are there ways my game could fulfill more of the player's needs?
- What needs does my game fail to fulfill that it could?

**Lens #20 — Judgment**
- Does my game judge the player in a way they feel is fair?
- Are the criteria for good and bad performance clear?
- Does the game communicate the player's performance clearly?
- Does the player have a way to feel pride in their performance?

### Projection & Identity (ch.17–18)
**Lens #64 — Projection**
- Does the player feel that the game world and characters invite imagination and projection?
- Is there enough detail to suggest the world without forcing too specific a vision?
- Does the player feel ownership of their avatar/character? Do they feel the character is "theirs"?
- Do players project themselves into the game? What breaks that projection?

**Lens #75 — The Avatar**
- Does the avatar let players express themselves?
- Does the avatar have the right combination of "me" and "not me"?
- Does the avatar's design invite projection rather than impose personality?

**Lens #85 — Expression**
- Does the game let players express who they are?
- Are there enough expressive options for players to feel their choices reflect themselves?

### Social Play (ch.19)
**Lens #84 — Friendship**
- Does my game bring people together as friends? Or does it put them against each other?
- What do players have to talk about after the game? Is that conversation positive?
- Does the game encourage behaviors that strengthen real-world relationships?

**Lens #86 — Community**
- Does the game support community formation? How?
- Are there reasons for players to keep coming back to the community even when not playing?
- Does the game give players shared experiences that create community identity?

**Lens #87 — Griefing**
- Are there ways players can use the game to harass or harm other players?
- How does the game handle players who abuse the system?
- Is it too easy to be an antisocial player?

**Lens #88 — Love**
- Does the player love this game? What would make them love it more?
- Do I love this game? If not, why not? What would it take?

## Tips from Other Sources

### Hodent: Cognitive constraints to check (s014)

**Perception:**
- Is critical gameplay information within the player's foveal field of view (center of screen)?
- Are Gestalt principles respected — do related elements group naturally?
- Are signifiers clear enough to be perceived in peripheral vision?
- Is Weber-Fechner accounted for? (Small incremental changes at high values are imperceptible)

**Memory:**
- How many new items is the player asked to remember at once? (Working memory holds ~3-4 items)
- Are key concepts reinforced through repetition before the player needs them?
- Are controls and actions designed to become procedural (automatic) with practice?
- Is tutorial information presented in the context where it will be used?

**Attention:**
- Is critical feedback salient enough to catch passive attention (motion, contrast, sound)?
- Are new instructions avoided during high-attention sequences (combat, chase, crisis)?
- Are dual-channel cues (visual + audio) used for critical events?

**Motivation:**
- Does the game satisfy Competence? (Player feels effective and growing)
- Does the game satisfy Autonomy? (Choices feel meaningful and consequential)
- Does the game satisfy Relatedness? (Connection to characters, other players, or community)

### Schell: Four mental abilities (s005, ch.9)
Ask whether the game engages all four:
1. **Modeling**: Does the game give the player a tractable model to reason about? Is the model learnable?
2. **Focus**: Does the game support sustained attention? Are goals clear? Is feedback immediate?
3. **Imagination**: Does the game leave enough unsaid to let the player's imagination work?
4. **Empathy**: Does the game create agents the player can care about and project emotions onto?

### Koster: Fun-as-learning diagnostic (s016)
- What patterns is the game teaching? Are they engaging enough to sustain hours of play?
- At what point does the player "grok" the game's patterns completely? Is that before or after the content ends?
- If the game is boring, which of these is happening?
  - Player mastered patterns too quickly (trivial)
  - Patterns are hidden behind noise (too hard)
  - New variations revealed too slowly or too quickly
  - Player lost control of the pattern

### Sellers: Interactive loop health check (s001, ch.7)
- Is there always at least one interesting decision for the player to make?
- Are there ever too many decisions at once (cognitive overload)?
- Does each decision produce visible, meaningful feedback?
- Does the player's mental model match the game's actual model? Test this by asking players to predict outcomes before they occur.

### Bartle: Player type coverage (s005, ch.8)
Check whether the game serves all four player types:
- **Achievers**: Are there clear goals, measurable progress, and meaningful rewards?
- **Explorers**: Is there hidden content, depth to discover, and systems to investigate?
- **Socializers**: Are there reasons to communicate and cooperate with others?
- **Killers**: Are there competitive systems with clear winners and meaningful stakes?

### Adams: Audience analysis questions (s006, ch.3)
- What is the age range of the target audience? How does that affect complexity tolerance?
- What is the experience level? How does that affect onboarding requirements?
- What is the gender distribution? (Avoid designing exclusively for one demographic)
- What are the play session length expectations? Does the game respect those?
- What platform will the audience use? Does the design suit the platform?
