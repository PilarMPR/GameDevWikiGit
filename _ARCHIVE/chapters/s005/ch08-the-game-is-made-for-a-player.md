# s005 · Ch.8 — The Game is Made for a Player

**Book:** [[../../sources/s005-art-of-game-design|The Art of Game Design (Schell)]] · **PDF pp. 97–112**
· **Lenses:** **#16 The Player, #17 Pleasure** · **Prev:** [[ch07-the-game-improves-through-iteration]]
· **Next:** [[ch09-the-experience-is-in-the-players-mind]]

## Chapter summary
To make a great experience you must **know your audience better than they know themselves**
(Einstein playing violin instead of lecturing). Covers **empathy/projection**, **demographics**
(age & gender), and **psychographics** — including two key taxonomies that connect directly to
other books in this wiki: **LeBlanc's 8 pleasures** and **Bartle's player types**.

## Subsections

### Einstein's Violin / Project Yourself
- Know what the audience will *actually* enjoy (often different from what they *say*). Achieve it
  through **empathy/projection** — Walt Disney crouching to a child's eye level at Disneyland.
- If designing for an audience you were part of, mine your **old memories**; if not, study them
  like a **cultural anthropologist**. "To truly communicate with someone, speak the language of
  their childhood." (Builds on **deep listening**, [[ch01-in-the-beginning-there-is-the-designer]].)

### Demographics (age & gender)
- The industry's **nine age groups**: 0–3 infant/toddler · 4–6 preschooler · 7–9 kids ("age of
  reason") · 10–13 tween ("age of obsession") · 13–18 teen (male/female interests diverge) ·
  18–24 young adult (time + money) · 25–35 (peak family formation; hardcore vs casual) · 35–50
  (family maturation; buys family games) · 50+ ("empty nesters"; social games). Younger groups
  divided by *mental development*, older by *family transitions*.
- **The Medium is the Misogynist?** — cites **Koster** ([[../../sources/s016-theory-of-fun|s016]]):
  the core of games is *mastering abstract formal systems*, generally enjoyed more by males —
  but that core can support story/creativity/learning/socialization ("games are like apples —
  enjoy the fruit even if you don't like the core").
  - **5 things males tend to like:** Mastery, Competition, Destruction, Spatial Puzzles, Trial & Error.
  - **5 things females tend to like:** Emotion, Real World, Nurturing, Dialog/Verbal Puzzles,
    Learning by Example. (Toontown: *you can only heal others, not yourself* — encourages nurturing.)
  - Generalizations for large audiences, not individuals. *Pox* designers chose boys-only after
    observing girls rarely run spontaneous large-group competitive games (conflict-resolution friction).

### 🔎 Lens #16 — The Lens of the Player
Stop thinking about the game; think about the player: What do they like/dislike and why? What do
they expect? What would I want in their place? What would they like/dislike about *my* game?
**Be the player's advocate** — hold this with **Lens #8 Holographic Design** at once; even better
than imagining the player is *watching them play* (cross-link [[../../concepts/player-experience-and-ux]]).
- *Pirates VR* demographic case: boys (offensive play), girls (defensive, social, love treasure →
  added treasure-snatching skeletons), men ("tall boys with credit cards"), women/mothers (steer
  from the back, manage the crew — gave mom a real role). Balanced only via prototyping/playtesting.

### Psychographics — what players find pleasurable
- **Demographics = external** (age, gender, income); **psychographics = internal** (what each
  person enjoys). Some are "lifestyle" (dog lover, hardcore FPS); deeper ones are *pleasure preferences*.

#### LeBlanc's Taxonomy of Game Pleasures (8)
**Sensation, Fantasy, Narrative, Challenge, Fellowship, Discovery, Expression, Submission**
(submission = the pleasure of entering the magic circle).
> ⭐ **Cross-book link:** this is the *same list* as **MDA's "8 kinds of fun"** — because Marc
> LeBlanc co-authored MDA. See [[../../concepts/mda-framework]] and [[../../sources/s011-mda-framework|s011]].
> (Sensory pleasure = "the pleasure of the toy," [[ch07-the-game-improves-through-iteration|Lens #15]].)

#### Bartle's Taxonomy of Player Types (4)
- ♦ **Achievers** (Challenge — act on the world) · ♠ **Explorers** (Discovery — interact with
  world) · ♥ **Socializers** (Fellowship — interact with players) · ♣ **Killers** (competition/
  destruction — act on players). Plotted on an **acting↔interacting × world↔players** grid.
> ⭐ **Cross-book link:** Bartle's player types are flagged as an open thread in the wiki
> [[../../syntheses/thesis|thesis]] and are cited by Sellers ([[../../sources/s001-advanced-game-design|s001]]).

#### More pleasures (beyond the taxonomies)
Anticipation, Schadenfreude (delight in misfortune), Gift Giving, Humor, Possibility, Pride/*naches*,
Purification, Surprise, Thrill ("fear minus death equals fun"), Triumph/*fiero*, Wonder. Taxonomies
are rules of thumb — stay alert for the **unclassified pleasure** that gives your game its unique quality.

### 🔎 Lens #17 — The Lens of Pleasure
What pleasures does my game give (can they be improved)? What pleasures are missing (can they be
added)? Always watch for unique, unlisted pleasures. *"Ultimately, the job of a game is to give pleasure."*
