# s005 · Ch.3 — The Experience Rises Out of a Game

**Book:** [[../../sources/s005-art-of-game-design|The Art of Game Design (Schell)]] · **PDF pp. 23–38**
· **Lenses:** **#2 Surprise, #3 Fun, #4 Curiosity, #5 Endogenous Value, #6 Problem Solving**
· **Prev:** [[ch02-the-designer-creates-an-experience]] · **Next:** [[ch04-the-game-consists-of-elements]]

## Chapter summary
The game is the **clay** the designer can actually shape. Schell builds working definitions of
**toy, fun, play, and game** — not as canon but as thinking tools — and lands on: **a game is a
problem-solving activity approached with a playful attitude.** Five lenses fall out along the way.

## Subsections

### A Rant About Definitions
- Definitions matter not for a "standard dictionary" (he rejects the academic "crisis" framing —
  cf. [[../../sources/s002-a-game-design-vocabulary|s002]]'s opposite view that vocabulary is the
  problem) but because **the act of defining forces clear thinking**. The real gap is *clear
  thinking about the ideas*, not a lack of words. Terms in the book are "clear expressions of
  ideas," not canon — replace them if you have better.

### So, What Is a Game? (building up)
A chain of provisional definitions, each tested by counterexample:
- **A toy is an object you play with**; **a good toy is an object that is fun to play with.**
- **Fun is pleasure with surprises.** → surprise and fun become Lenses #2 and #3.

### 🔎 Lens #2 — The Lens of Surprise
What will surprise players? Do story/rules/art/tech surprise? Do rules let players surprise
each other and themselves? (Surprise underlies humor, strategy, problem-solving; the brain is
hardwired for it — random sugar-water sprays felt more pleasurable than patterned ones.)

### 🔎 Lens #3 — The Lens of Fun
What parts of my game are fun, and why? What parts need to be *more* fun?

### What is play? (definitions surveyed)
- Schiller's "aimless expenditure of exuberant energy" (rejected — play has goals).
- Gilmore's pleasure/exhilaration/power/self-initiative (too broad).
- **Salen & Zimmerman: "play is free movement within a more rigid structure"** (cross-link
  [[../../sources/s013-rules-of-play|s013]], [[../../concepts/play-and-culture]]) — elegant but
  admits non-play counterexamples.
- **Santayana: "whatever is done spontaneously and for its own sake"** → work vs play =
  **servitude vs freedom**; *"whoever must play cannot play."* (Rico the assembly-line worker
  turns work into play by self-set goals; cites **Csikszentmihalyi** — link to flow.)
- **Schell's own: "play is manipulation that indulges curiosity."** Play = willful manipulation
  driven by questions ("What happens when I turn this knob?").

### 🔎 Lens #4 — The Lens of Curiosity
Think about the player's *true motivations*: What questions does my game put in the player's
mind? What makes them care? What makes them invent more questions?

### No, Seriously, What Is a Game? (qualities Q1–Q10)
Picks apart three scholarly definitions to extract 10 qualities:
- **Avedon & Sutton-Smith** → Q1 willful entry, Q2 goals, Q3 conflict, Q4 rules, Q5 win/lose.
- **Costikyan** ("interactive structure of **endogenous meaning**…") → Q6 interactive, Q7
  challenge, Q8 internal value. **Endogenous value** = value that exists *only inside the game*
  (Monopoly money). Example: *Sonic 2* rings (high endogenous value — protect you, grant lives)
  vs *Bubsy* yarn balls (none — ignored). → Lens #5.
- **Fullerton/Swain/Hoffman** ("a closed, formal system…") → Q9 engages players, Q10 closed
  formal system. "Closed" = boundaries = **Huizinga's magic circle** (link
  [[../../concepts/magic-circle]], [[../../sources/s004-homo-ludens|s004]]).

### 🔎 Lens #5 — The Lens of Endogenous Value
What is valuable to players in my game? How can I make it more valuable? How does in-game value
relate to player motivation? (Value/score reflects how much players care about succeeding.)

### Problem Solving 101
- Ten qualities is too many (Alan Kay: "if a subroutine takes >10 arguments, you missed a few").
  Reframe from the player's side: the one thing common to *all* games is **"I like solving
  problems."** Problem-solving is our species'核 advantage; enjoying it is an evolved survival trait.
- Even random games (*War*) are problem-solving for kids ("Can I control fate?") until they learn
  it's unsolvable — then it stops being a game.
- Mapping problem-solving onto Q1–Q10: stating a goal (Q2), framing boundaries/rules (Q4),
  building a simplified internal model (Q10, the **magic circle = our internal problem-solving
  system**), interacting (Q6), challenge (Q7)/conflict (Q3), engagement (Q9), endogenous value
  (Q8), win/lose (Q5). Only **Q1 (willful entry)** isn't covered → needs the playful attitude.

### 🔎 Lens #6 — The Lens of Problem Solving
What problems does my game ask players to solve? Hidden problems that arise in play? How does my
game generate *new* problems so players keep coming back?

### The Fruits of Our Labors (the definitions)
- **Fun is pleasure with surprises.**
- **Play is manipulation that satisfies curiosity.**
- **A toy is an object you play with** / **a good toy is fun to play with.**
- **A game is a problem-solving activity, approached with a playful attitude.**
- Caveat: these matter only insofar as they yield design insight — *the insights are the fruit,
  not the definitions.*
