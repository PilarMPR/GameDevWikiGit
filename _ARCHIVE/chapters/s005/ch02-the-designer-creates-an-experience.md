# s005 · Ch.2 — The Designer Creates an Experience

## Chapter summary
The designer's true goal is **not the game but the experience the game produces**. Games are
mere artifacts ("clumps of cardboard, bags of bits") — worthless unless played. Schell shows
how to study and capture experience, and introduces the book's signature tool: the **lens**.

## Subsections

### The Game Is Not the Experience
- *"The game enables the experience, but it is not the experience."* The tree-in-the-forest Zen
  question: designers care only about the **experience of hearing**, not the falling tree (s005, ch.2).
- We can't create experiences directly (the dream of "artificial reality"); we can only build
  **artifacts likely to create certain experiences** when interacted with — "like building a
  ship in a bottle." We never directly see our output, since it's an unsharable experience in
  another mind. **This is why deep listening ([[ch01-in-the-beginning-there-is-the-designer]]) is essential.**

### Is This Unique to Games?
- All entertainment designers face the artifact↔experience gap, but it's **far more pronounced
  for games** because games hand the player **control over pacing and sequence** (plus random
  events). That makes the resulting experience much harder to predict — but games offer feelings
  (choice, freedom, responsibility, accomplishment, friendship) **available no other way** (s005, ch.2).

### Three Practical Approaches to Chasing Rainbows
To understand human experience, borrow from three fields (s005, ch.2):
- **Psychology** — mechanisms of the mind. Notes the **behaviorism vs. phenomenology** schism;
  behaviorism "won" (better science) but avoids the subjective experience designers care about.
  Designers can freely use *both* behaviorist experiments and phenomenological introspection.
- **Anthropology** — holistic study of people; **cultural anthropology**'s fieldwork (live with
  your subjects, put yourself in their place) is the model for understanding players.
- **Design** — rules of thumb from every design discipline (music, architecture, film…); be
  **"xenophilic,"** pulling good ideas from anywhere and bridging them via psychology/anthropology.

### Introspection: Powers, Perils, and Practice
Introspection = examining your own experience. Two perils:
- **Peril #1 — false conclusions about reality.** (Socrates→reincarnation; Aristotle's wrong
  physics.) Designers get a "get out of jail free card": *game design is not science*; we care
  about **what feels true**, so introspection is trustworthy for judging experience quality.
- **Peril #2 — your experience may not generalize to others** (the real danger; subjectivity).
  Both extreme responses ("only design for people like me" / "trust only playtesting") have
  documented problems. The way out is, again, **listening** — observe your own experience, then
  others', and model the difference like a cultural anthropologist.

### Dissect Your Feelings
- It's not enough to know you like/dislike something — you must state **what** and **why**
  (the "bad pizza" anecdote). Put **concrete words to abstract feelings** so you can communicate
  the target experience to the team. Practice this on *every* experience, not just your own games.

### Defeating Heisenberg
- Observing an experience disturbs it ("paralysis by analysis"). Four techniques:
  **Analyze Memories**, **Two Passes** (experience once raw, once analytically), **Sneak Glances**
  (brief mental checks: "Exciting enough? Yes."), and **Observe Silently** (a still observer-self
  watching the engaged self — a Zen-like skill that takes practice).

### Essential Experience
- You don't replicate a real experience — you **capture its essence**. (Snowball-fight example:
  convey "cold" via animation, sound, or even a *rule* — gloves vs. better snowballs.)
- **Wii Sports baseball** case: designers focused entirely on the **swing** (the essential,
  unique part) and dropped innings/base-stealing. Knowing the essential experience tells you
  **which elements you can safely change and which you cannot**.

### All That's Real Is What You Feel
- The only reality we know is the reality of experience (a "filtered illusion" that *is* us).
  Great news for designers: **designed experiences can feel as real and meaningful as everyday
  ones** (developed in [[ch09-the-experience-is-in-the-players-mind]]).

## 🔎 Lens #1 — The Lens of Essential Experience
Stop thinking about the game; think about the player's experience. Ask:
- **What experience do I want the player to have?**
- **What is essential to that experience?**
- **How can my game capture that essence?**

If the desired and actual experiences diverge, change the game.
