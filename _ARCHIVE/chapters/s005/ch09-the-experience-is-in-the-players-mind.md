## Chapter summary
Experiences happen in the brain — mostly **subconscious** and hidden. Covers the **four mental
abilities** that make gameplay possible (modeling, focus, imagination, empathy), plus
**motivation** (Maslow) and **judgment**. Introduces **flow** — a cornerstone concept that links
across the whole wiki.

## Subsections

### (The hidden mind)
- Most mental work is subconscious (the "boast→toaster→toast" priming trick; mouth watering at
  "hamburger"). The subconscious exerts huge control over what we say and do.

### Modeling
- The mind never deals with reality, only **simplified models** of it (color is an artifact of
  the retina; Charlie Brown "works" because he matches our internal face/head model; magicians
  amaze by breaking models). **Games are pre-digested models** — relaxing because they strip away
  reality's complexity; this is why chess is taught at West Point (practice digesting models).
  > ⭐ **Cross-link:** the player's mental model = Norman's **conceptual model**
  > ([[../../concepts/usability-and-hcd]], s015) and the player's mental model in Sellers'
  > game+player system ([[../../concepts/fun-and-interactivity]], [[../../concepts/the-four-principal-loops]], s001).

### Focus → 🔎 Lens #18 — The Lens of Flow
- The brain focuses selectively (the "cocktail party effect"; dichotic-ear studies). Sustained
  focus + enjoyment = **flow** (Csikszentmihalyi). Components needed: **clear goals, no
  distractions, direct feedback, continuous (achievable) challenge.**
- **The flow channel** — stay in the narrow band between **boredom** (too easy) and **anxiety**
  (too hard). As skill rises, challenge must rise (the "Alex learning tennis" diagram). Flow
  activities *grow* the player. Best games climb the channel via a **"tense and release"** cycle
  (challenge → reward/power → easier spell → new challenge).
- Lens #18: clear goals? player's goals = mine? distractions? a steady stream of not-too-easy/
  not-too-hard challenges as skill improves? Flow is hard to test — needs *long* observation;
  watch for the moment a player leaves the channel.
  > ⭐ **Cross-link / open question resolved:** this is the wiki's main **flow** treatment, the
  > thread flagged open in [[../../syntheses/thesis]]. Connects to Koster's "learnable middle"
  > ([[../../concepts/theory-of-fun]], s016) and difficulty/[[../../concepts/game-balance]] (s001).

### Empathy
- We **project into others** (the actors' eye-contact exercise; dogs evolved expressive faces to
  capture our empathy). We empathize with *mental models*, so a drawing or game character works
  too. In games we project our whole **decision-making capacity** into a character (ch.18).

### Imagination
- The everyday imagination **fills gaps** ("the mailman stole my car" — you invent details).
  Games needn't show everything; the art is knowing **what to show and what to leave to the
  player's imagination**. Two functions: **communication (storytelling)** and **problem solving**.

### Motivation → 🔎 Lens #19 — The Lens of Needs
- **Maslow's hierarchy** (physiological → safety → belonging/love → self-esteem → self-
  actualization): higher needs aren't pursued until lower ones are met. Most games sit at
  **self-esteem** (achievement/mastery); **multiplayer** games reach lower, more basic needs
  (belonging) — explaining their pull. Content-creation + community games reach levels 3–5.
- Lens #19: which Maslow levels does my game operate on? Can it fulfill more basic needs? Can it
  fulfill them *better*? (Must *deliver* the need, not just promise it.)

### Judgment → 🔎 Lens #20 — The Lens of Judgment
- Deep human need: **to be judged** (fairly). Games are excellent **objective judgment systems** —
  a key part of their appeal. Lens #20: what does the game judge? how does it communicate it? is
  it fair? do players care? does it make them want to improve?
