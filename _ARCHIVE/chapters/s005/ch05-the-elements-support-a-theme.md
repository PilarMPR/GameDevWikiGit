# s005 · Ch.5 — The Elements Support a Theme

**Book:** [[../../sources/s005-art-of-game-design|The Art of Game Design (Schell)]] · **PDF pp. 47–56**
· **Lenses:** **#9 Unification, #10 Resonance** · **Prev:** [[ch04-the-game-consists-of-elements]]
· **Next:** [[ch06-the-game-begins-with-an-idea]]

## Chapter summary
The four elements ([[ch04-the-game-consists-of-elements|tetrad]]) should all serve a single
**theme** — what the game is *about*. A unifying theme focuses every decision; a **resonant**
theme (one that taps deep desires or truths) elevates craft into art and powerfully moves players.

## Subsections

### Mere Games
- Rebuts "games are meaningless diversions" — the meaningful parts of a beloved game (drama,
  camaraderie, rivalry) are *determined by the design*. The "too primitive" argument echoes
  early film and is fading as tech advances. Bold claim: **"all other media are subsets of
  games… at their technological limit, games will subsume all other media."**
- But artistic expression is **not** the goal — *powerful experiences* are; strong themes just
  make those experiences much stronger.

### Unifying Themes
- **Primary benefit:** all elements reinforce one another toward a common goal, and theme
  becomes a **decision filter** — *if it reinforces the theme it stays, if not it goes.*
- Two steps: **(1) figure out your theme; (2) use every means possible to reinforce it.**
- Most game themes are **experience-based** (deliver an essential experience). Example: Rich
  Gold's elephant book cut into the *shape* of an elephant — reinforce the theme everywhere.
- **Extended case — *Pirates of the Caribbean: Battle for Buccaneer Gold* (Disney VR):**
  "pirates" wasn't a theme until they distilled it to **"the fantasy of *being* a pirate."**
  Then everything reinforced it: custom 4-screen CAVE, stereoptics, 3D glasses with blinders
  removed (peripheral vision = sense of motion), pneumatic motion platform, **ship's-wheel +
  real metal cannons** interface, hyper-real visuals, the ride's music, 10-speaker audio (cannon
  blasts you *feel* in the stomach), freedom-of-sailing mechanics, no mid-game death (you only
  sink dramatically at the end — *theme over videogame tradition*), special treasure rendering,
  water-reflection lighting, fishing-net bag holders, AC vents blowing back as sea breeze, and
  the cast-member-invented "Eyes of Bluebeard" (a strong theme lets *everyone* contribute).
  Theming is often cheap (a line of text, a color, a sound).

### 🔎 Lens #9 — The Lens of Unification
- What is my theme? Am I using every means possible to reinforce it?
- Pairs with **Lens #7 (Elemental Tetrad)**: use the tetrad to separate elements, then check
  each against the unified theme.

### Resonance
- The best themes **resonate** — touch players deeply. Two kinds:
  - **Experience-based** (e.g. the pirate fantasy → resonates with the desire to be *free*).
  - **Truth-based** — a hidden statement we believe in our hearts. *Titanic* = "love is more
    important than life, and stronger than death" (told where everyone dies; effects make it
    *feel* real). *Hercules* = "if you are virtuous enough, you can defeat death." *Toontown
    Online* = **"work vs. play"** ("work wants to destroy play, but play must survive because
    play is more important" — i.e. slavery vs. freedom from ch.3).
- Truth-based themes are often subconscious; worth excavating so you can state them and use them
  as a decision filter and team-alignment tool. **Resonant themes elevate craft to art.**

### 🔎 Lens #10 — The Lens of Resonance
- What about my game feels powerful and special?
- What ideas get people excited when I describe it?
- With no constraints, what would this game be?
- What's driving my instincts about how it should be? (A delicate tool for listening to self/others.)

### Back to Reality
- Not every game needs a *resonant* theme (*Super Monkey Ball* has only a unifying one), but
  every game is strengthened by a unifying theme. Theme often works **subconsciously** — players
  feel it without naming it; it's about focusing the work on meaning, not hiding secret symbolism.
