# Glossary

Alphabetical index of technical terms used across this wiki. Format: **Term** — definition. (Source citation) → concept page.

---

**3Cs** — Character, Camera, Controls. The three axes of core game feel; the foundation any action game must get right before anything else matters. (s007 Swink, s017) → [[concepts/feel-and-controls/game-feel]]

**Absorption** — A state of deep engagement that does not require the strict challenge=skill balance of flow; proposed by Macklin & Sharp as an alternative to pure flow theory. (s009) → [[concepts/player/flow-channel]]

**Aesthetic (MDA)** — The desirable emotional response evoked in the player. One of 8 named aesthetics: Sensation, Fantasy, Narrative, Challenge, Fellowship, Discovery, Expression, Submission. (s011) → [[concepts/theory/mda-framework]]

**Affordance** — A relationship between an object and an agent that enables a possible action. Affordances are not properties of objects — they are relational and must be perceivable to be useful. (s015 Norman) → [[concepts/interface/usability-and-hcd]]

**Balancing loop** — A feedback loop that resists change and tends toward equilibrium. Catch-up mechanics are balancing loops. Contrast with reinforcing loop. (s001) → [[concepts/theory/systems-thinking]]

**Bartle types** — A taxonomy of player motivations: Achievers, Explorers, Socializers, Killers. Derived from MUD research. (s005 ch.8, Bartle 1996) → [[concepts/player/player-types]]

**Cerny Method** — A production approach attributed to Mark Cerny: invest in pre-production until 2 "publishable" levels are demonstrably fun, then enter full production. Prevents building expensively around an unproven core. (s005 ch.7) → [[concepts/production/iterative-design]]

**Cognitive load** — The total mental demand placed on working memory at any moment. High cognitive load impairs performance and enjoyment; design must manage it. (s014 Hodent) → [[concepts/player/player-psychology]]

**Constraints (Norman)** — Design properties that limit or prevent certain actions, guiding users toward correct behavior. Four types: physical, cultural, semantic, logical. (s015) → [[concepts/interface/usability-and-hcd]]

**Core loop** — The shortest repeating cycle of actions that defines a game's moment-to-moment play. (s001, s005) → [[concepts/mechanics/game-loops]]

**Core mechanic** — The essential repeated action that defines the game's nature; the atom from which gameplay is built. (s013 Salen & Zimmerman ch.23) → [[concepts/mechanics/core-mechanics]]

**Design pillars** — 3–5 defining principles that describe what a game is and is not; used to align team decisions and filter feature ideas. (s009 Macklin & Sharp) → [[concepts/production/documentation]]

**Design values** — Explicit statements of what a game is trying to achieve; the Macklin & Sharp framework for anchoring iterative design. (s009) → [[concepts/production/iterative-design]]

**Diegetic** — Existing within the story world of the game; visible/audible to the characters as well as the player. Contrast with non-diegetic. (s012 Kremers, s010 Fernández-Vara) → [[concepts/interface/ui-design]]

**Discernability** — Property of an action in meaningful play: the player can perceive what happened as a result of their action. Failure of discernability means the gulf of evaluation is not closed. (s013) → [[concepts/theory/meaningful-play]]

**Dominant strategy** — A strategy that is better than all others in all circumstances; its existence makes a game effectively a solved puzzle. (s005 ch.12) → [[concepts/mechanics/puzzles]], [[concepts/mechanics/game-balance]]

**Dynamics (MDA)** — The run-time behavior of mechanics acting on player inputs and each other over time. The layer between rules and experience; where emergence lives. (s011) → [[concepts/theory/mda-framework]]

**Elemental Tetrad** — Schell's four game elements: Technology, Mechanics, Story, Aesthetics. Good design requires all four to reinforce each other. (s005 ch.4) → [[concepts/theory/formal-elements]]

**Emergence** — Behavior or properties arising from a system that were not explicitly designed into any individual part. The source of depth and the source of exploits. (s001, s013) → [[concepts/mechanics/emergence]]

**Extrinsic motivation** — Motivation arising from external rewards (points, money, social recognition) rather than the activity itself. Can undermine intrinsic motivation when added to intrinsically rewarding activities. (s014 Hodent) → [[concepts/player/motivation]]

**Flow channel** — The narrow band of challenge where skill and difficulty match, producing focused, enjoyable engagement. First described by Csikszentmihalyi; cited extensively in s005, s016. → [[concepts/player/flow-channel]]

**Formal elements** — The structural components shared by all games: players, objectives, procedures, rules, resources, conflict, boundaries, outcome. (s008 Fullerton, s009 Macklin & Sharp) → [[concepts/theory/formal-elements]]

**Game feel** — The tactile sensation of controlling a game character; produced by real-time control + simulated space + polish. (s007 Swink) → [[concepts/feel-and-controls/game-feel]]

**Grokking** — Fully internalizing a pattern to the point of complete, intuitive comprehension; related to Heinlein's coinage. When a game's patterns are grokked, boredom follows. (s016 Koster) → [[concepts/theory/theory-of-fun]]

**Gulf of evaluation** — The difficulty of determining whether a goal has been achieved; closed by feedback. (s015 Norman) → [[concepts/interface/usability-and-hcd]]

**Gulf of execution** — The difficulty of determining what action to take to achieve a goal; closed by affordances and signifiers. (s015 Norman) → [[concepts/interface/usability-and-hcd]]

**Identity extension** — The state in which a player stops feeling like they are controlling a character and starts feeling like they *are* the character; the upper target of game feel design. (s007 Swink) → [[concepts/feel-and-controls/input-and-controls]]

**Integration** — Property of an action in meaningful play: the result matters to the larger game over time. Discernability + integration together = meaningful play. (s013) → [[concepts/theory/meaningful-play]]

**Interest curve** — A plot of audience engagement over time; the fundamental pacing analysis tool. A good curve has a hook, rising action, peaks and valleys, a climax, and a satisfying resolution. (s005 ch.14 Schell) → [[concepts/level-design/pacing-and-flow]]

**Intrinsic motivation** — Motivation arising from the activity itself; the activity is inherently rewarding. More durable than extrinsic motivation; can be undermined by external rewards. (s014 Hodent, s016 Koster) → [[concepts/player/motivation]]

**Juiciness** — The property of a game where every interaction feels alive and satisfying, even incidentally; refers to visual and audio polish on interactions. (Purho/Jonasson GDC 2012; s007) → [[concepts/feel-and-controls/feedback-systems]]

**Lenses (Schell)** — A design methodology of 100+ perspective-shifting prompts, each framed as a set of questions to ask about a game design. (s005) → [[design-reference/_index]]

**Loss aversion** — The cognitive tendency to feel losses approximately twice as strongly as equivalent gains; a powerful and ethically fraught design tool. (Kahneman & Tversky; s014 Hodent) → [[concepts/player/motivation]]

**Ludonarrative dissonance** — Conflict between what a game's narrative says and what its mechanics allow or reward. Coined by Clint Hocking regarding BioShock. → [[concepts/narrative/story-and-games]]

**Lusory attitude** — The psychological stance of voluntarily accepting inefficient means (game rules) as a condition of play; the attitude that makes play possible. (Suits, cited in s013) → [[concepts/theory/magic-circle]]

**Magic circle** — The conceptual boundary separating play from ordinary life, within which a game's rules apply and special meanings accrue. (s004 Huizinga; s013 Salen & Zimmerman) → [[concepts/theory/magic-circle]]

**MDA** — Mechanics–Dynamics–Aesthetics. A three-layer framework for analyzing and designing games; designer reads M→D→A, player experiences A→D→M. (s011 Hunicke, LeBlanc, Zubek) → [[concepts/theory/mda-framework]]

**Meaningful play** — Play where the relationship between player action and system outcome is both discernible and integrated. The central design goal of Salen & Zimmerman. (s013) → [[concepts/theory/meaningful-play]]

**Mental model** — The player's internal representation of how the game system works; good design aligns the player's mental model with the actual system. (s015 Norman, s014 Hodent) → [[concepts/player/player-psychology]]

**Meta-progression** — Persistent progress that carries over across multiple play sessions; the outer loop that motivates return visits. (s017; s001) → [[concepts/mechanics/progression-systems]]

**Non-diegetic** — Existing outside the story world; visible to the player but not the characters. HUDs, health bars, and pause menus are non-diegetic. Contrast with diegetic. → [[concepts/interface/ui-design]]

**Operative action** — An action the player can directly perform through the interface. Contrast with resultant action. (s005 ch.10 Schell) → [[concepts/mechanics/core-mechanics]]

**Parts / Loops / Wholes** — Sellers' decomposition of systems: individual components (parts), the feedback relationships between them (loops), and the emergent property of the system as a whole (wholes). (s001) → [[concepts/theory/systems-thinking]]

**Playcentric design** — Design methodology centered on the player's experience; prototyping and playtesting with real players as the primary feedback mechanism. (s008 Fullerton) → [[concepts/production/iterative-design]]

**Procedural rhetoric** — The implicit argument a game makes about how the world works through its mechanics and rules. (Bogost; cited in s010 Fernández-Vara) → [[concepts/theory/meaningful-play]]

**Reinforcing loop** — A feedback loop that amplifies change; tends away from equilibrium. The "rich get richer" dynamic in Monopoly is a reinforcing loop. Contrast with balancing loop. (s001, s011) → [[concepts/theory/systems-thinking]]

**Resultant action** — An action that emerges from the combination of operative actions; emergent gameplay the designer may not have explicitly intended. (s005 ch.10 Schell) → [[concepts/mechanics/core-mechanics]]

**Rubber banding** — A catch-up mechanic that disadvantages the current leader to keep competition close; the explicit version of a balancing loop applied to player scores/positions.

**SDT (Self-Determination Theory)** — Ryan & Deci's model identifying three innate needs whose fulfillment enhances intrinsic motivation: Competence, Autonomy, Relatedness. (s014 Hodent) → [[concepts/player/motivation]]

**Signifier** — A perceivable signal that communicates to the user where and how to act; distinct from affordance (which is about possibility, not communication). (s015 Norman) → [[concepts/interface/usability-and-hcd]]

**Systems thinking** — A mode of understanding that sees the world in terms of interacting systems — parts, loops, wholes — rather than isolated components. The foundational lens of s001. (s001 Sellers) → [[concepts/theory/systems-thinking]]

**Tell** — Feedback that signals an upcoming event before it occurs, giving the player time to respond. Tells are the mechanism that makes reactive gameplay feel fair. (s007 Swink) → [[concepts/feel-and-controls/feedback-systems]]

**Transitive relationship** — A is better than B is better than C; always. Creates dominant strategies. Contrast with intransitive (rock-paper-scissors) relationships. (s005 ch.11) → [[concepts/mechanics/game-balance]]

**Undermining effect** — Adding expected, tangible extrinsic rewards to an intrinsically motivating activity can decrease intrinsic motivation. Also called the overjustification effect. (s014 Hodent) → [[concepts/player/motivation]]

**Variable ratio reinforcement** — A reward schedule that rewards after an unpredictable number of actions; produces the highest, most persistent engagement of all reward schedules. The psychological basis of loot boxes and gacha. (Skinner; s014 Hodent) → [[concepts/business/monetization]]

**Verb** — Anthropy & Clark's term for the operative actions available to the player; the game's expressive vocabulary. A game where the only verb is "shoot" tells a story about shooting. (s002) → [[concepts/mechanics/core-mechanics]]

**Weber-Fechner law** — Perceived changes in intensity are logarithmic, not linear; a +10 upgrade feels large at level 1 and negligible at level 50. Requires exponential scaling to maintain perceived impact. (s014 Hodent) → [[concepts/player/player-psychology]], [[concepts/mechanics/progression-systems]]

**Working memory** — The cognitive system that actively holds and manipulates a small number of items (~3–4) in the short term. The primary bottleneck for onboarding and complexity management in games. (s014 Hodent) → [[concepts/player/player-psychology]]
