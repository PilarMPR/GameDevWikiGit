# Formal Elements of Games

The structural "anatomy" every game shares — multiple sources converge here with slightly
different lists. This is one of the strongest cross-source agreements in the wiki.

## The lists, side by side
- **Fullerton** ([[../sources/s008-game-design-workshop|s008]], ch.3): **players, objectives,
  procedures, rules, resources, conflict, boundaries, outcome** — plus *dramatic elements*
  (challenge, play, premise, character, story) (s008).
- **Macklin & Sharp** ([[../sources/s009-games-design-and-play|s009]], ch.1): six basic elements —
  **actions, goals, rules, objects, playspace, players** (s009).
- **Schell** ([[../sources/s005-art-of-game-design|s005]]): the **elemental tetrad** —
  **Mechanics, Story, Aesthetics, Technology** — which must reinforce one another (s005).
- **Adams** ([[../sources/s006-fundamentals-of-game-design|s006]], ch.13–14): gameplay =
  **challenges + actions**, implemented by **core mechanics** (s006).
- **Anthropy & Clark** ([[../sources/s002-a-game-design-vocabulary|s002]]): the atoms of meaning
  are **verbs** (what the player does) and **objects** (s002).
- **Salen & Zimmerman** ([[../sources/s013-rules-of-play|s013]]): the **core mechanic** as the
  essential repeated action (s013, ch.23); **Fernández-Vara** (s010) uses formal elements as an
  analysis category.

## Synthesis
Across all of them: a game is **players acting (verbs/procedures) on objects/resources, under
rules, toward goals/outcomes, within boundaries**. These map onto **MDA's Mechanics**
([[mda-framework]]) and onto **parts** in the systems view ([[parts-loops-wholes]]). The
*dramatic/aesthetic* wrappers (story, theme, character) sit alongside the formal skeleton.
