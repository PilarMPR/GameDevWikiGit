# Theory of Fun (fun as learning)

From [[../sources/s016-theory-of-fun|Raph Koster, A Theory of Fun]] (s016), with cross-source
debate about what "fun" actually is.

## Koster's thesis
**Fun is the act of mastering a pattern — fun is just another word for learning** (s016). The
brain craves patterns; a game is "noise" until you find its pattern, pleasurable while you're
learning it, and **boring once mastered** ("used up"). Too easy → boring; too hard →
frustrating; fun lives in the learnable middle (anticipates **flow**).

## The wider debate about "fun" in this wiki
- **MDA** ([[mda-framework]], s011) rejects "fun" as too vague and replaces it with **8 aesthetics**
  (sensation, fantasy, narrative, challenge, fellowship, discovery, expression, submission).
- **Koster** (s016) gives "fun" a specific meaning: *learning*.
- **Sellers** ([[../sources/s001-advanced-game-design]], s001) argues a game **need not be "fun"**
  at all, as long as it stays **interactive and engaging** ([[fun-and-interactivity]]).
  > ⚠️ **Live contradiction.** Koster ≈ "engagement is ongoing learning, so sustained
  > engagement basically *is* fun"; Sellers separates engagement from fun; MDA dissolves "fun"
  > into many aesthetics. These are reconcilable as different *definitions* rather than factual
  > conflicts — all agree the real target is **sustained engagement**, they just name it differently.
- **Anthropy & Clark** (s002) and **Koster** both stress games as an **expressive medium**, where
  meaning matters beyond challenge.
