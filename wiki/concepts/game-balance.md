# Game Balance

From [[../sources/s001-advanced-game-design|Advanced Game Design]] (s001, ch.9–10).

## What balance is
- How well a game plays, arising from the **relationships between parts** — ultimately their
  attributes, values, and behaviors (s001, ch.9; see [[parts-loops-wholes]]).
- A property of the whole **game + player system**: involves player psychology, the game's
  systems, and math tools to evaluate them (s001, ch.9).
- A balanced game avoids a single dominant strategy and insurmountable advantages; it offers
  an **explorable possibility space** (s001, ch.9).
- Balancing is **never perfect and never done** — an ongoing exercise; every player can
  unbalance a game in new ways, so build **resilient systems** (s001, ch.10).

## Four families of balancing methods (s001, ch.9)
- **Designer-based** (intuition/judgment)
- **Player-based** (from how players actually play)
- **Analytical** (data/analytics on play)
- **Mathematical** (probability and modeling)

## Transitive vs intransitive systems (s001, ch.9)
- **Transitive**: ordered power relationships (A > B > C) — balanced largely via cost/benefit
  curves.
- **Intransitive**: cyclic relationships (rock-paper-scissors) — no single dominant option.

## Progression & power curves (s001, ch.10)
- Core principle: **every benefit has a cost, and the two are inextricably linked**. As costs
  rise, so do benefits. The *delay* in being able to pay higher costs is what paces the game
  while letting the player feel they're improving (s001, ch.10).
- Curves regulate linked cost/benefit progression for players and objects (weapons, XP levels).
