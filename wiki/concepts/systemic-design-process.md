# The Systemic Design Process

How to actually work as a systemic game designer — Parts II & III of
[[../sources/s001-advanced-game-design|Advanced Game Design]] (s001, ch.5–12).

## Decompose into parts, loops, and wholes
- Getting started is daunting; the method is to break the game into **parts, loops, and
  wholes** — not necessarily in that order — and define it at each level (s001, ch.5).
  See [[parts-loops-wholes]] and [[the-four-principal-loops]].

## Vision & the concept document (ch.6)
- Every game has one **big driving idea / vision**; clarify it early and state it succinctly.
  A clear, compelling, **shared vision** is the single practice most correlated with
  successful games (s001, ch.6, ch.11).
- The vision needn't be grand — smaller, more focused is often better (e.g. *Angry Birds*;
  *For Honor* = "combat as dance") (s001, ch.6).
- Capture it in a brief **concept document** with **high concept / product / detailed design**
  sections; it's the touchstone for development (s001, ch.6).

## Defining loops then parts (ch.7–8)
- Specify the game's functional **loops** ([[the-four-principal-loops]]), then drill into
  **parts** — attributes, values, behaviors — documented in text and spreadsheets (s001, ch.7–8).

## Balance (ch.9–10)
- See [[game-balance]].

## Working as a team (ch.11)
- Games are nearly always made by **teams**, which are themselves systems; success depends on
  shared vision and understanding functional/studio/corporate roles. Cites real data on
  effective teams (s001, ch.11).

## Making the game real (ch.12)
- **Pitching** (communicate + transmit excitement), **iterative prototyping & playtesting**,
  **stage-gating**, and the dev **phases**: concept → preproduction → production → alpha → beta.
- The hardest and most essential part is **finishing**: most ideas never get prototyped, and
  most prototypes never get finished (s001, ch.12).
