# Design Reference: Narrative & World

---

## Schell's Lenses (s005)

### Story Structure (ch.15)
**Lens #65 — The Story Machine**
- Does my game generate interesting stories by itself?
- Does the story machine work in concert with the game's mechanics?
- Are the stories the game generates interesting for spectators as well as players?
- Do players feel invested in the stories the game generates?

**Lens #66 — The Obstacle**
- Does the protagonist have a clear goal? What is in the way?
- Is the obstacle the right level of difficulty?
- Is the obstacle interesting in its own right — does it have depth and motivation?
- Does the obstacle reveal something about the protagonist?

**Lens #67 — Simplicity and Transcendence**
- Is the world of the story simpler than real life in ways that are interesting?
- Is the world of the story more wonderful (transcendent) than the real world?
- Does the combination of simplicity and transcendence create a good fantasy?

**Lens #68 — The Hero's Journey**
- Does the protagonist face a call to adventure? Do they initially refuse?
- Is there a threshold crossing (the point of no return)?
- Are there trials, helpers, and enemies along the way?
- Is there an ordeal (the nadir, all seems lost)?
- Does the protagonist return transformed?

**Lens #69 — The Weirdest Thing**
- What is the weirdest thing in the game's story or world?
- Is there only one weirdest thing? (One strange premise; the rest is normal)
- Does the weirdest thing make the world more interesting, or just confusing?

**Lens #70 — Story**
- Does the game tell a good story through play, not just through cutscenes?
- Are there multiple stories being told simultaneously? (Macro, session, moment-to-moment)
- Does the story make sense? Is it consistent within the world's rules?

### Indirect Control (ch.16)
**Lens #71 — Freedom**
- Do players have real freedom of choice, or only the illusion of it?
- Does the game give players the *feeling* of freedom even when choices are limited?
- Where are players constrained? Do the constraints feel natural or arbitrary?
- Where do players feel overwhelmed by too much freedom?

**Lens #72 — Indirect Control**
- What methods of indirect control am I using? (Constraints, goals, interface, visual design, characters, music)
- Are my indirect controls working? Do players go where I want?
- Are my indirect controls too heavy-handed? Do players feel manipulated?
- Are there opportunities to add more subtle indirect control?

**Lens #73 — Collusion**
- Am I asking NPCs and the game environment to secretly cooperate to create good experiences?
- Do multiple elements reinforce each other to guide the player naturally?
- Does the collusion feel like design, or does it feel like railroading?

### World Design (ch.17)
**Lens #74 — The World**
- Is the game world interesting enough to explore? Does it have depth?
- Is the world consistent? Do things in the world make sense within the world's own rules?
- Does the world have a tone and feel that the mechanics and story reinforce?
- Would players want to spend time in this world independent of the game's goals?

### Character Design (ch.18)
**Lens #75 — The Avatar**
- Does the avatar invite identification? Is there an appropriate mix of "me" and "not-me"?
- Does the avatar's design invite projection?

**Lens #76 — Character Function**
- What function does each character serve in the story? (Hero, mentor, trickster, shadow, etc.)
- Is every character serving their function?
- Do any characters serve redundant functions?

**Lens #77 — Character Traits**
- What traits define each character? Are they consistent?
- Are the character's traits reflected in their actions, not just described in dialogue?
- Are the character's traits interesting and memorable?

**Lens #78 — The Interpersonal Circumplex**
- Do my characters occupy different parts of the "dominant/submissive, friendly/hostile" spectrum?
- Do the characters create interesting dynamics when they interact?

**Lens #79 — The Character Web**
- What are all the relationships between characters? Are they interesting?
- Do character relationships change over the course of the story?
- Do relationships feel real and dynamic, or static?

**Lens #80 — Status**
- What is each character's relative status? How is status established and maintained?
- Are status transactions (gaining/losing status) dramatically interesting?
- Do players feel their character's status changes during the game?

**Lens #81 — Character Transformation**
- Does the protagonist change over the course of the game?
- Is the transformation believable and well-motivated?
- Does the player feel they caused the transformation, or just watched it happen?

**Lens #82 — Inner Contradiction**
- Does the protagonist have an inner contradiction or flaw?
- Is resolving (or failing to resolve) this contradiction part of the story?
- Does the inner contradiction make the character more interesting?

**Lens #83 — The Nameless Quality**
- Does the world of my game have a sense of life and wonder?
- Does the game have a quality beyond the describable — something that draws players in?

## Tips from Other Sources

### Schell: Story/game integration (s005, ch.15)
- "Story and gameplay are like oil and vinegar — they don't naturally mix, but shaken together can be good on a salad." Use the friction, don't fight it.
- Traditional storytelling mechanics (tension, pacing, escalation, climax) still apply to interactive stories.
- Ask: does the story enhance the gameplay? Does the gameplay enhance the story? If either answer is no, the integration is failing.
- The test: would this game be better with less story? Would it be better with more? Calibrate accordingly.

### Schell: Indirect control methods (s005, ch.16)
For each method, audit your current implementation:
1. **Constraints**: do they feel like natural features of the world, not arbitrary blocks?
2. **Goals**: are they placed to pull the player in the right direction?
3. **Interface**: does it reveal what the designer wants revealed, no more?
4. **Visual design**: does color, lighting, and composition guide the eye?
5. **Characters**: do NPCs direct attention through actions, dialogue, and gaze?
6. **Music**: does the audio cue danger, safety, direction, and emotional tone?

### Kremers: Environmental storytelling checklist (s012, ch.12)
For each space in the game, ask:
- What happened here before the player arrived? Is that history legible in the environment?
- Who lives or works here? Does the space reveal their character?
- What is valued here? Is that reflected in what is prominent?
- Is something happening right now? (Active elements make the world feel alive)
- What is the space NOT showing? (Restraint in environmental storytelling is as important as expression)

### Kremers: Narrative and gameplay integration (s012, ch.12)
- Don't interrupt play for story delivery. Can you communicate this narrative beat through the environment, through NPC behavior, or through gameplay consequences instead of a cutscene?
- Every story beat delivered through play teaches the player something. What does this beat teach?
- Does the player's agency feel real during story moments? Or is agency suspended while the story plays out?

### Sellers: Mental model alignment for narrative (s001, ch.4)
- Is the player's mental model of the game world consistent with the actual world? (If not, story decisions will feel arbitrary)
- Does narrative information arrive when the player is ready to receive it — not during high-attention sequences?
- Does each new narrative element connect to something the player already knows?

### Adams: World-building consistency test (s006, ch.12)
- List your world's fundamental rules. Does every element obey them?
- Test the "rule violation" question: if this element violated the world's rules, would players notice? If yes, the rule is canonical and must be honored.
- Are there narrative non-sequiturs — story elements that don't fit the world?
