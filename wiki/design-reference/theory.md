# Design Reference: Theory & Foundations

## Schell's Lenses (s005)

### The Core Experience (ch.2)
**Lens #1 — Essential Experience**
- What experience do I want the player to have?
- What is essential to that experience?
- How can I capture the essence of that experience in the game?
- When players experience my game, what do they actually feel versus what I intended?

**Lens #2 — Surprise**
- What will surprise players when they play this game?
- Does the game have mechanisms that generate novel surprises?
- Does the story have surprising twists?
- Are the surprises surprising for the right reasons?

**Lens #3 — Fun**
- What parts of the game are fun? Why?
- What parts are not fun? Why not?
- When players aren't having fun, what is causing that?

**Lens #4 — Curiosity**
- What questions does the game make the player ask?
- Does the game reward curiosity?
- Are there things the player wants to know but doesn't? Does the game control that information well?

### Structure (ch.4)
**Lens #7 — The Elemental Tetrad**
- Is my design using all four elements: Mechanics, Story, Aesthetics, Technology?
- Are the four elements in balance? Do they reinforce each other?
- Is any element being neglected? Is any element dominating in a way that hurts the others?

**Lens #8 — Holographic Design**
- Can I describe every single element of my game and explain how it supports the core experience?
- Is there any element that doesn't serve the core experience? Can it be removed?
- Can every team member describe the game's core experience? Do they agree?

### Theme (ch.5)
**Lens #9 — Unification**
- Does everything in my game support a single theme?
- Is the theme stated clearly in the design?
- Do the mechanics, story, and aesthetics all reinforce the same theme?
- When an element doesn't fit the theme, is there a good reason for it?

**Lens #10 — Resonance**
- What is the deepest meaning behind this game?
- Does the game resonate with players at a level deeper than the mechanics?
- Is there something about this game that players will want to talk about, think about, or remember?

### Imagination & Beauty (ch.13)
**Lens #44 — Character**
- Does my game have a distinctive character — a quality that makes it unique and memorable?
- Is there anything strange in the game that players talk about excitedly?
- Does the game have funny or eccentric qualities that make it unique?
- Are there any deliberate "flaws" that players love?

**Lens #45 — Imagination**
- What must the player understand to play my game?
- Can imagination help them understand that?
- What high-quality, realistic details can I provide?
- What details would be low quality if provided? Can imagination fill them in instead?

## Tips from Other Sources

### Hunicke, LeBlanc & Zubek: MDA analysis (s011)
**Mechanics → Dynamics → Aesthetics audit:**
For each game element, trace the chain:
1. What mechanic is this? (Data representation, algorithm, rule)
2. What dynamic does it produce? (Run-time behavior, feedback loop, player pattern)
3. What aesthetic does it create? (Emotional response: sensation, fantasy, narrative, challenge, fellowship, discovery, expression, submission)

**The designer's direction test:**
- Are you designing Mechanics with an eye toward the Dynamics they produce?
- Are you defining the target Aesthetics first, then working backward to Mechanics?
- Experience-first or feature-first? (MDA advocates experience-first — define the aesthetic target, derive the dynamics, then design the mechanics)

### Sellers: Systems thinking audit (s001, ch.2)
**Before building:**
- What are the parts of this system? List them explicitly.
- What are the relationships between parts? Which interactions are designed?
- Which loops exist? Are they reinforcing or balancing?
- What should the whole produce? What emergent behavior is intended?

**After building:**
- What emergent behaviors are appearing that weren't intended?
- Are any feedback loops running away (reinforcing too strongly)?
- Is the game+player system balanced — does the challenge scale with the player's growing skill?

### Salen & Zimmerman: Meaningful play audit (s013)
For each action in the game:
- Is the outcome **discernable** — can the player perceive what their action caused?
- Is the outcome **integrated** — does it matter to the larger game over time?
If either answer is no, the play is not meaningful at that point.

**Three-schema check:**
- Formal: are the rules complete, consistent, and clear?
- Experiential: does the play feel meaningful to the player?
- Cultural: does the game's content relate appropriately to the real world?

### Koster: Fun-as-learning evaluation (s016)
- What patterns does this game teach?
- Are the patterns engaging to discover?
- Is there enough pattern depth to sustain play through the intended game length?
- Is the game accessible enough that the player can find the first patterns quickly?
- Is the mastery curve — from novice to expert — long enough to be satisfying?

### Norman: HCD evaluation (s015)
- Have you observed actual users interacting with the prototype? (Not just asked their opinions)
- Does the system close the gulf of execution? (Players know what to do)
- Does the system close the gulf of evaluation? (Players know what happened)
- Does the system support an accurate conceptual model?
- What assumptions have been made about the user that haven't been tested?

### Fullerton: Formal elements completeness check (s008, ch.3)
Verify each formal element is defined:
- **Players**: roles, number, interaction patterns
- **Objectives**: are they clear, achievable, and motivating?
- **Procedures**: are all legal moves defined?
- **Rules**: are they complete? Are there loopholes or ambiguities?
- **Resources**: are all resources meaningful and appropriately scarce?
- **Conflict**: is the conflict interesting and proportional?
- **Boundaries**: does the player understand what's in and out of the game?
- **Outcome**: is the outcome quantifiable, meaningful, and fair?
