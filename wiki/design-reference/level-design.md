# Design Reference: Level Design

---

## Schell's Lenses (s005)

### Pacing & Interest (ch.14)
**Lens #61 — The Interest Curve**
- If I draw an interest curve of my game/level, how is it generally shaped?
- Does the experience have a hook early on?
- Is there a clear, exciting climax?
- Does interest generally rise over the course of the experience?
- Are the valleys (rest moments) brief enough, and are the peaks high enough?
- Is there a fractal structure — does each section have its own internal interest curve?
- What changes would give me a better interest curve?
- Do my intuitions about the curve match what playtesters actually experience?

**Lens #62 — Inherent Interest**
- What is inherently interesting about the level/space?
- Does curiosity drive players forward? What makes them want to see what's around the next corner?
- Is there something unique or surprising in this space that players will want to experience?

**Lens #63 — Beauty**
- Is the game world beautiful? Is that beauty appropriate to the experience?
- Does the beauty of the game world serve the gameplay, or does it distract from it?
- Are there moments of unexpected beauty that reward exploration?

### Space & Freedom (ch.16, ch.19)
**Lens #71 — Freedom**
- When do players have freedom of action in this level? Do they feel free at these times?
- When are they constrained? Do the constraints feel natural or arbitrary?
- Are there places where more freedom could be given without disrupting design intent?
- Are there places where players are overwhelmed by too much freedom?

**Lens #72 — Indirect Control**
(See also narrative.md — applies directly to level design)
- Does the level's visual design (lighting, composition, color) guide the player naturally?
- Are goals placed to pull the player in the right direction?
- Do characters, audio, and environmental cues support the intended path?
- Does the player feel guided without feeling railroaded?

## Tips from Other Sources

### Kremers: Level design goal hierarchy (s012, ch.3)
For every level, verify three levels:
1. **Macro goal**: can the player always articulate the level's ultimate objective?
2. **Intermediate goals**: are there 2–5 sub-objectives that structure progress toward the macro goal?
3. **Micro goals**: is there always a clear, immediate task (this minute or this moment)?
If the player ever says "what am I supposed to do?" the micro goal has failed.

### Kremers: Teaching mechanics through levels (s012, ch.2)
- Does the level introduce each mechanic at low stakes before requiring it under pressure?
- Is there a "show, then ask" structure? (Player observes the mechanic in action before using it)
- Are mechanics scaffolded — introduced simply, then combined, then challenged?
- Is reinforcement spaced — does the mechanic reappear after an interval to move it from short-term to long-term memory?

### Kremers: Level design methodology checklist (s012, ch.4)
- Has the floorplan (2D layout) been validated in gameplay before art investment?
- Has the level been played with no hints to test clarity?
- Are encounters spaced with adequate recovery time?
- Are resources replenished after significant resource-draining challenges?
- Are there conceptual non-sequiturs (elements that don't make sense in the world)?
- Is every short-term goal clearly communicated to the player at all times?
- Are the risks, rewards, and consequences of decisions clear?

### Hourences: Spatial design checklist (s003)
- **Spatial flow**: can the player navigate without confusion? Are dead ends meaningful?
- **Encounter rhythm**: are enemies/challenges spaced with appropriate recovery time?
- **Visual clarity**: can the player read the space — where to go, what is dangerous, what is interactable?
- **Landmark density**: are there enough distinctive reference points for orientation?
- **Item placement**: are rewards placed to encourage desired behavior?
- **Sightlines**: does the player's eye pull them forward? Is there always something to see ahead?

### Hourences: Audiovisual composition (s003)
- Is there a clear focal point in each space?
- Does foreground/background separation help or hinder readability?
- Does lighting guide movement? (Bright areas = safety/goals; dark areas = danger/mystery)
- Do moving elements (fans, creatures, water) attract attention to important locations?
- Does the space have unity — does it read as a single coherent environment?

### Schell: Architecture as experience design (s005, ch.19)
- What experience do you want the player to have in this space? Does the architecture support it?
- What is the organizing principle for this space? (Linear / grid / web / points-in-space / divided)
- Is the scale of the space appropriate to the emotional tone? (Large = awe/exposure; small = tension/intimacy)
- Does the space change character as the player progresses through it?

### Adams: Five core spatial organizing principles (s006, ch.12)
For each area, identify its organizing principle:
- **Linear**: one path — appropriate for narrative-driven, paced sequences
- **Grid**: structured — appropriate for strategic clarity
- **Web**: networked — appropriate for exploration and player choice
- **Points-in-space**: open field — appropriate for open-world and discovery
- **Divided spaces**: compartmentalized — appropriate for controlled density and contrast

Use the right type for the intended experience. Mixed-type areas require clear transitions.

### Adams: Level design principles (s006, ch.16)
- Make early levels tutorial levels. Never assume players know how to play.
- Vary the pacing. Alternating tension and release is not optional — it is the structure.
- After resource-draining challenges, replenish resources.
- Avoid conceptual non-sequiturs (elements that make no sense in the game world).
- The player should never wonder "what do I do next?" — the next short-term goal must always be obvious.
- Be clear about risks, rewards, and consequences of decisions *before* the player commits.
