## What the book is about
A unified approach to game design built on **[[../concepts/systems-thinking|systems thinking]]**:
games are systems, and game design is system design. The book argues that by understanding
in detail how systems operate — parts forming loops that create persistent, emergent wholes
— a designer can deliberately build games that are "systemic, creative, and engaging"
(s001, Intro). It combines **foundational theory, design principles, and practical process**
into one method (s001, Intro).

## Structure (three parts, 12 chapters)
- **Part I — Foundations** (ch.1–4): what systems are, a formal definition of systems
  (parts/loops/wholes), games as systems, and a systemic account of interactivity & fun.
- **Part II — Principles** (ch.5–8): how to actually work as a systemic designer —
  the whole experience (vision + concept doc), creating game loops, and defining game parts.
- **Part III — Practice** (ch.9–12): game balance (methods + practice), working as a team,
  and making the game real (pitching, prototyping, dev phases, finishing).

## Chapter-level takeaways
1. **Foundations of Systems** (ch.1) — Designers need metacognition and a "ways of seeing"
   shift toward seeing the world as systems: networks of interrelated parts forming
   metastable wholes (s001, ch.1).
2. **Defining Systems** (ch.2) — Formal definition: *"a set of parts that together form
   loops of interaction between them to create a persistent whole; the whole has its own
   properties and behaviors belonging to the group but not to any single part"* (s001, ch.2).
   See [[../concepts/parts-loops-wholes]]. Introduces reinforcing vs balancing loops.
3. **Foundations of Games & Game Design** (ch.3) — Games sit in a nonconsequential
   **magic circle** with their own tokens & rules; play is voluntary and participatory.
   Games analyzed in systemic terms: **structures** (tokens/rules = nouns & verbs),
   **functional elements** (loops = "phrases" creating the game's world model), and
   **architectural/thematic** construction (the whole experience). See [[../concepts/game-as-system]].
4. **Interactivity & Fun** (ch.4) — Play exists only in the combined **game + player system**.
   Player builds a **mental model** alongside the game's model via **interactive loops**;
   engagement (and "fun") emerges from this. Provocative claim: games need not be "fun" so
   long as they stay highly interactive and engaging (s001, ch.4). See [[../concepts/fun-and-interactivity]].
5. **Working as a Systemic Game Designer** (ch.5) — How to start: decompose the game into
   **parts, loops, and wholes** (not necessarily in that order). See [[../concepts/systemic-design-process]].
6. **Designing the Whole Experience** (ch.6) — Every game has one big driving **vision**;
   capture it in a brief **concept document** (high concept / product / detailed design)
   that acts as the touchstone for development. A clear shared vision is the single practice
   most correlated with successful games (s001, ch.6).
7. **Creating Game Loops** (ch.7) — Loops are "the beating, cycling heart" of a game.
   The **four principal loops**: the game's model, the player's mental model, the interactive
   loop (incl. the **core loop**), and the designer's loop around all of these.
   See [[../concepts/the-four-principal-loops]].
8. **Defining Game Parts** (ch.8) — The most detailed, grounded level: specify parts,
   attributes, values, and behaviors (incl. "atomic"/indivisible parts), documented in text
   and in spreadsheets so the design is implementable.
9. **Game Balance Methods** (ch.9) — Four families of balancing methods: **designer-based,
   player-based, analytical, and mathematical**; plus **transitive vs intransitive** systems.
   See [[../concepts/game-balance]].
10. **Game Balance Practice** (ch.10) — Progression & power curves; every benefit has a
    cost, and the two are inextricably linked; balancing is never "done."
11. **Working as a Team** (ch.11) — Games are made by teams; teams are systems too.
    Cites real data on what makes effective teams; understand functional/studio/corporate roles.
12. **Making Your Game Real** (ch.12) — Pitching, iterative prototyping & playtesting,
    stage-gating, and the dev **phases** (concept → preproduction → production → alpha → beta);
    the hardest, most essential part is actually *finishing*.
