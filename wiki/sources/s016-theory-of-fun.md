## What it's about
A short, illustrated argument for a single thesis: **fun is the act of mastering a pattern —
fun is just another word for learning.** Games are engines that deliver this learning in a
compressed, safe, pleasurable form (s016).

## Core ideas
- **The brain craves patterns.** Games present patterns to grok; "fun" is the feedback of the
  brain successfully learning/chunking a pattern. When the pattern is mastered, the game is
  "used up" and becomes **boring** (s016).
- A game is **noise** until you find the pattern; too easy = boring, too hard = frustrating —
  fun lives in the learnable middle (anticipates flow). See [[../concepts/theory-of-fun]].
- **Games teach** — they are fundamentally educational; the content is often less important
  than the underlying formal system being learned (s016).
- Distinguishes **fun** from other sources of enjoyment (aesthetic appreciation, social
  status, storytelling) — these can accompany a game but are not the "fun" of the game itself (s016).
- Argues games are a legitimate **expressive medium** that can carry meaning, like other arts (s016).

## Relationship to other sources
- Complements **MDA's** move away from the vague word "fun" toward specific aesthetics
  ([[s011-mda-framework]]) — here "fun" is specifically *learning*.
- Tensions with [[s001-advanced-game-design]]'s claim that a game *need not be fun* if
  interactive — Koster would say sustained engagement basically *is* ongoing learning.
