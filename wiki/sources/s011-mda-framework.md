## What it's about
The single most-cited framework in game design. **MDA** decomposes a game into three linked
layers and insists designer and player approach them from **opposite directions** (s011).

## The framework
- **Mechanics** — the components of the game at the level of data representation and algorithms
  (the rules/systems).
- **Dynamics** — the run-time behavior of mechanics acting on player inputs and each other's
  outputs over time.
- **Aesthetics** — the desirable **emotional responses** evoked in the player.

> **Mechanics → Dynamics → Aesthetics** (designer's view); **Aesthetics → Dynamics → Mechanics**
> (player's view). Each layer is a **lens** on the game, separate but causally linked (s011).
> Small changes in one layer cascade into the others — so design **experience-first, not
> feature-first** (s011).

## "Aesthetics" — 8 kinds of fun (s011)
A directed vocabulary to replace the vague word "fun":
1. **Sensation** (sense-pleasure) · 2. **Fantasy** (make-believe) · 3. **Narrative** (drama) ·
4. **Challenge** (obstacle course) · 5. **Fellowship** (social framework) · 6. **Discovery**
(uncharted territory) · 7. **Expression** (self-discovery) · 8. **Submission** (pastime).

Example decompositions (s011): *Quake* = Challenge, Sensation, Competition, Fantasy;
*The Sims* = Discovery, Fantasy, Expression, Narrative.
Games are **artifacts, not media**: their content is their *behavior*, not the media streaming
out of them (s011).
