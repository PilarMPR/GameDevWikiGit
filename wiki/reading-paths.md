# Reading Paths

Five curated paths through the wiki for different goals. Not a linear textbook — the wiki is non-linear — but suggested sequences for specific situations.

**Choose your path:** → [New to theory?](#path-1) → [Hot Potato design sprint?](#path-2) → [Educational games pitch?](#path-3) → [Playtesting session tomorrow?](#path-4) → [Just read a book?](#path-5)

## Path 1: Complete Foundation
*You're new to game design theory and want the full shape of the field before diving into specifics.*

1. **[[wiki/syntheses/thesis]]** — the one-page map of what the 17 sources agree on and where they disagree; read this to orient everything that follows
2. **[[wiki/concepts/theory/magic-circle]]** — what play is, why it's different from "ordinary life," and why that matters for design
3. **[[wiki/concepts/theory/formal-elements]]** — what games are made of; the vocabulary all other frameworks build on
4. **[[wiki/concepts/theory/mda-framework]]** — the dominant analytical tool; designer vs. player perspective; the 8 aesthetics
5. **[[wiki/concepts/mechanics/core-mechanics]]** — the building blocks of the interactive layer
6. **[[wiki/concepts/player/flow-channel]]** — the experiential target; what good engagement feels like and why it breaks
7. **[[wiki/concepts/production/iterative-design]]** — the method; why you cannot think your way to a good game
8. **[[wiki/design-reference/_index]]** → pick the category most relevant to your current work; use as a design checklist

**External complement:** Raph Koster's "A Theory of Fun" (s016) or Jesse Schell's "The Art of Game Design" (s005) for the practitioner-friendly framing of the above.

## Path 2: Hot Potato Design Sprint
*You're entering a design or production sprint on Hot Potato (party brawler, UE5).*

1. **[[wiki/genres/party-brawler]]** — genre constraints, the skill-gap problem, the Hot Potato mechanic's specific design questions, and the checklist
2. **[[wiki/analyses/smash-bros]]** — the primary brawler reference; MDA + formal elements + design highlights with Hot Potato delta questions
3. **[[wiki/analyses/jackbox]]** — the primary party loop reference; what minimal mechanics + social dynamics looks like
4. **[[wiki/concepts/feel-and-controls/game-feel]]** — 3Cs foundation; what "feels good" means mechanically for the potato passing and brawler component
5. **[[wiki/concepts/feel-and-controls/feedback-systems]]** — the potato's timer feedback is the game feel; tells; calibration
6. **[[wiki/concepts/player/player-types]]** — managing Achievers + Socializers + Killers in the same session; designing for the party audience
7. **[[wiki/concepts/mechanics/game-loops]]** — the round loop → session loop → meta loop architecture
8. **[[wiki/concepts/level-design/pacing-and-flow]]** — drawing the interest curve for an individual round; the 90-second structure
9. **[[wiki/design-reference/mechanics]]** + **[[wiki/design-reference/feel-and-controls]]** — full lens checklist for the current design pass

**Design reference session:** Run through Lenses #27 (Skill), #31 (Challenge), #33 (Triangularity), #40 (Reward), #57 (Feedback), #61 (Interest Curve) from **[[wiki/design-reference/schell-lenses-all]]** against the current build.

**External complement:** Masahiro Sakurai's YouTube design videos — watch the episodes on game feel, responsiveness, and party game design.

## Path 3: Educational Games Pitch Prep
*You're preparing a pitch or proposal for the B2B educational games studio.*

1. **[[wiki/concepts/theory/theory-of-fun]]** — Koster's fun=learning thesis is your intellectual foundation; "games teach" is not a metaphor, it is the mechanism
2. **[[wiki/concepts/player/motivation]]** — intrinsic vs. extrinsic; why external rewards can undermine learning; SDT as your design framework for educational engagement
3. **[[wiki/concepts/player/flow-channel]]** — the flow channel ≡ Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development; this is your bridge from learning theory to game design
4. **[[wiki/genres/educational-games]]** — B2B market, procurement, design patterns, MDA for educational contexts (build this page first if it doesn't exist)
5. **[[wiki/concepts/production/documentation]]** — how to structure a pitch deck; GDD format for stakeholder communication
6. **[[wiki/concepts/player/player-types]]** — understanding the school as a client: teachers (decision-makers), students (users), administrators (budget approvers) all have different motivations
7. **[[wiki/concepts/theory/mda-framework]]** — use MDA as the pitch structure: "the mechanics are X, which create dynamics of Y, which produce the aesthetic/learning experience of Z"
8. **[[wiki/resources/external]]** → educational games section

**The pitch argument (from the wiki):** Koster's theory proves that fun is learning — the separation between "educational game" and "good game" is artificial. A well-designed educational game uses the same flow channel that all good games use; it simply names the skill being developed as "multiplication" rather than "headshots."

**External complement:** Look up Games for Change annual festival talks; search for "serious games GDC" on YouTube.

## Path 4: Playtesting Prep
*You have a playtesting session tomorrow and want to prepare.*

1. **[[wiki/concepts/production/playtesting]]** — the full protocol: session types, data types, facilitator role, session structure
2. **[[wiki/design-reference/production]]** — Schell's Lens #91 (Playtesting) and Lens #13 (Eight Filters) as the design questions to bring to the session
3. **[[wiki/concepts/player/player-psychology]]** — perception, attention, and memory constraints; what to observe when watching players
4. **[[wiki/concepts/player/flow-channel]]** — watch for boredom exits (phone checking, disengagement) and anxiety exits (repeated failure, frustration tells)
5. **[[wiki/concepts/interface/feedback-systems]]** — observe which feedback the player notices vs. misses; this reveals which feedback channels are working
6. **[[wiki/concepts/interface/usability-and-hcd]]** — gulf of execution (do they know what to do?) vs. gulf of evaluation (do they know what happened?) as diagnostic frames

**Preparation checklist:**
- [ ] Define 3–5 specific questions this session will answer (not "is it fun?")
- [ ] Prepare observation template with those questions
- [ ] Brief facilitator: observe, do not explain or help
- [ ] Record session if possible (even phone audio)
- [ ] Plan debrief questions for after play
- [ ] Have a note-taking method that captures behavior separately from interpretation
