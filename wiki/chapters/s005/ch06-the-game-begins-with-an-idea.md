# s005 · Ch.6 — The Game Begins with an Idea

**Book:** [[../../sources/s005-art-of-game-design|The Art of Game Design (Schell)]] · **PDF pp. 57–74**
· **Lenses:** **#11 Infinite Inspiration, #12 Problem Statement** · **Prev:** [[ch05-the-elements-support-a-theme]]
· **Next:** [[ch07-the-game-improves-through-iteration]]

## Chapter summary
The "amateurish" loop — *think of an idea, try it, keep changing it until good enough* — **is
exactly what real designers do**; ch.6–7 just teach how to do it *well*. This chapter covers
where ideas come from (inspiration + the subconscious) and **how to brainstorm**.

## Subsections

### Inspiration → 🔎 Lens #11 — The Lens of Infinite Inspiration
- The juggler parable: don't copy other jugglers (or other *games*) — **look everywhere else**
  for inspiration (ballet, a flock of geese, a paper-punch machine). Others can copy your moves
  but not your inspiration.
- Lens questions: *What experience from my life would I want to share? In what small way can I
  capture its essence and put it in my game?* Works hand-in-hand with **Lens #1 Essential
  Experience** ([[ch02-the-designer-creates-an-experience]]).

### State the Problem → 🔎 Lens #12 — The Lens of the Problem Statement
- **Design = problem solving.** Before generating ideas, write a **problem statement** that
  names both your **goal** and your **constraints** (e.g. "How can I make a *web-based* game
  *teenagers will love*?"). Watch for **over-constraining** (maybe it needn't be a "game" at all).
- Three advantages: **broader creative space**, **clear measurement** (does the idea solve the
  problem?), **better team communication**.
- A complete design covers all four tetrad elements; the problem statement often pre-decides one
  or more quadrants — check the tetrad to see where you have freedom. If you have *no*
  constraints, *invent* some (pick a story/mechanic) — that gives you a problem statement.
- Lens questions: What problem am I really solving? Bad assumptions? Is a game even the best
  solution? How will I know it's solved?

### How to Sleep / Your Silent Partner (the subconscious)
- Salvador Dali's "slumber with a key" — a fraction-of-a-second nap to tap the subconscious.
- Good ideas "pop up from below consciousness." Kekulé dreamed the benzene **ring**. Treat your
  **creative subconscious as a separate person** (Stephen King's basement "muse"). Its profile:
  **can't talk** (communicates in imagery/emotion), **impulsive, emotional, playful, irrational**
  (Schell's "patron saint": Harpo Marx).
- **Five subconscious tips:** ① **Pay attention** (listen to thoughts/feelings/dreams — the
  banana-surfboard→surfing-monkeys example); ② **Record your ideas** (frees the mind, "feels
  clean"; carry a voice recorder); ③ **Manage its appetites judiciously** (Maslow — unmet
  needs/obsessions block creativity; some appetites curb, don't feed); ④ **Sleep** (sleep is for
  the *mind*: sorting/filing/reorganizing); ⑤ **Don't push too hard** (the forgotten-name effect —
  give it the problem and let it work in the background).

### Fifteen Nitty-Gritty Brainstorming Tips
1. Write down the answers · 2. Write or type? (whatever frees you) · 3. **Sketch** (drawing
   triggers ideas) · 4. **Toys** (visual + tactile engagement) · 5. **Change your perspective**
   (literally — stand on the chair) · 6. **Immerse yourself** (in audience, tech, theme) ·
   7. **Crack jokes** ("he who derails, rerails") · 8. **Spare no expense** (don't let materials
   limit ideas) · 9. **The writing on the wall** (big Post-Its/butcher paper, repositionable) ·
   10. **The space remembers** (we recall *positions* well — Tom Kelley) · 11. **Write everything**
   (Linus Pauling: "the best way to have good ideas is to have a lot of ideas"; no self-censoring) ·
   12. **Number your lists** (numbers lend dignity + ease discussion) · 13. **Mix and match
   categories** (tetrad lists → combine tech×mechanics×story×aesthetics into novel games) ·
   14. **Talk to yourself** (saying it aloud makes it real) · 15. **Find a partner** (small
   groups ≤4; avoid hole-pokers; works best on *narrow* problems).

### Look At All These Ideas! Now What?
- The goal was just "think of an idea" — now you have a hundred. Next step (ch.7): **narrow and
  iterate.**
