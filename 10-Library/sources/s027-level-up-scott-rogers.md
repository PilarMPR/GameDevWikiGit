# s027 · Level Up! (Rogers)

**Full title:** Level Up! The Guide to Great Video Game Design, 2nd Edition
**Author:** Scott Rogers | **Year:** 2014 | **Publisher:** Wiley
**Type:** Book | **Pages:** ~500

## Summary

A comprehensive, practitioner-focused guide to video game design structured as 18 "levels" (chapters), written by a veteran designer whose credits include Maximo and God of War: Chains of Olympus. Rogers explicitly rejects theory in favour of practical, nuts-and-bolts advice, covering the full arc from idea generation and GDD writing through character design, the Three Cs (Character, Camera, Controls), HUD design, level design, combat systems, enemy AI, power-ups, multiplayer, and monetization. The book's distinctive voice — illustrated with the author's own cartoon characters throughout — makes it accessible to beginners while still useful to working professionals. Second edition (2014) adds content on mobile, touch input, AR cameras, and digital monetization models.

## Key claims & concepts

- **The Three Cs (Character, Camera, Controls)** form the experiential backbone of any action game; all three must be solved together, not independently. (s027, level 5-7)
- **GDD as a 6-step progression**: One-Sheet → Ten-Pager → Gameplay Progression document → Beat Chart → full GDD → team collaboration etiquette. The Ten-Pager is the most universally useful. (s027, level 4)
- **"Why I Hate Fun"**: Rogers argues "fun" is a useless target word for designers; designers should aim for specific experiences or emotions instead. (s027, level 2)
- **Camera design as a discipline**: every camera type (first-person, third-person, top-down, isometric, side-scrolling, AR) has distinct design rules; always point the camera at the objective; never let the character leave the camera's view. (s027, level 6)
- **The Rule of Threes** structures game content: three acts, three abilities, three enemy types — triadic rhythm provides pacing and completeness. (s027, level 4)
- **Level design as emotional guidance**: levels teach through Disneyland-style environmental cues (foreshadowing, goal-setting, beat charts); graybox/whitebox before art. (s027, level 9)
- **Combat system anatomy**: melee (combos, timing windows), ranged (gun taxonomy), defensive mechanics (dodge, guard, parry), boss encounters — each with specific design heuristics. (s027, level 10-11)
- **Enemy design**: behavior types, movement archetypes, "bestiary of choices," the importance of telegraphing before attack. (s027, level 11)
- **Power-ups and rewards**: "Love Thy Player" principle — rewards should feel meaningful and well-timed; reward variety (score, achievements, collectibles, money) serves different player types. (s027, level 13)
- **Multiplayer level design** requires different spatial logic than single-player; "Dirty Half Dozen" heuristics for multiplayer maps. (s027, level 14)
