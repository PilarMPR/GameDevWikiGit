# s034 — Introduction to Game Systems Design

**Author:** Dax Gazaway · **Publisher:** Pearson, 2022 · **Pages:** 385

## Summary

A spreadsheet-first textbook on the craft of game systems design. Where most design books treat balance abstractly, Gazaway operationalizes it with Excel/spreadsheet workflows throughout: every concept — attributes, scaling curves, probability distributions, macrosystems — is built and tested in a spreadsheet before touching a game engine. The book positions the systems designer as the role that translates creative vision into quantifiable, playable data.

## Key claims

- A **game** is defined by five properties: agreed-upon artificial rules; players have an impact on outcome; players can opt out; sessions are finite; intrinsic rewards (s034, ch.1).
- **Player attributes** (audience profiling) include: age, gender, rule-learning tolerance, challenge interest, time investment, pace preference, competitiveness, platform preference, skill level, genre preference (s034, ch.1).
- The core **game systems design role** sits between game designer (vision) and implementer (code/art); systems designers own the numbers (s034, ch.2).
- **Attributes** quantify game entities: every stat, value, and property is an attribute that needs a range, type, and scale (s034, ch.9).
- **Range balancing and data fulcrums** — the fulcrum is the value at which two competing attributes are exactly balanced; hierarchical design structures stat relationships top-down (s034, ch.13).
- **Exponential growth and diminishing returns:** progression systems almost always use exponential XP curves or power curves; diminishing returns prevent dominant strategies (s034, ch.14).
- **Macrosystems** = the systems that operate above the core gameplay loop (meta-progression, economy, social features); player engagement lives at this layer (s034, ch.16).
- **Probability as a design tool:** randomness, expected value, standard deviation, and distribution shapes are crafted intentionally — random ≠ arbitrary (s034, ch.19).
- **Systems communication and psychology:** players read systems through feedback; cognitive biases (anchoring, loss aversion, sunk cost) shape how players interpret numerical feedback (s034, ch.18).
