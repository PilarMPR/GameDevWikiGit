## Summary

Every element of a game pitch must answer exactly two questions: "Is this game worth making?" and "Can this team make it?" Everything that doesn't serve one of those questions actively hurts the pitch. Upton catalogs 30 specific failure modes, all of which trace back to presenters forgetting what publishers are actually evaluating.

## Key claims

- All pitch failures trace to the same root: forgetting that publishers are evaluating a business investment and a team bet — not aesthetics in isolation. (s021)
- Design pillars (internal compass for design decisions) and hooks (external pitch to players/publishers) are different things with different audiences — confusing them is a category error. (s021)
- "Is this game worth making? Can this team make it? Everything that doesn't respond to these doesn't need to be in your pitch." (s021)
- Unrealistic financial projections (citing Minecraft or Among Us as baseline) destroy credibility more than bad art. (s021)
- Backstory and lore are internal development tools; they are not pitch content. (s021)
- Pitching before you know your resource requirements is a disqualifying mistake. (s021)

## Detailed notes

### The two foundation questions

Every decision in a pitch deck should be evaluated against:
1. **Is this game worth making?** — Is there a market? Is the concept original? Is it fun? Does it fill a gap?
2. **Can this team make it?** — Does the team have the skills? The track record? The scale awareness?

A publisher is making a multi-year financial and relational commitment. The pitch exists to answer these two questions, not to share the designer's creative excitement.

### The 30 failure modes (complete list)

1. **Backstory obsession** — extensive lore before the core concept is explained. Backstory is worldbuilding for the team; it's not what publishers need to evaluate the business.
2. **Inventory system minutiae** — implementation details presented before the publisher knows whether they want the game at all
3. **Incomplete design** — gaps in the design that the presenter expects the publisher to fill; signals unreadiness
4. **Pillars ≠ hooks** — design pillars define the game's essence for the team; hooks attract players. A pitch deck is hooks, not pillars.
5. **Missing gameplay description** — narrative and theme are not gameplay; describe what the player *does*
6. **Realism as design excuse** — "it's realistic" does not justify unfun design decisions
7. **Unnecessary framing** — story setup before the concept that wastes room time
8. **Lack of originality** — an existing IP with a cosmetic change
9. **Ignored technical risks** — pretending hard engineering problems don't exist destroys credibility
10. **Weak proof of concept** — a prototype that doesn't validate the core loop
11. **Poor art quality** — programmer art signals low production value and under-resourcing
12. **Unclear placeholders** — temp art not explicitly labeled as temp
13. **Premature polish** — cinematics and UI before the game loop works
14. **Weak sample dialogue** — reveals actual writing quality
15. **Chasing tech trends** — VR/blockchain/AI because it's hot, not because it serves the game
16. **Wrong platform pitch** — mobile sensibilities in a console pitch, or vice versa
17. **Me-too concepts** — direct clones that will be superseded by the time the game ships
18. **IP negotiation requests** — asking the publisher to solve your licensing problem
19. **Monetization over mechanics** — revenue model before explaining what the game is
20. **Vague resource estimates** — "we'll figure out the budget"
21. **No assembled team** — asking publishers to solve a staffing problem
22. **Unrealistic projections** — citing Minecraft or Among Us as your baseline
23. **Difficult personality** — publishers are betting on a multi-year relationship
24. **Anonymous background** — no intro, no team credentials
25. **Defensive about questions** — treating engagement as an attack reveals insecurity
26. **Technical presentation failures** — wrong connector, no adapter, dead laptop
27. **Missing audio equipment** — pitching an audio-heavy game with no headphones available
28. **Impaired judgment** — arrive sober
29. **Industry criticism** — disparaging competitors
30. **Poor personal presentation**

### Key distinction: design pillars vs. pitch hooks

**Design pillars** are the internal compass. They guide design decisions: "Does this feature serve the pillars? No → cut it." They define what the game *is* for the people making it. They are not inherently communicable to outsiders who don't share the design context.

**Hooks** are the external pitch. They answer: why will a player (or publisher) care? They are audience-facing, emotionally immediate, and require no design knowledge to understand.

A pitch deck filled with design pillars fails because the audience has no shared context. The pillars are preconditions for understanding why the game is coherent — they are not why someone will fund it or play it.

### Resource awareness as credibility signal

Item 20 (vague resource estimates) and item 21 (no assembled team) are disqualifying not because resources are the most interesting part of a pitch, but because they signal whether the team has thought seriously about execution. A team that "hasn't figured out the budget yet" hasn't figured out whether their game is actually makeable.

Upton: *"Understand how many people and how much money and time you need to finish the game before doing the pitch."*

### Connection to production planning

The talk is primarily about pitching, but items 3, 9, 10, and 20–21 map directly onto production planning failures. The pitch forces the team to confront questions they may have been avoiding: What does a prototype need to prove? What are the technical risks? What does the team actually need?

In this sense the pitch is a forcing function for production clarity — a well-structured pitch requires that the team have answered the fundamental design and production questions.
