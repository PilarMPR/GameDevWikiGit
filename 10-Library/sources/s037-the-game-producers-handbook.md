# s037 — The Game Producer's Handbook

**Author:** Dan Irish (formerly EP at Relic Entertainment; produced Homeworld 2, Myst franchise)
**Publisher:** Thomson Course Technology PTR, 2005 · **Pages:** 353
**Foreword:** Dave Perry (Shiny Entertainment)

## Summary

The most comprehensive treatment of the game producer role in the wiki. Irish enumerates ~30 distinct responsibilities of a video game producer, then builds chapter-by-chapter coverage of job descriptions across roles, scheduling formulas, risk management templates, financial management, QA pipeline, and marketing support. The book positions production as a profession — not just a title — that bridges creative teams, publishers, hardware partners, and business stakeholders. Published in 2005, some specifics are dated (console submission process, disc manufacturing) but the core frameworks remain valid.

## Key claims

### The producer's role
- A game producer carries approximately **30 distinct responsibilities** (s037, ch.1), including:
  - Actively contribute; apply decision-making; attend budget meetings; be forward-thinking; build consensus; deliver animation schedules; develop pre-production and production plans; generate game-design documentation; handle hardware manufacturers, legal/contractual issues, licensing, middleware, platform transition, PR; handle QA; help sales; hire/interview; interact with upper management; know games; manage assets, big teams, foreign localization, resources, art process, audio process, vendor relationships, and time; pitch; possess industry experience; provide clarity, focus, and marketing support; schedule; sow discipline; take ownership; teach others; understand cinematic production.
- The role is fundamentally cross-functional: the producer is the person who "understands everything enough to connect everyone" (s037, ch.1).

### Pre-production and production plans
- **Pre-production plan** establishes: game concept, target platform, team structure, rough schedule, technology selection, prototype goals (s037, ch.1).
- **Production plan** covers: detailed task breakdown, team assignments, milestones with acceptance criteria, risk register, budget tracking (s037, ch.1).

### Scheduling
- Irish provides concrete scheduling formulas: estimate task duration, multiply by a risk factor (1.5x for first-time tasks), buffer sprints before milestones.
- **Milestones** are contractual checkpoints with the publisher; milestone acceptance criteria must be agreed in writing before the project starts.
- Common scheduling failure modes: no buffer, no risk accounting, scope creep between milestones.

### Risk management
- Formal **risk register** template: risk description, likelihood (1–5), impact (1–5), risk score (likelihood × impact), mitigation action, contingency plan, owner.
- High-risk areas in game development: technology unproven, key person dependency, scope without research, external dependencies (middleware, licensed IP).

### Job descriptions and team structure
- Irish provides full job description templates for: producer, associate producer, executive producer, lead designer, game designer, lead programmer, programmer, lead artist, artist, QA lead, QA tester, audio director, composer.
- Team size correlates to budget tier; he gives rough headcount ranges by project budget.

### QA
- QA is not a "last mile" activity — embedding QA from day one catches design problems before they become code problems.
- **Bug severity levels:** S1 (crash/data loss), S2 (major feature broken), S3 (minor feature broken), S4 (cosmetic/polish).

### Financial management
- Royalty structures, advance recoupment, milestone payment schedules.
- Producer owns the budget: tracks actuals vs. plan; alerts publisher early on overruns.

### Marketing support
- Producer bridges dev team and marketing: asset delivery schedules for trailers, press demos, screenshots, review builds.
