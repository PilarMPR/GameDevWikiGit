# s036 — Agile Game Development (Keith, 2e)

**Author:** Clinton Keith · **Publisher:** Pearson, 2021 · **Pages:** 572
**Subtitle:** Build, Play, Repeat · Part of Mike Cohn's Agile Signature Series

## Summary

The definitive book on applying Scrum and Agile methodologies to game development. Keith draws on fifteen years of implementing Scrum at game studios to adapt the framework for the unique challenges of creative, highly iterative game work: scope uncertainty, creative discovery, art and audio pipelines, player-facing QA, and live service post-launch. The second edition updates coverage for distributed teams, scaled Agile, and live game operations. The central thesis is that Agile is not merely a project management method — it is a philosophy of **learning through rapid feedback**, which aligns naturally with good game design practice.

## Key claims

- Game development's core crisis: scope and design uncertainty are **fundamental**, not failures of planning. Traditional milestone/waterfall schedules systematically fail because they assume unknowns can be known upfront (s036, ch.1).
- **Scrum for games** uses the standard framework (sprint, product backlog, sprint backlog, daily standup, sprint review, retrospective) but requires game-specific adaptations at every layer (s036, ch.3).
- **Sprint length:** 2 weeks is standard for most teams; short enough to get frequent feedback on playable builds (s036, ch.4).
- **Great teams** are cross-functional, self-organizing, and psychologically safe; the Scrum Master removes impediments; the Product Owner owns prioritization (s036, ch.5).
- **Kanban** is preferable for teams doing bug-fix/live service work where work is flow-based rather than sprint-based (s036, ch.6).
- **Product Backlog:** all work is expressed as **user stories** in the backlog, estimated in story points; backlog grooming is continuous (s036, ch.7).
- **User stories for games:** "As a [player type], I want [game action] so that [experience goal]." Acceptance criteria define done-ness in playable terms (s036, ch.8).
- **Minimum Viable Game (MVG):** the smallest playable version that tests the core experience hypothesis — analogue of MVP but emphasizes *fun* as the first hypothesis to validate (s036, ch.9).
- **Agile Design:** game designers work in sprints like everyone else; design is documented "just enough" — heavy GDD upfront is waste. Design discoveries in play must be able to change the backlog immediately (s036, ch.14).
- **Agile Art and Audio:** art and audio integrate via definition of done per sprint; style guides replace full upfront spec; audio designers need seats in sprint reviews (s036, ch.13).
- **Agile QA:** bugs are backlog items; QA engineers are embedded in teams, not isolated; "zero bug tolerance" sprints (s036, ch.15).
- **Live game development:** post-launch the team restructures around player data feedback loops; live operations requires different Kanban-heavy cadences (s036, ch.22).
- **Scaling Agile:** Scrum of Scrums, SAFe, and LeSS patterns for large studios (s036, ch.21).
- **There are no "best" practices:** the final chapter explicitly argues against cargo-culting Agile; retrospectives should continuously question and adapt the process (s036, ch.23).
