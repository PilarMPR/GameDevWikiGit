# Mechanics as Verbs — The Anthropy-Clark Model

Anna Anthropy and Naomi Clark argue that a game's mechanics are best understood as **verbs** — the things the player *does* — and that objects exist to give those verbs context and meaning. The designer's primary task is selecting and refining the right verbs, then populating the world with objects that let the verbs be performed meaningfully (s002, ch.2).

## Detail

- **Verbs = mechanics.** What the player can *do* is the mechanic. In *Mario*: jump, run, stomp. In a fighting game: punch, kick, block, throw.
- **Objects = context for verbs.** Objects (enemies, platforms, items) are the nouns that give verbs something to act on. A jump verb is only interesting if there are meaningful things to jump on, over, or past.
- **Rules govern verb relationships.** Rules define when verbs can be performed, what they cost, and what they produce. Duration, cost, and constraint define a verb's design space.
- **Meaning flows from verbs.** The *experience* of a game comes from what actions it lets you perform and how those actions feel, not from the setting or story alone. "A game about war that only lets you push buttons has nothing to say about war."
- **Design test:** if you can't describe your game in terms of active verbs, the core interaction is unclear.
