# Schell's Six Mechanic Categories

Schell organizes the Mechanics layer into six conceptual categories: Space, Objects/Attributes/States, Actions, Rules, Skill, and Chance. Together these six answer the question "what is this game made of?" at the formal, rule-level — independently of any story or aesthetic framing (s005, ch.10).

## Detail

1. **Space** (Lens #21 — Functional Space): the mathematical space a game inhabits — not necessarily the visual space. Discrete vs. continuous. 2D vs. 3D. Connected vs. disconnected. The functional space defines what positions and movements are possible.
2. **Objects, Attributes, and States**: the nouns of the game — everything that exists in the game world. Every object has attributes (properties) with values that change state over time. The complete object-attribute-state model defines the full game state.
3. **Actions**: operative actions (designed moves the player can take) and resultant actions (behaviors that emerge when operative actions combine). Good games have many resultant actions per operative action — they reward discovery.
4. **Rules**: constraints and allowances that govern the game. Rules define what is and isn't possible, and what happens when conditions are met. Rules are the engine of Dynamics.
5. **Skill**: the player-side challenges — physical (speed, accuracy), cognitive (memory, reasoning), social (bluffing, cooperation). Skill is what lets the player affect the outcome through mastery.
6. **Chance**: the randomness mechanic. Dice, shuffled cards, procedural generation. Chance creates uncertainty and variety, and interacts with skill to define the game's luck-skill balance.
