# Multiplayer Conflict Types

Multiplayer games are fundamentally different from single-player: other players are uncontrollable variables with their own goals, skills, and intentions, producing emergent human dynamics no mechanic can fully predict. Fullerton identifies four structural conflict types (s008, s013).

## Detail

1. **Direct conflict (competitive):** players act against each other directly. Zero-sum or near zero-sum. Examples: chess, fighting games, battle royale. Design challenge: balance, fairness, skill gap management.
2. **Indirect conflict (comparative):** compete without acting directly on each other. Who achieves the goal faster/better? Examples: racing, score-attack, leaderboards. Reduces emotional intensity of being targeted.
3. **Cooperative conflict (players vs. system):** all players against the game. Examples: Left 4 Dead, Portal 2 co-op. Design challenge: free-rider problem, failure attribution (blaming teammates destroys cooperative spirit).
4. **Asymmetric conflict (different roles/powers):** players have different abilities, win conditions, or information. Examples: Among Us (Impostors vs. Crewmates). Design challenge: balance between radically different experience types.
5. **Mixed conflict:** cooperation + competition simultaneously. Most socially rich; hardest to balance. Examples: Mario Party, Fall Guys team events.

**For Hot Potato specifically:** party games need observable play (single focal point, player identification, state readability at 2+ meters), short sessions (15-20 mins), and skill gap management (catch-up mechanics, chaos injection, short rounds, punchline mechanics when losing is funny rather than punishing).
