# Parts, Loops, and Wholes — Sellers' System Model

Sellers decomposes any system into four aspects: **Parts** (individual entities with properties, behaviors, relationships), **Loops** (dynamic feedback relationships that give the system behavior over time), **Wholes** (emergent properties that the system has but no single part has), and **Goals** (the targets toward which the loops drive). Understanding all four is required to design a game as a system (s001, ch.2–7).

## Detail

- **Parts:** objects/entities in the game with properties (stats), behaviors (how they act over time), and relationships (how they connect). A sword's damage is a property; its effectiveness is a relationship between sword, character, and enemy.
- **Loops:** reinforcing (positive feedback, creates snowball dynamics) and balancing (negative feedback, creates stability). The interaction between loop types determines the game's dynamic character. Most games need predominantly balancing loops to maintain the flow channel.
- **Wholes:** gameplay, balance, and narrative that emerge from the system's interactions. The whole is not predictable from parts alone — this is why emergent gameplay exists.
- **Goals:** every system loop drives toward a target. When game goals, player goals, and designer goals conflict, disengagement results. Good systemic design aligns them.
- **Design methodology (s001, ch.5):** Define the experience → Identify parts needed → Design relationships → Predict dynamics → Prototype and observe → Tune parts. This works for combat, economy, progression, or social systems.
