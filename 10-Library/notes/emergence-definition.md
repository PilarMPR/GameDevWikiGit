# Emergence — Behavior Greater Than the Sum of Parts

Emergence occurs when a system produces behaviors or properties not explicitly programmed into any of its individual parts, but arising from the interactions between them. In games, emergence is the origin of depth: players discover strategies and behaviors the designer never intended, from a small, consistent rule set (s001, ch.2; s013).

## Detail

- **Sellers' definition (s001, ch.2):** "Emergent behavior arises when a system produces behaviors or properties not explicitly designed into any of its parts but arising from the interactions between them."
- **Positive emergence (depth):** players discover uses of mechanics the designer didn't anticipate. Chess has unchanging rules yet billions of unique games. Minecraft's block-placement mechanic produces cathedrals, circuits, and game-within-game constructions (s005, ch.10).
- **Negative emergence (exploits):** the same property that produces depth also produces glitches, degenerate strategies, and dominant strategies. Negative emergence isn't a design failure — not catching it before release is.
- **MDA connection (s011):** the Dynamics layer is where emergence lives. The designer creates Mechanics; the Dynamics that emerge are not fully predictable until the game runs. This is why MDA emphasizes reasoning about Dynamics explicitly.
- **Salen & Zimmerman (s013):** a game exhibits emergence when: small numbers of rules produce large numbers of game states; player decisions have far-reaching consequences; the system has interdependencies (changing one part changes others).
- **Authoring vs. systems:** an RPG with 50 authored quests produces 50 possible stories. An RPG with 10 interacting faction systems produces millions of emergent stories. Systemic design scales; authored content doesn't.
