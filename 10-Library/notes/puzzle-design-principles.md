# Puzzle Design — Ten Principles

A puzzle is "a game with a dominant strategy" (Scott Kim, cited in s005, ch.12) — a problem with a knowable right answer. Once the dominant strategy is found, the puzzle is "used up." Schell's ten principles guide designing puzzles that feel fair, solvable, and satisfying (s005, ch.12).

## Detail

**10 principles (s005, ch.12):**
1. Make the goal easily understood.
2. Make it easy to get started (build a good toy first — something people naturally want to manipulate).
3. Give a sense of progress (players can see themselves approaching the solution incrementally — not a binary riddle).
4. Give a sense of solvability (Rubik's Cube ships solved — obviously it can be solved).
5. Don't impose unnecessary frustration (always give something to try).
6. Parallelism — multiple sub-puzzles to work on simultaneously; progress on one when stuck on another.
7. Pyramid structure — simple versions of the mechanic first, building toward complexity.
8. Hints — optional and graduated; minimum information to suggest a new avenue.
9. Give a satisfying solution — logically derivable, not a guess.
10. Use progressively complex puzzles — calibrate difficulty as a learning curve.

**Explicit vs. integrated puzzles:** Explicit = game stops for puzzle (7th Guest, chessboard-in-haunted-house). Integrated = puzzle elements woven into normal play (Zelda Wind Waker — dungeon items used to manipulate switch-controlled doors). Modern design strongly prefers integrated.

**Puzzles as pattern learning (s016):** a puzzle is fun while the player is learning its patterns. Once the dominant strategy is found and internalized, it's "used up."
