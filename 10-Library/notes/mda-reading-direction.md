# MDA Reading Direction — Designer vs. Player Asymmetry

The designer reads a game **Mechanics → Dynamics → Aesthetics** (from the engineered rules up to the felt experience), while the player experiences it **Aesthetics → Dynamics → Mechanics** (from the felt experience down to an emerging understanding of the rules). This opposite orientation is the central practical implication of MDA: adding a feature to the Mechanics layer does not directly produce a desired Aesthetic — it must propagate through the Dynamics layer first (s011).

## Detail

- **Designer's view (M→D→A):** design rules → observe emergent behaviors → evaluate whether the resulting experience matches the desired aesthetic. A small mechanics change can cascade unexpectedly through Dynamics into Aesthetics.
- **Player's view (A→D→M):** experience emotions first → notice patterns in what happens → eventually understand the rules. Most players never fully understand the Mechanics layer of complex games.
- **Design implication:** experience-first design, not feature-first. Ask "what emotional response do I want?" (Aesthetics), then work backward to what behaviors must emerge (Dynamics), then design rules that produce those behaviors (Mechanics).
- **The failure mode:** "adding features" — building Mechanics without tracing the causal chain to the intended Aesthetics. Mechanics that look good on paper may produce unexpected Dynamics that ruin the target Aesthetics.
