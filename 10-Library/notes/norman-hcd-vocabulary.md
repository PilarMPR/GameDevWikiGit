# Norman's HCD Vocabulary — Affordances, Signifiers, Feedback

Don Norman's six design principles from *The Design of Everyday Things* form the foundational vocabulary for human-centered design (HCD). Applied to games, they are the bedrock of playability: a mechanically excellent game fails if the player cannot figure out what to do, what is possible, or what happened (s015).

## Detail

1. **Affordances** — relationships between objects and agents that enable possible actions. A ledge at avatar-height affords climbing. Affordances exist whether or not they're visible.
2. **Signifiers** — perceivable signals that communicate available actions and where to perform them. A glowing item signifies collection; a crack signifies destructibility. The most important design principle: affordances must be signified to be useful.
3. **Constraints** — limits that reduce possible actions. Physical (key fits one lock), cultural (red = danger), semantic (saving the village NPC), logical (only one piece left to place). Constraints guide toward correct behavior without explicit instruction.
4. **Mappings** — relationships between controls and effects. Good mappings are natural (joystick left → character moves left). Inverted controls violate spatial mapping and require learned override.
5. **Feedback** — communication of what happened after an action. Must be immediate, informative, and proportional. Delayed feedback breaks causal association.
6. **Conceptual models** — the player's mental model of how the system works. Design for model accuracy: consistent rules, visible system state, familiar metaphors.
- **Gulf of Execution** — player wants to act but doesn't know how (affordances not signified).
- **Gulf of Evaluation** — player acts but doesn't understand what happened (feedback absent or unclear).

Hodent (s014) extends these with cognitive science: perception constraints, memory limits, and mental model alignment.
