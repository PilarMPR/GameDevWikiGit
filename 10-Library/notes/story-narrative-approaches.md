# Story in Games — Three Approaches

Story and gameplay have different requirements: stories want predetermined meaning; games want player agency. Schell identifies three approaches for combining them, each representing a different resolution to this tension (s005, ch.15).

## Detail

1. **String of pearls** — authored linear story with gameplay connecting the story moments (pearls). Player has agency within gameplay but the narrative path is predetermined. Advantage: strong narrative control, reliable pacing. Used in: most cinematic action games (Uncharted, The Last of Us).
2. **Story machine** — game mechanics generate narrative as a byproduct of play. No authored story; player's choices produce story-like sequences. Advantage: infinite variety, deep player investment. Used in: Dwarf Fortress, RimWorld, Crusader Kings.
3. **Branching/choice-based** — player choices affect story direction. Exponential content cost; usually funnel back together ("illusion of choice"). Used in: RPGs with dialogue trees (Mass Effect, Disco Elysium).

- **Ludonarrative dissonance** — when mechanics and narrative contradict each other. The story says "you have no choice"; the gameplay says "you have total choice." Breaks player engagement.
- **Embedded vs. emergent narrative (s013):** embedded = pre-authored elements (cutscenes, dialogue); emergent = stories arising from gameplay. Best narrative games work at both levels and make them reinforce each other.
- **Traditional storytelling still applies:** hook → escalation → climax → resolution; character motivation; thematic resonance; pacing of revelation. "Game designers who dismiss traditional storytelling make a strategic error." (s005, ch.15)
