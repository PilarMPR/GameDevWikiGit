# Self-Determination Theory Applied to Games

Ryan & Deci's Self-Determination Theory (SDT) identifies three innate psychological needs whose satisfaction enhances intrinsic motivation: **competence** (feeling effective and growing), **autonomy** (feeling in control with meaningful choices), and **relatedness** (feeling connected to others). Games that satisfy all three produce the strongest intrinsic engagement. Adding expected extrinsic rewards to an intrinsically motivating activity can *reduce* intrinsic motivation — the "undermining effect" (s014, ch.7).

## Detail

- **Competence:** the player feels effective, growing in skill. Delivered by: the flow channel, level-up moments, skill trees, challenge scaling. The primary driver of engagement in skill-based games.
- **Autonomy:** consequential choices, not scripted paths. "I decided to do this." Delivered by: branching narratives, open worlds, build variety, meaningful character choices.
- **Relatedness:** connection to others — social play, shared culture, community, story empathy. Multiplayer games can deliver relatedness; single-player narrative can too through character attachment.
- **Undermining effect (overjustification):** adding expected extrinsic rewards (points, trophies, monetization) to an intrinsically motivating activity can erode intrinsic motivation. Players reframe the activity as "work for reward." Especially relevant in F2P monetization design (s014; s017).
- **Variable ratio rewards** (slot-machine schedule) produce highest sustained engagement but exploit the undermining effect when overdone. Ethical design ensures the activity itself is rewarding, not just the rewards.
