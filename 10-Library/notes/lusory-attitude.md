# The Lusory Attitude

Bernard Suits defined playing a game as **"the voluntary attempt to overcome unnecessary obstacles."** The **lusory attitude** is the player's *acceptance* of those unnecessary obstacles for the sake of making the activity possible — choosing to use only the means the rules permit, even when easier means exist. It is the psychological stance that makes a game a game (Suits, cited in s013).

## Detail

- **Suits' definition unpacked (s013):** a game has a goal (the *prelusory goal*, e.g. "ball in hole"), **constitutive rules** that forbid the most efficient means of reaching it, and the **lusory attitude** — the willing acceptance of those rules. In golf, the prelusory goal is getting the ball in the hole; nothing stops you from carrying it there by hand except your agreement to play by the rules.
- **It is voluntary.** No one can be forced into the lusory attitude; it is freely adopted. This links directly to the **magic circle** — players step into the game's special frame by choice and agree to its constraints (s004, s013).
- **Why constraints create value.** The "unnecessary obstacles" are the point — they are what generate the experience. Remove the obstacle (let the player win trivially) and the game collapses. This reframes rules not as limits but as the *source* of play.
- **Connection to Huizinga (s004):** Suits' lusory attitude is a sharper statement of play's voluntary, bounded, rule-governed character that Huizinga described as the play-element of culture.
- **Design implication:** a game only works while players hold the lusory attitude. Cheating, exploits, or a broken difficulty curve can break it — once a player stops accepting the unnecessary obstacles, they have left the game even if still pressing buttons.
