# Emergence from Simple Rules

The convergent insight across Schell, Sellers, and Salen & Zimmerman: **a high ratio of resultant actions to operative actions** is the mark of a rich mechanic. Simple rules that interact in complex ways produce more depth than complex rules. This is the systems design principle underlying all emergent gameplay (s005, ch.10; s001, ch.2; s013).

## Detail

- **Operative actions** (Schell, s005, ch.10): the actions the designer explicitly provides — the verbs the player can perform.
- **Resultant actions**: behaviors that emerge when players combine operative actions in ways the designer didn't fully anticipate. *Chess* has few operative actions but enormous resultant depth.
- **The ratio:** games with a high resultant-to-operative ratio feel deep and discoverable. Games where most actions are operative (explicitly designed) feel narrow and exhausting to extend.
- **Sellers' systems principle (s001):** "emergent, engaging game systems can only be created by designing interacting systems, not by hand-authoring every outcome." Systems multiply. Authored content adds.
- **Salen & Zimmerman (s013):** small numbers of rules producing large numbers of possible game states; player decisions with far-reaching consequences across interdependent parts.
- *Space Invaders* case (s005): one rule (remaining aliens speed up) produces two emergent properties — acceleration as game-over approaches, and increasing difficulty for skilled players. Parsimony at its finest.
