# Level Design — Teaching, Goals, and Space

Level design is the craft of creating spaces, encounters, and sequences through which players experience a game. Its primary function is **teaching** — introducing and reinforcing mechanics through the environment itself. "Bad level design isn't actually designed." Level design implements game design's abstract systems in concrete rooms, enemy placements, and pickups (s012).

## Detail

- **Game design vs. level design (s012):** Game design defines the rules and systems (the "grammar"); level design implements them in specific spaces (the "sentences"). Bad levels ruin good mechanics; good levels cannot salvage broken mechanics.
- **Primary function: teaching (s012, ch.2):** show before ask (observe mechanic at low stakes before requiring use), scaffold complexity (isolated first, combined later), environmental storytelling as tutorial, reinforcement spacing to counteract the forgetting curve.
- **Goals hierarchy (s012, ch.3):** macro goal (complete the mission) → intermediate goals (find the key, clear the room) → micro goals (kill this enemy, jump this gap). Players must always have a clear micro goal that contributes to an understood intermediate goal.
- **Level design process (s012, ch.4):** define goals → floorplan (spatial layout) → block out (rough geometry; gameplay before art) → place enemies/pickups/triggers → iterate → visual pass → final playtest. Never art before function.
- **Architect analogy (s005, ch.19):** "The primary purpose of architecture is to control a person's experience." Levels engineer experience through space; the player's eyes pull them through.
- **Indirect control (s005, ch.16):** constraints, goals, visual design, characters, and music guide the player without explicit instruction. The player believes they are exploring freely while moving through an authored sequence.
