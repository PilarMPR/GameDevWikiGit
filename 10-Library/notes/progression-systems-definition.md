# Progression Systems — XP, Levels, Skill Trees

Progression systems track player growth over time and reward advancement. They primarily satisfy **competence** (SDT) and provide the structural backbone of long-term engagement by answering "what am I working toward?" at every scale (s006, ch.13–14).

## Detail

- **Five functions:** pacing long-term engagement, teaching mastery (gates advanced content behind demonstrated competence), expressing player identity (skill trees), creating anticipation (visible next milestone), communicating growth (stats make skill explicit).
- **XP loop:** actions → XP → levels → new content. XP curve shapes pacing: flat (predictable), exponential (later levels more prestigious, "grinding" risk), adjustable.
- **Weber-Fechner problem (s014):** logarithmic perception means +10 damage at level 1 feels enormous; at level 50, negligible. Numbers must grow faster than linearly to maintain felt impact.
- **Power curves (s001, s005):** player power curve must run roughly parallel to challenge curve. Runaway (player grows too fast) → trivial. Difficulty spike (challenge jumps above player) → frustration or grinding.
- **Skill trees:** each node should produce qualitative change (not just stat %). No mandatory funnels; no trap choices; readable branches; each node rewarding independently.
- **Meta-progression (s017):** capabilities earned in one run carry into subsequent runs. The outer loop in F2P architecture. Risk: if meta-power accumulates too much, per-run challenge is undermined.
- **The treadmill problem:** when XP/leveling becomes the *goal* rather than a byproduct, the game becomes a Skinner box.
