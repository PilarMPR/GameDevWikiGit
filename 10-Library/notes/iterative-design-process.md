# Iterative Design — Making and Testing as the Method

Iterative design is the professional standard: design, build, test, refine — repeatedly. Games are interactive dynamic systems whose quality can only be assessed in play; iteration is how the designer closes the gap between the designed artifact and the desired experience. It cannot be skipped or compressed, only made more efficient (s008; s005, ch.7).

## Detail

- **Fullerton's cycle (s008):** Conceptualize → Prototype → Playtest → Evaluate → Revise → Repeat. Key rule: move to the next cycle as fast as possible. A day of playtesting teaches more than a week of thinking.
- **Schell's "Rule of the Loop" (s005, ch.7):** "The more times you go around the loop, the better your game will be." 8 prototyping tips: answer a question; forget quality; don't get attached; prototype the hardest thing first; expect the unexpected; use physical prototypes when possible; build the toy first; get it in front of people fast.
- **Macklin & Sharp's design values (s009):** anchor iteration to explicit design values — what experience is this game creating? Prevent feature creep by filtering every playtest suggestion through the design values.
- **Sellers' designer's loop (s001, ch.7):** one of the four principal loops. The designer interacts with the game+player system from outside; it is a balancing loop reducing the gap between intended and actual experience.
- **Common failure modes:** design by committee (every comment incorporated); feature creep; playtesting too late; sunk cost attachment to features that don't serve the experience goal.
- **Cerny's Method (s005):** make a small polished version of the core experience first; pre-production ends when those core moments are fun.
