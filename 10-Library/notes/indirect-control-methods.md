# Indirect Control — Six Methods for Guiding Players

The designer cannot control what a player does — only the conditions under which they make choices. Schell's six methods of indirect control guide players through designed experiences while preserving the *feeling* of freedom. The key insight: players want the feeling of freedom, not freedom itself. Too much freedom without guidance is disorienting (s005, ch.16).

## Detail

1. **Constraints** — limit choices to the ones worth making. Walls, locked doors, action sets. Constraints work best when invisible — when they feel like natural features of the world.
2. **Goals** — pull players toward objectives. A player moves toward a goal; place goals at the end of the intended path. Goal clarity is essential: fuzzy goals produce wandering.
3. **Interface** — reveal only what you want revealed. The UI determines what information players have, and therefore what they think about and prioritize.
4. **Visual design** — guide the eye, guide the body. The eye moves to: bright areas (safety/reward), contrast, motion, unusual forms, openings. If the designer controls what the eye goes to, they control where the player goes.
5. **Characters** — players follow human-like agents. Explicit guidance (NPC says "go north"), implicit guidance (NPC walks that way), environmental storytelling (dead NPC slumped against a door), character gaze direction.
6. **Music** — the fastest emotional channel. Threatening music = caution; peaceful ambient = relax; directional audio = orientation. Players are often completely unaware of being guided by music.

Combining all six simultaneously produces irresistible guidance that feels entirely self-directed.
