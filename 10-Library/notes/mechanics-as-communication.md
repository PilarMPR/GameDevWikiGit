# Mechanics as Communication

Mechanics don't just produce challenge — they **say something**. What a game lets you *do* (and what it refuses to let you do) communicates meaning to the player. Anthropy & Clark's verb-centered framing makes this explicit: the verbs are the message, and a designer chooses verbs the way a writer chooses words (s002).

## Detail

- **Verbs carry meaning (s002).** A game about a janitor whose only verb is "mop" says something different from one where the janitor can also "quit," "sabotage," or "befriend." The available actions define what the game is *about* — meaning is authored through the action set, not just the story text.
- **Absence communicates too.** What the player *cannot* do is as expressive as what they can. A game that offers "shoot" but not "talk" makes an argument about its world. Removing or withholding a verb is a design statement (s002).
- **Objects give verbs context (s002).** Verbs act on objects; the same verb ("push") means something different applied to a box, a person, or a button. Mechanics communicate through the verb–object pairing.
- **Procedural rhetoric (s013).** Games make arguments through their *processes* — the rules and systems model a claim about how some part of the world works. A resource-management game about farming argues, through its economy, about scarcity and labor. The system *is* the rhetoric. Salen & Zimmerman's meaningful play is the precondition: an action communicates only if its outcome is **discernible** and **integrated** into the larger game.
- **Design implication:** when a mechanic and the intended message diverge (**ludonarrative dissonance**), players believe the mechanics over the story — because the verbs are what they actually experience.
