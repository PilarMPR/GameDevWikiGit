# Game Mechanics — What They Are

"Mechanics" is a contested term. Four major framings converge: Schell sees mechanics as the rules and procedures that implement the game's essential experience; MDA places them at the level of data representation and algorithms; Adams defines them as the implemented rules that mediate player action and game response; Anthropy & Clark define them as the verbs players perform on objects. All agree that mechanics are the *formal* level — what the game *does*, independently of any narrative or aesthetic layer (s005, s011, s006, s002).

## Detail

- **Schell (s005, ch.10):** mechanics are the rules and procedures of the game that give rise to the experience — the formal skeleton under the aesthetic flesh. The elemental tetrad places Mechanics as one of four interdependent elements (Mechanics, Story, Aesthetics, Technology).
- **MDA (s011):** Mechanics are "the components of the game at the level of data representation and algorithms" — the rules and systems that produce Dynamics when run.
- **Adams (s006, ch.14):** the rules of the game — the core logic that turns player input into game-state changes. Adams separates gameplay (challenges + actions) from mechanics (the implemented rules mediating them).
- **Anthropy & Clark (s002, ch.2):** mechanics = verbs; the player's actions are the mechanics. Objects exist to give the verbs context. This player-centered framing emphasizes what the player *does* rather than what the system computes.
