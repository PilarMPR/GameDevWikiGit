# Design Vision and the Concept Document

Every game has one big driving vision — the core idea that makes it worth playing. Capturing and communicating that vision clearly is the single practice most correlated with successful games. The concept document is the mechanism for doing so (Sellers, s001, ch.6).

## Detail

- **Vision first:** the design vision is not the game's features — it is its essential experience. "A multiplayer tag game where everyone is terrified of holding the object" (Hot Potato) is a vision. A feature list is not.
- **Clear, shared vision (s001, ch.6):** a clear vision shared across the team is the single most correlated practice with game success. Vague visions produce design by committee; clear visions produce purposeful decision-making.
- **The concept document (s001, ch.6):** captures the vision in three layers: high concept (1-2 sentences), product (the game's core identity and audience), detailed design (how the vision is implemented). Serves as the touchstone for development decisions.
- **Smaller, focused visions are often better:** "Angry Birds" or "For Honor = combat as dance" — not grand ambitions, but precise, communicable ideas. (s001, ch.6 examples)
- **Schell's theme principle (s005, ch.5):** the deepest version of vision is a theme — the statement the game is making. Theme unifies mechanics, story, aesthetics, and technology (elemental tetrad) when all four serve the same thematic statement.
