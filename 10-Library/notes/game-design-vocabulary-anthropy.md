# Anthropy & Clark — Games as Expressive Medium

Anthropy & Clark argue that better language for game design yields better games — and that the core vocabulary is simple: games speak in **verbs** (what the player does) and **objects** (what verbs act upon). Design carries authorial meaning, not just challenge; games are an expressive medium like literature or film (s002).

## Detail

- **Why vocabulary matters:** imprecise language is a real obstacle to good design work. A shared precise vocabulary lets designers reason about and critique craft.
- **Verbs/objects = core:** the things the player *does* are the seat of meaning. Objects give verbs context. Design = creating meaningful choices through rules.
- **Scenes (s002, ch.3):** how verbs and objects are arranged with shaping, pacing, layering, and moments of inversion.
- **Context (s002, ch.4):** visual/fictional framing teaches the player how to read the verbs. A war game that only lets you shoot has nothing to say about war.
- **Expressive medium stance:** design choices carry authorial meaning; a game about love should have mechanics that express love, not just label it. This aligns with Koster's fun-as-learning (pattern content can be anything, including meaning about the human condition) and Fernández-Vara's procedural rhetoric.
