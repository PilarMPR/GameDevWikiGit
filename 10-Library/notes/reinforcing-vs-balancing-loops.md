# Reinforcing vs. Balancing Loops

Every feedback loop in a game system is either **reinforcing** (positive feedback: output amplifies the condition) or **balancing** (negative feedback: output reduces the gap). Reinforcing loops create a sense of growing power and momentum; balancing loops maintain challenge and prevent dominance. Sustainable engagement requires designing both in deliberate proportion (s001, ch.2; s011; s005, ch.11).

## Detail

- **Reinforcing (positive feedback):** winning → gain more resources → win more easily → gain even more resources. Creates snowball dynamics. XP loops, rich-get-richer economies. Runaway unless countered by a balancing loop.
- **Balancing (negative feedback):** challenge too hard → player practices → skill increases → challenge feels easier. Creates stability. The flow channel is sustained by balancing loops.
- **Game design balance:** good games combine reinforcing loops (power progression creates a sense of growth) with balancing loops (challenge scaling maintains difficulty). The tension between them is the engine of engagement.
- **The Monopoly problem (s011):** Monopoly's core mechanic is a reinforcing loop (more properties → more rent → more properties) with no effective balancing loop. The intended aesthetics of Challenge and Fellowship are sabotaged by the Dynamics.
- **King-of-hill social dynamic:** in multiplayer, players targeting the leader is an emergent balancing loop — it triggers without design but requires visibility of standings and ability to target the leader.
