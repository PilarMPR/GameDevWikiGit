# Formal Elements of Games — Cross-Source Synthesis

The irreducible structural anatomy every game shares. Multiple authoritative sources converge on slightly different lists but agree on the core: **players act (verbs/procedures) on objects/resources, under rules, toward goals/outcomes, within boundaries**. This is the strongest cross-source agreement in game design theory (s008, s009, s005, s002, s013).

## Detail

| Component | Fullerton (s008) | Macklin & Sharp (s009) | Schell (s005) | Anthropy (s002) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Who plays | Players | Players | — | — |
| What to achieve | Objectives | Goals | Mechanics | — |
| Legal moves | Procedures | Actions | Mechanics | Verbs |
| Constraints | Rules | Rules | Mechanics | — |
| Things of value | Resources | Objects | — | Objects |
| Opposition | Conflict | — | — | — |
| Where | Boundaries | Playspace | Technology | — |
| Terminal state | Outcome | Goals | Mechanics | — |
| Experience layer | Dramatic elements | — | Story/Aesthetics | — |

- **Fullerton's 8 + 5:** Players, Objectives, Procedures, Rules, Resources, Conflict, Boundaries, Outcome + 5 Dramatic elements (Challenge, Play, Premise, Character, Story). The key move: separating formal (structural) from dramatic (experiential) elements.
- **Macklin & Sharp's 6:** Actions, Goals, Rules, Objects, Playspace, Players — the parsimonious minimum.
- **Schell's elemental tetrad:** Mechanics, Story, Aesthetics, Technology — a design *alignment* tool rather than a decomposition list.
- **Salen & Zimmerman's core mechanic (s013):** the essential repeated action that defines the game's nature. "The atom from which gameplay is built."
- **MDA's Mechanics layer ≡ formal elements.** MDA adds causal role; Fullerton adds design decomposition role. Complementary views.
