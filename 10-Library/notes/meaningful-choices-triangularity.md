# Meaningful Choices and Triangularity

A **meaningful choice** is one where the number of options matches the player's desires, the options are genuinely distinct, and the outcomes matter. **Triangularity** is the most compelling form of meaningful choice: a safe option with low reward vs. a risky option with high reward — where neither is trivially dominant and the right answer depends on context (s005, ch.11).

## Detail

- **The formula (Mateas, cited in s005):** Choices = Desires → player feels freedom. Choices > Desires → overwhelmed. Choices < Desires → frustrated.
- **50 cars that drive identically = 0 meaningful choices.** 2 genuinely different weapons = 2 meaningful choices. Quantity of options is not depth.
- **Triangularity:** the most emotionally engaging choice structure. Low risk/low reward vs. high risk/high reward. When calibrated correctly, neither option is trivially better; the player must evaluate their situation each time.
- ***Space Invaders* example (s005, ch.11):** Normal aliens (safe, few points) vs. the mystery ship (dangerous, 100–300 points). The "should I break cover to shoot the saucer?" decision is the triangle. It recurs throughout the session, never losing its tension.
- **Value expected balance:** for triangularity to work, the expected value of the risky path must be comparable to the safe path. The probability of success × the high reward ≈ the probability of success × the low reward. *Qix* case: slow (doubles points, lower success rate) vs. fast (fewer points, higher success rate) were calibrated to equal expected value.
- **"About 8 out of 10 times someone asks for help on a prototype 'that just isn't fun,' the game is missing this kind of meaningful choice."** (Schell, s005, ch.11)
