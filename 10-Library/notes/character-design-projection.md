# Character Design — Player Projection and NPC Believability

In games, the player doesn't empathize with the player character — they **project** onto them. The player IS the character; decisions feel personal, failures feel shameful, growth feels rewarding. This projection relationship is unique to the game medium (Schell, s005, ch.9).

## Detail

- **Projection over empathy:** film audiences empathize with characters who are other. Game players project themselves into characters who are their proxy. This is why the player character's death feels personal and their growth feels earned.
- **Silent vs. voiced protagonist:** Silent (Gordon Freeman, Link) = maximum projection, limited narrative arc. Voiced/characterized (Geralt, Kratos) = rich narrative potential, narrower identification. Neither is superior; the choice should align with narrative goals.
- **NPCs serve four functions:** narrative delivery, indirect control (guiding player attention), emotional texture (making the world feel inhabited), and mechanical challenge (enemies as gameplay mechanics).
- **Believability, not realism:** NPCs don't need to simulate fully — they need to be consistent with the world's logic. Simpler but consistent behavior beats complex but contradictory.
- **Empathy mechanism (s005, ch.9):** humans project personality onto any agent perceived to have intentions (Heider & Simmel). Large eyes trigger protective instincts; consistent motivation makes agents feel real. The cost of creating relatable characters is lower than intuition suggests.
