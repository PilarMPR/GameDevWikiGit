# Player Lifecycle — How Type Emphasis Shifts with Mastery

Player taxonomies (Bartle, LeBlanc) are often misread as fixed labels. In practice the *emphasis* a player places on different motivations **shifts over their lifecycle** as they master the game. A design that only serves the day-one motivation loses players who have moved on (s001, ch.4).

## Detail

- **The Bartle drift (s001, ch.4).** New players tend toward **Achiever** behavior (clear goals, visible progress). As they gain competence they shift toward **Explorer** (probing systems, seeking depth). Long-term players polarize toward **Killer** (mastery expressed competitively) or **Socializer** (the community becomes the reason to stay).
- **Design implication:** content must exist for each stage. Onboarding feeds Achievers; mid-game depth feeds Explorers; endgame competition and social systems retain the long tail. A game with only early-game goals empties out once mastery arrives.
- **F2P framing (s017).** The commercial funnel mirrors this: acquisition → onboarding → retention → monetization. Each phase serves a different player state; the retention phase depends on giving mastered players a reason (social, competitive, or creative) to keep returning.
- **Why it happens.** As a pattern is learned it stops rewarding (Koster: fun is learning, and a mastered pattern is no longer fun). The player seeks a *new* source of engagement — deeper systems, other players, or self-expression — which reads as a "type shift."
- **Caveat.** This is a tendency, not a law. Players are mixtures and the drift is statistical; use it to plan content cadence, not to predict an individual.
