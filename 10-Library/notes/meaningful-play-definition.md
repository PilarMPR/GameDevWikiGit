# Meaningful Play — Discernable and Integrated

Salen & Zimmerman's central design goal: **meaningful play** emerges when the relationship between player action and system outcome is both (1) **discernable** — the player can perceive the result — and (2) **integrated** — the result matters to the larger game over time. Both conditions are necessary; neither alone is sufficient (s013).

## Detail

- **Discernable:** the player can see, hear, or otherwise perceive what their action caused. Failures: stat changes not communicated visually; damage that looks the same at 10% or 90% effectiveness; strategic progress too complex to read at a glance. Connected to Norman's feedback principle and gulfs of execution/evaluation (s015).
- **Integrated:** actions compound over time, shaping future states. An action that only affects the immediate moment is not integrated. Failures: busywork with no lasting consequence; choices that converge on the same outcome; progression that resets.
- **Game definition (s013):** "A game is a system in which players engage in an artificial conflict, defined by rules, that results in a quantifiable outcome."
- **Synthesis across sources:** meaningful play (s013) ≡ MDA aesthetics (s011) ≡ fun-as-learning (s016) ≡ sustained engagement (s001). Four descriptions of the same design target from different angles. All agree: games should produce experiences where the player's actions matter and where the player can tell they matter.
