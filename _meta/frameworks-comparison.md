# Frameworks Comparison

Every major analytical framework in this wiki, laid out side by side. Use this page when you need to decide *which tool to reach for*, or when you want to understand how thinkers in the canon agree and disagree.

---

## 1. What is a game?

| Framework | Source | Core claim | What it adds |
|-----------|--------|------------|-------------|
| **Magic Circle** | Huizinga s004, Salen & Zimmerman s013 | A game is a voluntary, bounded, non-consequential activity in a space "set apart" from ordinary life | The psychological + spatial framing; why stepping into a game is a meaningful act |
| **Formal Elements** | Fullerton s008, Macklin & Sharp s009 | A game is defined by 8 structural components: players, objectives, procedures, rules, resources, conflict, boundaries, outcomes | The design vocabulary; gives you things to actually design and check |
| **Elemental Tetrad** | Schell s005 | A game has four reinforcing elements: Technology, Mechanics, Story, Aesthetics | The alignment tool; reminds you that mechanics aren't the whole game |
| **MDA** | Hunicke/LeBlanc/Zubek s011 | A game is an artifact whose *content is its behavior* (not its media); Mechanics generate Dynamics which evoke Aesthetics | The causal model; shows why small mechanic changes cascade to experience |
| **Systems Model** | Sellers s001 | A game is a rule-governed system of parts, loops, and wholes that couples with a player to form a larger system | The design lens; lets you reason about emergent behavior before it exists |
| **Meaningful Play** | Salen & Zimmerman s013 | A game produces meaningful play when player actions have discernible + integrated outcomes | The evaluation criterion; tells you when a design is working vs. hollow |
| **Lusory Attitude** | Suits (cited s013) | A game is any activity where players voluntarily accept constitutive rules as conditions of play | The player-side definition; explains *why* players follow arbitrary rules |

**When to use each:**
- Designing from scratch → **Formal Elements** (vocabulary to fill in)
- Evaluating whether a design is working → **Meaningful Play** (discernible + integrated?)
- Understanding why something feels off → **MDA** (trace the chain M→D→A)
- Thinking about systemic depth → **Systems Model**
- Communicating to non-designers (player, parent, school) → **Magic Circle** (accessible framing)
- Checking team alignment → **Elemental Tetrad** (are all four reinforcing each other?)

## 2. What is "fun"? (The central debate)

| Framework | Source | Claim | Scope |
|-----------|--------|-------|-------|
| **Fun = Learning** | Koster s016 | Fun is the pleasurable signal produced when the brain successfully acquires a new pattern. Boredom = pattern exhausted. | Cognitive / mechanistic; explains *why* fun happens |
| **8 Aesthetics** | Hunicke/LeBlanc/Zubek s011 | "Fun" is too vague; replace with 8 specific aesthetic experiences: Sensation, Fantasy, Narrative, Challenge, Fellowship, Discovery, Expression, Submission | Taxonomic / design-facing; tells you *what kind* of fun to target |
| **Engagement ≠ Fun** | Sellers s001 | Games need not be "fun" at all; what matters is that they remain interactive and engaging. Fun is a subset of engagement. | Definitional; frees design from the obligation to be pleasant |
| **Flow Channel** | Csikszentmihalyi (cited s005, s016) | Optimal experience occurs in a narrow band where challenge matches skill; below = boredom, above = anxiety | Phenomenological; describes the *state* the player is in when it works |
| **Lazzaro's 4 Keys** | Lazzaro (external) | Hard Fun (challenge + mastery), Easy Fun (exploration + wonder), Serious Fun (meaning + real-world goals), Social Fun (group + sharing) | Practitioner-friendly; maps to audience segments more directly than MDA |
| **Bartle's 4 Types** | Bartle (cited s005, s001) | Players seek different experiences: Achievers (goals), Explorers (discovery), Socializers (community), Killers (competition) | Player-orientation; describes motivational profiles, not aesthetic targets |

**The apparent contradiction — resolved:**

These frameworks are not in factual conflict. They describe the same phenomenon at different levels:
- **Koster** is *mechanistic* (why fun happens: pattern acquisition)
- **MDA's 8 aesthetics** is *taxonomic* (what kinds of fun exist: 8 named experiences)
- **Flow channel** is *phenomenological* (what the fun state feels like: focused engagement)
- **Sellers** is *definitional* (what the design goal should be: engagement, not hedonic pleasure specifically)
- **Bartle** is *motivational* (why different players find different things fun: orientation to world vs. players)
- **Lazzaro** is *audience-segmented* (four audience profiles, each requiring different design emphasis)

**Practical synthesis:** Use MDA's 8 aesthetics as the *vocabulary* for what to design toward (explicit and testable). Use Koster's theory as the *mechanistic explanation* for why Challenge (mastery/learning) produces sustained engagement. Use flow channel as the *calibration target* during difficulty design. Use Bartle/Lazzaro for *audience analysis* when designing feature priorities.

> ⚠️ **One active tension remains:** Koster's theory privileges Challenge/learning as the *primary* source of lasting engagement (you can learn from Fellowship, Discovery, etc., but the deepest fun comes from mastery). MDA treats all 8 aesthetics as equally valid. This isn't a factual conflict — it's a difference in emphasis. Koster would likely say that Fellowship-first games are excellent but don't produce the deepest mastery satisfaction. MDA would say that's fine — Fellowship-first is a legitimate design target.

## 3. Design process frameworks

| Framework | Source | Core claim | Emphasis |
|-----------|--------|------------|---------|
| **Playcentric Design** | Fullerton s008 | Design revolves around the player's experience, assessed through continuous playtesting. Prototype early, test with players, iterate. | Process and method |
| **Design Values** | Macklin & Sharp s009 | Anchor every iteration to explicit design values — stated goals for the experience. Without values, iteration is reactive rather than directed. | Intention and evaluation |
| **Designer's Loop** | Sellers s001 | The designer's feedback loop wraps the interactive loop: define experience → design system → observe player → revise system → repeat. Designers themselves are part of a loop. | Systemic / recursive |
| **Rule of the Loop** | Schell s005 | The more times you iterate the design loop, the better your game will be. Maximize loop count by minimizing prototype cost. | Speed and volume |
| **Cerny Method** | Cerny (cited s005 ch.7) | Build two polished, fun levels before entering full production. Pre-production ends only when the core experience is proven. | Risk and phase discipline |
| **Lenses Approach** | Schell s005 | Use 100+ perspective-shifting lenses (design questions) to interrogate the design from every angle. No single "correct" perspective. | Breadth of consideration |

**When to use each:**
- Setting up a new project → **Design Values** (what are we actually trying to do?)
- Running a playtest session → **Playcentric Design** (protocol, observation, debrief)
- Deciding whether to enter full production → **Cerny Method** (is the core fun yet?)
- Reviewing a design decision → **Lenses** (which lens reveals what I'm missing?)
- Building feedback into team culture → **Designer's Loop** (the team is in the loop too)
- Prioritizing prototype speed → **Rule of the Loop** (faster iteration > more polished prototypes)

**The process consensus (all sources agree):**
Prototype → Playtest → Evaluate → Iterate. Every source in this wiki, regardless of their theoretical framing, converges on this method. The differences are in how they anchor the iteration (design values, lenses, systemic targets) not in whether to iterate.

## 4. Player frameworks

| Framework | Source | What it classifies | Granularity |
|-----------|--------|-------------------|-------------|
| **Bartle's 4 Types** | Bartle 1996 (cited s005 ch.8) | Player motivational orientation: Achiever, Explorer, Socializer, Killer | Coarse; 4 categories; easy to apply |
| **LeBlanc's 8 Aesthetics** | Hunicke et al. s011 | Target experience types: Sensation, Fantasy, Narrative, Challenge, Fellowship, Discovery, Expression, Submission | Medium; 8 named experiences; design-facing |
| **Lazzaro's 4 Keys** | Lazzaro external | Audience segments: Hard Fun, Easy Fun, Serious Fun, Social Fun | Coarse; 4 segments; audience-facing |
| **Maslow's Hierarchy** | Maslow (cited s005 ch.9) | Need levels: physiological → safety → belonging → esteem → self-actualization | Theoretical foundation for why people play at all |
| **SDT** | Ryan & Deci (cited s014) | Three innate needs: Competence, Autonomy, Relatedness | Fine; evidence-based; directly actionable in retention design |
| **Demographics / Psychographics** | Adams s006, Schell s005 | Observable audience characteristics (age, gender, experience) and psychological characteristics (values, lifestyle, orientation) | Variable; research-dependent |

**Practical synthesis:** The frameworks are complementary at different scales:
- **Maslow + SDT** → *why* people need games (fundamental motivation theory)
- **Bartle's 4** → *how* different people orient to play (design feature priorities)
- **8 Aesthetics** → *what* experiences to design for (target vocabulary)
- **Lazzaro's 4 Keys** → *who* to design for when targeting a specific segment (audience segmentation)

For a party brawler (Hot Potato context): SDT (Competence from skill expression, Autonomy from character choice, Relatedness from shared experience) + Bartle (must serve Achievers/Socializers/Killers simultaneously) + 8 Aesthetics (Fellowship + Challenge as dual-primary targets) together give a complete picture.

## 5. Formal models vs. craft approaches

The field's deepest methodological tension: how much should game design be *formalized* into models, systems, and frameworks — vs. approached as a *craft* developed through practice, taste, and deep listening?

| Camp | Sources | Position |
|------|---------|----------|
| **Formal/Systems** | Sellers s001, Salen & Zimmerman s013, MDA s011, Fernández-Vara s010 | Games are formal systems; design is system design; formal analysis produces rigorous, reproducible insights |
| **Craft/Lens** | Schell s005, Koster s016, Anthropy & Clark s002, Hourences s003 | Games are expressions of experience; design is craft developed through listening, play, and iteration; formal models miss the intuitive dimension |
| **Player-centric** | Fullerton s008, Macklin & Sharp s009, Norman s015, Hodent s014 | The player's experience is the ground truth; neither formal models nor designer intuition substitute for watching real people play |

**Resolution:** These are not contradictory. Experienced designers typically use formal models as *scaffolding* for thinking but rely on *playtesting* as the final arbiter, while developing *craft* over time. The formal camp is more useful for analysis and communication; the craft camp is more useful for generation and feel. The player-centric camp is the universal check on both.

A designer who only uses formal models produces games that are technically coherent but feel lifeless. A designer who only trusts intuition produces games that feel inspired but may have structural problems they can't diagnose. A designer who tests both against real player behavior produces the best games.
